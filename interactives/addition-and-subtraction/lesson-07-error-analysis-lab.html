Challenge 1 of 3 · Specialising
Investigate the error where you forget to carry the tens digit in addition. Design a 3-digit addition where forgetting to carry the tens gives an answer that is exactly 100 less than the correct answer.
How many different solutions can you find? What do the tens digits have in common in each case?
Mathematician's thinking

Let me start by thinking about what causes a difference of exactly 100. That must mean a missing hundred, so I need to find where a carry of 1 to the hundreds column is being made.

First, I'll try 235 + 168.

Ones: 5 + 8 = 13 (carry 1)

Tens: 3 + 6 + 1 = 10 (carry 1) → Correct answer: 403

If I forget the carry to hundreds: I'd get 303. Difference: 403 - 303 = 100 ✓

But wait, let me try 162 + 237 to see if this is always true...

Ones: 2 + 7 = 9 (no carry)

Tens: 6 + 3 = 9 (no carry) → Correct answer: 399

That doesn't work because the tens didn't carry. I see. The tens digits must add to 10 or more to create a carry.

So the pattern is: both pairs where the tens digits add to at least 10 will work. Examples: 154 + 156, 173 + 137, 245 + 268. Hundreds of solutions!

The key constraint: the tens column must produce a carry of 1. When you forget that carry to the hundreds, you always lose exactly 100.
Challenge 2 of 3 · Conjecturing
You make an error in your calculation, but your estimate is still very close to your answer. Does this mean your calculation must be correct?
Investigate this by designing examples. What is the largest error that could "hide" inside a close estimate?
Mathematician's thinking

This seems obvious at first. Let me test it. If I calculate 1,247 + 3,856 and estimate 1,000 + 4,000 = 5,000, my estimate is roughly 5,000.

Correct answer: 5,103. Close to my estimate!

But what if I made an error and got 5,099? My estimate says I'm right, but I'm actually off by 4.

What if the error was even bigger? Say I forgot to carry the tens and got 5,003. My estimate of 5,000 is still close, yet I'm wrong by 100!

Aha! So the Radar (estimation) catches big errors like forgetting entire carries, but what about cascading errors where mistakes cancel out? If I add one digit wrong but miss trading in another column, the errors might partly cancel.

Let me check if estimation can catch an error of 50: 2,340 + 1,670 estimates to roughly 4,000. Correct answer: 4,010. If I got 3,960 (off by 50), my estimate ≈4,000 still looks close!

Answer: No, definitely NOT always true. Your estimate can hide errors up to about 10% of the size of your number. That could be 50 in a 500, or 200 in a 2,000.

Estimation is powerful for catching gross errors, but it's not a complete check. A "close" estimate doesn't guarantee your work is correct. The Radar spots problems, but it's not perfect proof.
Challenge 3 of 3 · Justifying
A student calculated 5,304 - 2,867 and wrote:
5304
−2867
2567
This student made exactly ONE error. But the effect spreads to two columns of their answer.
Where is the root cause? Explain why this error cascaded into the next column.
Mathematician's thinking

Let me check the student's answer against the correct working. First, I'll calculate correctly: 5,304 - 2,867 = 2,437.

They got 2,567, so the hundreds and tens are wrong.

Let me trace through their work column by column.

Ones: 4 - 7 needs borrowing. 14 - 7 = 7. They got 7. ✓ Correct.

Tens (where the error is): After borrowing 1 from the tens, I have 0 - 1 = -1 remaining in the tens. Now subtract: -1 - 6 = -7. So I must borrow from hundreds: 10 - 1 - 6 = 3.

But wait. I think the student forgot to reduce the tens digit by 1 after borrowing for the ones. So they thought the tens digit was still 0 (not -1). Then 0 - 6 = -6, borrow: 10 - 6 = 4... no, they got 6, so that's not it either.

Let me look at the hundreds: after borrowing for tens, I have 3 - 1 = 2. Then 2 - 8 = -6, so borrow from thousands: 12 - 8 = 4. They got 5. That's wrong too.

The root error: The student didn't carry/reduce the tens digit after borrowing from it for the ones. This means in the tens column they calculated 0 - 6 instead of the correct -1 - 6. That error forced them to borrow from the hundreds, and since they borrowed wrong, the hundreds column answer was also wrong.

Correct answer: 2,437

When you trade/borrow from a column, you must reduce that column by 1. If you skip that step, the error propagates down the line. One mistake causes the next column to be wrong too.
