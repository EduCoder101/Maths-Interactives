Word Problems & Multi-Step Reasoning

Lesson 8: Hidden Steps

Translating complex text into a mathematical plan, and spotting the trap-doors

🕵️

Learning Intention & Success Criteria ▼

Learning Intention

We are learning a 4-step protocol to break down multi-step word problems and spot hidden steps that try to trick us.

Success Criteria

  • I can use the 4-step protocol: Visualise, Estimate, Calculate, Check
  • I can identify hidden steps in a word problem (like return trips or repeated items)
  • I can draw a bar model that has more than two parts
Phase 1 · The Hook, 10 mins
When the obvious answer is wrong

🎯 Problem 1, Alex's Bike Ride

Read the whole problem carefully, then shout out your answer:

On Tuesday, Alex rode 4 km to a friend's house, then 12 km to the beach.

How far did Alex travel before he got home?
🪤 The Hidden Step

Most people blurt out 16 km, but the question asks how far Alex travelled before he got home. That means the return journey is included: 4 km + 12 km + 12 km + 4 km = 32 km.

The hidden step is the return trip, which the question implies without spelling out. Reading "before he got home" carefully is the key.

🎯 Problem 2, The Swimming Carnival

Here's another one. Read every word:

At the school swimming carnival, the Year 5 relay team swam 4 laps of the 25 m pool in the heats. In the final, they swam 6 laps.

How many metres did the team swim across both races in total?
🪤 The Hidden Step

The trap is adding 4 + 6 = 10 and stopping there. But 10 what? The pool is 25 m long, you have to multiply by 25 to get metres. The hidden step is converting laps to metres first: (4 × 25) + (6 × 25) = 100 + 150 = 250 m.

🎯 Problem 3, Tuck Shop Tuesday

One more, this one has a different kind of hidden step:

The school tuck shop sells pies for $3.50 each. On Tuesday, 3 students each buy a pie and a drink. The drinks cost $2.00 each.

How much does the tuck shop collect from these 3 students in total?
🪤 The Hidden Step

Some students add $3.50 + $2.00 = $5.50 and call it done. But each of 3 students buys both items, the hidden step is multiplying the per-person cost by 3: $5.50 × 3 = $16.50.

The word "each" is almost always a signal that a multiplication step is hiding inside the problem.

💡 What do these three problems have in common?

The Pattern

Every problem contains information that sounds straightforward but contains a step that is easy to skip. The problems are not trying to trick you unfairly, all the information is there. But you have to read every single word and ask: "Have I used all of the important information?"

📋 Teacher Notes, Running the Hook ▼

Present each problem one at a time. Pause after each for students to call out answers, then reveal.

  • Problem 1: Students who say 16 km missed the return journey. Ask: "Did we use all the important information in the problem?"
  • Problem 2: The key misconception is treating laps as metres. Ask: "What unit is the question asking for? Do our numbers give us that unit yet?"
  • Problem 3: The word "each" is the signal. Once students identify it, write it on the board and tell them: "From now on, whenever you see the word 'each' in a word problem, circle it immediately, it almost always means a hidden multiplication."
  • Frame the whole sequence positively: the problems aren't tricks. All the information is there. The skill is learning to slow down and read everything.
Phase 2 · Explicit Instruction (I Do), 15 mins
The 4-Step Protocol in Action
1

Meet the Problem

Alex is going on a bike ride.

Alex cycles 4 km to his friend's house, then 12 km to the beach. After visiting, he cycles home the same way.
How far does he cycle in total before getting home?

Read the problem carefully. What is being asked? What numbers are given? What's the hidden information?

2

Step 1: Visualise, Draw the Bar Model

Show the hidden information.

The problem asks about a return journey. Draw all four parts:

❓ Total distance (Whole)
4 km
to friend's
12 km
to beach
12 km hidden!
beach back
4 km hidden!
home
🪤 The Hidden Steps

The problem doesn't explicitly say Alex cycles 12 km back from the beach and 4 km from his friend's home, but "the same way" means he must. These are the hidden steps.

3

Step 2: Estimate, Radar for Error

Set your safety net.

📡 Round and estimate

4 + 12 is about 15. The whole trip is there and back, so double it: 15 × 2 = 30

My answer should be near 30. If I get something very different, I'll know something is wrong.

Before calculating the exact answer, always estimate. This is your safety net.

4

Step 3: Calculate, Do the Maths

Use your chosen method.

🔢 Exact calculation

4 + 12 + 12 + 4 = 32 km

We could use a mental strategy here because the numbers are friendly. We could also write it as an algorithm. The method is our choice.

5

Step 4: Check, Compare to Estimate

Does the answer make sense?

✅ Does it match?

Our estimate: 30 km
Our exact answer: 32 km

32 is very close to 30. ✓ Confidence is high. We can be sure our answer is correct.

Always compare your exact answer to your estimate. A result of 320 km when you estimated 30 would be a red flag. Always go back and find the error.

6

The Protocol Works for Subtraction Too

Finding a missing part.

Some problems give you the whole and two of the parts. You need to subtract to find the missing part.

A shire has a total population of 35,084. Town A has 14,230 people. Town B has 12,100 people.
How many people live in the rest of the shire?
35,084 (Whole, given)
14,230
Town A
12,100
Town B
❓
Rest of shire
🔑 Key Insight

You must subtract both known parts from the whole, not just one. The hidden step is remembering to use both numbers.

📡 Estimate

35,000 − 14,000 − 12,000 ≈ 9,000. Answer should be near 9,000.

🔢 Calculate

35,084 − 14,230 = 20,854
20,854 − 12,100 = 8,754

✅ Check

8,754 is close to our estimate of 9,000. ✓ The 4-step protocol works for all problem types.

📋 Teacher Notes, Modelling Tips ▼
  • Draw the Bar Model on the board in real time, do not pre-draw it. Let students see the thinking process.
  • When you reach the return journey parts, pause and say: "Wait, I haven't finished reading the problem." Model the habit of reading all the way to the end before drawing anything.
  • Emphasise that the Bar Model works for both addition problems (unknown whole) and subtraction problems (unknown part). The structure is the same.
  • For the population problem, explicitly name the hidden step: "The problem asks about 'the rest of the shire', that means I need to subtract both Town A and Town B, not just one."
Phase 3 · Guided Practice (We Do), 10 mins
Find the hidden step together

✏️ Mini-Whiteboard Challenge

I have $50. I buy a book for $14 and two pens for $3 each.
How much change do I get?

On your mini-whiteboards: draw the Bar Model and write down the hidden step before you calculate.

🪤 The Hidden Step

The problem says "two pens for $3 each", you must calculate the cost of two pens first: $3 × 2 = $6. If you just use $3, you will get the wrong answer.

I'm spending about $14 + $6 = $20. Change should be about $30.

🔢 Calculate

Step 1: 2 × $3 = $6 (hidden step!)
Step 2: $14 + $6 = $20 (total spent)
Step 3: $50 − $20 = $30 change

  • Look specifically for students who write $3 instead of $6 for the pens. Use this as a teachable moment for the whole class.
  • If a student gets the hidden step immediately, ask them to explain their thinking to a partner rather than calling it out, this gives other students more time to identify it themselves.

Phase 4 · Practice Problems
5 problems · 4 steps every time · highlighters out
📌 Reminder

For every problem: draw the Bar Model first, write your Radar estimate, show your calculation, then check. Do not skip steps.

🟢 Tier 1, Whole Numbers
Problem 1
The school library has 1,240 fiction books and 876 non-fiction books. A donation adds another 350 books, all fiction. How many fiction books does the library have now?
Draw the bar model. The hidden step: the 350 adds only to fiction, not to the total library. What is the question actually asking for?
1,590 fiction books.
1,240 + 350 = 1,590
The 876 non-fiction books is extraneous, the question only asks about fiction.
Problem 2
A town has a population of 24,500. Over summer, 3,120 people move in and 1,875 people move out. What is the population at the end of summer?
Two operations in this one, you both add and subtract. Which happens first? Does it matter? Try both ways and see.
25,745 people.
24,500 + 3,120 = 27,620
27,620 − 1,875 = 25,745
🟡 Tier 2, Decimals
Problem 3
A plank is 4.5 m long. Two pieces measuring 1.15 m each are cut off. How much plank is left?
Hidden step: TWO pieces are cut off. Find the total removed first: 1.15 + 1.15 = 2.30. Then subtract from 4.5. Line up your decimal points!
2.2 m remaining.
1.15 + 1.15 = 2.30
4.50 − 2.30 = 2.20
Problem 4
Mia runs 2.75 km to the park, then 1.8 km to her grandmother's house. After visiting, she runs home the same way. How far does she run in total before getting home?
Return journey, just like Alex! She runs the same route back. Draw all 4 parts in your bar model: park, grandmother, grandmother back, home.
9.1 km total.
2.75 + 1.80 + 1.80 + 2.75 = 9.10
🟣 Tier 3, Extension
Problem 5 ⭐
A recipe needs 0.75 kg of flour per batch. Ava wants to make 3 batches. The bag of flour she has weighs 2.5 kg. How much flour will be left in the bag after she bakes all 3 batches?
Two hidden steps: first find total flour used (0.75 × 3 = ?). Then subtract from 2.5. Draw a bar model where the whole is the bag and the parts are each batch plus the leftovers.
0.25 kg left.
0.75 × 3 = 2.25 (total used)
2.50 − 2.25 = 0.25 (remaining)

You've mastered the 4-step protocol. Ready to think like a mathematician? Here are three challenges to stretch your thinking.

Task 1, Specialising (Create Your Own)
Write a 2-step word problem where the hidden step is finding the total BEFORE you can find the difference.
Your problem must use these numbers: 245, 380, and 175.

Write the problem, then solve it showing all steps. Hint: Think about problems where two amounts need to be added first, then something is subtracted from that total.

Mathematician's thinking:

Example problem: A school collected 245 books in Week 1 and 380 books in Week 2. They gave 175 books to the library. How many books do they have left?

Solution:

Hidden Step 1: Find total books collected
245 + 380 = 625 books

Hidden Step 2: Subtract books given away
625 − 175 = 450 books left

Why this works: The bar model shows the whole (total books) split into "given away" and "kept". To find the kept amount, you first need the whole amount. That's why finding the total is a hidden step.
Task 2, Justifying (Explain Your Reasoning)
A student solved this problem two different ways:

Problem: A shop had 1,250 apples. They sold 478 on Monday and 356 on Tuesday. How many are left?

Method 1: One bar model showing 1,250 at the top, with 478 and 356 as parts, and the unknown as a third part.

Method 2: Two bar models, first showing 478 + 356 = 834, then showing 1,250 − 834 = 416.

Both students got the same answer: 416 apples. Which bar model approach is better, and why?

Mathematician's thinking:

Both methods are correct and give 416 apples. But the two-model approach is better for avoiding errors.

With Method 1, you have one bar model with three parts: you can see 478 and 356, but the problem hides the fact that you need to add them first. A student might try to subtract each separately (1,250 − 478 − 356 = 416) and get the same answer by luck, but without understanding why.

With Method 2, the hidden step becomes visible:

  • First bar shows what you must calculate: 478 + 356 = 834 (total sold)
  • Second bar shows what you then calculate: 1,250 − 834 = 416 (left)

The lesson: When you draw TWO models instead of one, each step is separate and checkable. The hidden step is no longer hidden.
Task 3, Generalising (Find the Pattern)
When you solve a multi-step problem, does it matter what ORDER you do the steps in?

Think about this problem: Sam had $500. He spent $125 on shoes and $230 on a jacket. How much is left?

Try solving it in two different orders. Does the order matter? Can you write a rule about when order matters and when it does not?

Mathematician's thinking:

Order 1: 500 − 125 = 375, then 375 − 230 = 145

Order 2: 500 − 230 = 270, then 270 − 125 = 145

Same answer! Order does not matter in this case.

The rule:

  • If the steps are independent (subtracting different amounts from the same total), order does not matter. You get the same answer.
  • If one step's answer feeds into the next step as a NEW quantity (like calculating a total first, then using that total), the order matters.
Why this matters: Understanding when order matters helps you set up your bar models correctly. Independent steps can be drawn as separate branches. Dependent steps must be drawn one after the other.

✅ Key Takeaway to Emphasise

Drawing the Bar Model works exactly the same whether the numbers are whole numbers or decimals. The structure of the problem does not change, only the arithmetic changes. If you can draw it, you can solve it.
