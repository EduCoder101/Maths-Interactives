Learning Intention and Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning to use visual models to understand the relationship between numbers in a word problem before calculating an answer.

Success Criteria

  • I can identify if a word problem is asking me to find a missing part or the total whole.
  • I can draw a bar model to represent a whole number equation.
  • I can use a visual model to determine the correct operation (addition or subtraction).
Card 1 of 13

Word problems love to trick us

Figuring out whether you need to add or subtract can be tricky. A great strategy is to draw the maths first. Once you can see it, you cannot be tricked.

Card 2 of 13

Read this problem

Byron Shire had a population of 35,084.
14,230 people lived in the town centre.
The rest lived in the surrounding hills.

How many people lived in the hills?
Think about it

Don't calculate yet. Do you add these numbers or subtract them? How do you know?

The Reasoning

We know the total (whole), 35,084, and we know one part, 14,230. We're looking for the other part. That means we subtract.

If you draw this before calculating, it becomes obvious. That's exactly what we're going to learn to do.

The Bar Model

Part-Part-Whole

Every addition or subtraction problem has the same structure: a whole made up of two parts. The Bar Model makes this visible.

Card 5 of 13

The Golden Rule

The Golden Rule
➕ We know both parts
Add to find the whole
➖ We know the whole and one part
Subtract to find the missing part
Card 6 of 13

Worked Answer, Byron Shire

Worked Answer

Operation: Subtraction, whole minus known part

35,084
−14,230
20,854
20,854 people lived in the hills

Missing Parts Challenge

Look at the wheels below. Some pieces are missing. Can you calculate the decimal value of the missing piece?

Challenge 1
The wheel has two parts: 0.35 and 0.40. What is the value of the missing part?
Current: 0.75
Solution

1. Add the known parts: 0.35 + 0.40 = 0.75

2. Subtract from the whole (1.00): 1.00 - 0.75 = 0.25
