Lesson 9: Problem Solving Clinic

Applying strategies independently and identifying extraneous information

🏥

Learning Intention & Success Criteria ▼

Learning Intention

We are learning to apply our chosen strategies independently while identifying extraneous (useless) information in word problems.

Success Criteria

  • I can ignore numbers in a word problem that do not help me solve it
  • I can work independently to solve problems using the most efficient strategy
  • I can justify why I chose a specific strategy for a specific problem
Phase 1 · The Hook, 5 mins
The Messy Problem

🎯 Solve this, all the information you need is here

John is 12 years old. He has 3 dogs. He goes to the shop with $20.00 and buys a toy for $14.50. It takes him 15 minutes to walk home.
How much change does he have?
John is 12 years old. He has 3 dogs. He goes to the shop with $20.00 and buys a toy for $14.50. It takes him 15 minutes to walk home.
How much change does he have?
✅ Useful numbers
$20.00 and $14.50
These are needed to find the change.
❌ Extraneous information
12, 3, 15
Put there purely to distract.
✅ Answer

$20.00 − $14.50 = $5.50 change

📚 The Term

Numbers in a word problem that do not help you solve it are called Extraneous Information. Real-world problems and test questions often include them. Your job is to identify them and cross them out before you calculate.

📋 Teacher Notes, Running the Hook ▼
  • After students answer $5.50, ask: "Did you use all five numbers in the problem?" Most will say no. Ask: "Why were those extra numbers there?"
  • Explicitly introduce the term extraneous information. Write it on the board. Ask: "What does extraneous mean?" (Irrelevant. Not needed. A distraction.)
  • Emphasise: from now on, their first job when reading a word problem is to ask, "Does this number actually help me answer the question?"
Phase 2 · Clinic Setup (I Do), 10 mins
How the Clinic works

🏥 What is a Problem Solving Clinic?

Today the class runs as three simultaneous streams. You will work on the problem set that is right for where you are. The teacher will run short, focused workshops at the front for students who need extra support.

Tier 1
🔵 Whole Number Mastery
2-step word problems using whole numbers, populations, shopping scenarios, distances. Focus: clean algorithm work with trading across zeros.
🎯 Teacher Action: Pull this group to the floor. Use MAB blocks for a final explicit reteach of the formal subtraction algorithm involving trading.
Tier 2
🟡 Proficient Decimals
Bank account-style problems using decimals to 2 places. Deposits, withdrawals, and running balances. Focus: decimal point alignment in the written algorithm.
🎯 Teacher Action: Check in with this group to ensure decimal points are perfectly aligned.
Tier 3
🟣 Extension
Missing Digit puzzles and 3-step decimal problems. Use inverse operations to reason backwards and find unknown digits in a completed algorithm.
🎯 Teacher Action: Monitor and challenge, ask students to justify each missing digit using inverse reasoning.

📌 The Non-Negotiable Rule

⚠️ For Every Single Question

Regardless of which tier you are on, the Radar for Error (Estimation) must be written down before you calculate. This is not optional. Every question. Every time.

Phase 3 · Clinic Rotations (We Do / You Do), 40 mins
Work independently on your problem set
Tier 1, Whole Number Mastery
🔵 2-Step Whole Number Problems
Work through these carefully. Show your Radar estimate before you calculate. Watch out for extraneous information.
Tier 1 · Problem A
A school has 1,205 students. At the start of Term 3, 48 students enrol and 23 students leave to move to another school. The school has 14 classrooms. How many students are at the school at the end of enrolment?
⚠️ Extraneous information is hidden in this problem.
Extraneous: 14 classrooms (not needed). Useful: 1,205 students + 48 enrolled − 23 left. Estimate: 1,200 + 50 − 20 = 1,230.
1,230 students.
1,205 + 48 = 1,253
1,253 − 23 = 1,230
Tier 1 · Problem B
A fruit shop starts the week with 2,400 kg of produce. On Monday they sell 347 kg. On Tuesday they receive a delivery of 580 kg. The shop has been open for 12 years. How much produce is in the shop after Tuesday's delivery?
Extraneous: 12 years. Two steps: subtract sales, then add the delivery. Estimate first!
2,633 kg.
2,400 − 347 = 2,053
2,053 + 580 = 2,633
Tier 1 · Problem C
A council has a budget of $45,000 for road repairs. They spend $18,760 fixing potholes and $9,400 repainting lines. Road works take 6 days to complete. How much budget remains?
Extraneous: 6 days. Add both expenses first, then subtract from $45,000. Or subtract twice in sequence, same result!
$16,840 remaining.
18,760 + 9,400 = 28,160
45,000 − 28,160 = 16,840
Tier 2, Proficient Decimals
🟡 Bank Account Problems
Keep a running balance. Remember: decimal points must line up perfectly in your algorithm. Show your Radar estimate before each calculation.
Tier 2 · Problem A
Maya's bank account has a balance of $450.75. She deposits $120.00. Then she withdraws $35.50 to pay for a birthday present. What is her final balance?
Two steps: add the deposit, then subtract the withdrawal. Line up the decimal points. Estimate: $450 + $120 − $35 ≈ $535.
$535.25
$450.75 + $120.00 = $570.75
$570.75 − $35.50 = $535.25
Tier 2 · Problem B
Tom starts with $1,250.00 in his account. He deposits his pay of $487.50. He then pays three bills: electricity $142.80, internet $59.95, and phone $45.00. What is his balance after paying all three bills?
Add the pay first. Then add all three bills together (hidden step!) and subtract the total. Or subtract each bill in sequence.
$1,489.75
$1,250.00 + $487.50 = $1,737.50
$142.80 + $59.95 + $45.00 = $247.75
$1,737.50 − $247.75 = $1,489.75
Tier 2 · Problem C ⭐
A school fundraising account has $3,045.60. The school runs two stalls and earns $284.50 and $196.75. They spend $512.40 on new library books. How much is left in the account?
Add both stall earnings to the starting amount, then subtract the book purchase. Three decimal numbers to add, line up those points!
$3,014.45
$284.50 + $196.75 = $481.25
$3,045.60 + $481.25 = $3,526.85
$3,526.85 − $512.40 = $3,014.45

Tier 3 · Problem C, 3-Step Decimals
A landscaper has a budget of $2,500.00. She buys 3 bags of soil at $24.75 each, a garden hose for $38.90, and 4 paving stones at $15.60 each. How much of her budget remains?
Two hidden steps: 3 × $24.75 and 4 × $15.60. Find all individual totals first, add them together, then subtract from $2,500.
$2,323.85 remaining.
3 × $24.75 = $74.25
4 × $15.60 = $62.40
$74.25 + $38.90 + $62.40 = $175.55
$2,500.00 − $175.55 = $2,324.45
Note: Check your own calculation carefully step by step!
Task 1: Specialising, Design a Problem
Write a word problem that contains exactly 2 pieces of extraneous information AND 1 hidden step. Your problem must have a final answer of 350.
Design the problem, do not just solve one. Show your working to prove your answer is correct.
Example Response

A baker made 500 cupcakes on Monday. The shop is open 6 days a week. She sold 85 in the morning and 65 in the afternoon. Her oven can hold 24 cupcakes at a time. How many cupcakes are left?

Analysis:
Extraneous: "6 days a week" and "24 cupcakes at a time" (neither helps us find how many are left).

Hidden step: Finding the total sold. We must calculate 85 + 65 = 150 before we can subtract from 500.

Solution: 500 − (85 + 65) = 500 − 150 = 350
Task 2: Conjecturing, Test Your Reasoning
Extraneous information always makes a word problem harder to solve. Always, sometimes, or never?
Choose your answer and explain your reasoning.
Analysis

Answer: SOMETIMES.

Extraneous information usually makes problems harder because students have to decide which numbers actually matter. But not always.

Example 1 (Makes it harder):
In a money problem, if you mix the relevant amount (£35) with other money amounts (£20, £50), students easily confuse which numbers to use.

Example 2 (Does not confuse):
"A girl aged 12 with green eyes bought a book for £5. How much is left from £10?" The colour of her eyes is obviously irrelevant and doesn't confuse anyone.

Most confusing when: The extraneous information is the same type as the relevant numbers. For example, extra money amounts in a money problem, or extra distances in a journey problem, because students cannot easily spot which numbers matter.
Task 3: Justifying, Compare Two Solutions
Two students solved the same problem. Their answers match. But are both methods equally correct?
Problem: A school raised £1,250 from a cake sale and £830 from a raffle. They spent £475 on new books. How much money is left?
Student A's Method:
£1,250 − £475 = £775
£775 + £830 = £1,605
Student B's Method:
£1,250 + £830 = £2,080
£2,080 − £475 = £1,605
Both got £1,605.
Your question:

Which approach would you recommend to a younger student, and why?

Analysis

Both are mathematically correct. But Student B's method is better.

Why Student B's approach is stronger:
Student B finds the total money first (£1,250 + £830 = £2,080), then subtracts what was spent. This mirrors the bar model perfectly and matches the language of the problem: "they raised... and raised... then spent".

Why Student A's method works but has a hidden risk:
Student A subtracts first from one amount, then adds. This works here, but imagine a problem with 5 money amounts instead of 2. Student A would have to remember which amounts were already included, which creates confusion and room for error.

What to teach younger students:
"Find your total first, then subtract what goes out." This scales better, works for all problems, and is easier to check using a bar model.
📋 Teacher Notes, Clinic Management ▼

Timing guide for the 40-minute block:

  • Minutes 0–10: Tier 1 group to the floor with MAB blocks. Reteach formal subtraction algorithm with trading, use a concrete example like 4,003 − 1,567. Have students build the number with MABs and physically trade the blocks.
  • Minutes 10–20: Return Tier 1 to independent work. Move to Tier 2 and check their written algorithms. Look specifically for misaligned decimal points. Correct these immediately and privately.
  • Minutes 20–35: Circulate across all three groups. Tier 3 students should be attempting the missing digit puzzles by working column by column, right to left.
  • Minutes 35–40: Signal wrap-up. Ask one student from each tier to hold up their best piece of working. Prepare the Tier 2 bank account problem for the plenary.

✅ The Key Point

There is no single correct tool, the question is about efficiency and accuracy. A number like $450.75 + $120.00 can be done mentally. Three numbers with different decimal places might warrant a written algorithm. Many separate transactions are exactly what a calculator is designed for. A mathematician chooses, they do not just default to one method every time.

📋 Vocabulary Recap

New term today
Extraneous Information
Numbers or details in a word problem that are not needed to find the answer. Their purpose is to distract, your job is to ignore them.
From Lesson 8
Hidden Step
A calculation buried inside the problem that must be completed before you can find the final answer. Often signalled by words like "each", "both", "same way".
