Learning Intention & Success Criteria ▼

Learning Intention

We are learning to use estimation as a "Radar for Error" to check whether our calculations are reasonable, and to align numbers correctly in vertical algorithms.

Success Criteria

  • I can round numbers to get a quick estimate before calculating
  • I can set up a vertical algorithm with digits correctly aligned by place value
  • I can use my estimate to check whether my final answer is reasonable
Phase 1 · Hook
What's wrong with this calculation?

📡 Introducing the Radar for Error

The greatest mathematicians don't just calculate perfectly, they know roughly what the answer should be before they even pick up a pen. That's your Radar for Error.

Today we're going to build that radar, then use it to check our formal algorithms.

🔍 Spot the Error

💬 Think about it

Every digit was added correctly, 4+3=7, 5+5=10, so why is $8,062 the wrong answer?

Phase 2 · The Radar for Error
Could you have spotted this sooner?
💡 Think about it

If you had used your Radar for Error, you would have known $8,062 was impossible, before you even checked the working. How?

$4,562 is already more than $4,000. Adding just $35 should give something close to $4,600, so an answer of $8,062 is clearly wrong.

That's the Radar for Error. Estimate first → calculate → check your answer makes sense.

Go Deeper: Mastering Error Detection

▼

You have mastered the Radar Estimate technique. Now push your thinking further. Analyse real mistakes, test your conjectures, and create your own problem situations.

Task 1 · Specialising
Find the Hidden Error
Analyse a student's working and identify the exact mistake.

A student calculated 4,328 + 2,756 and got this answer:

4,328
+2,756
6,074

First: use your Radar Estimate. Round 4,300 + 2,800 = 7,100. The answer should be close to 7,100.

But 6,074 is too small. Something is wrong. Can you trace through the column addition and find exactly where the student made the error? What did they forget to do?

Analysis

Let me trace through the column addition:

Units: 8 + 6 = 14. Write 4, carry 1. ✓

Tens: 2 + 5 + 1(carry) = 8. But wait, the student wrote 7, not 8! They got 2 + 5 = 7 and forgot to add the carry.

Hundreds: 3 + 7 = 10. Write 0, carry 1. ✓

Thousands: 4 + 2 + 1(carry) = 7. But the student wrote 6! They got 4 + 2 = 6 and didn't add the carry from the hundreds.

The correct answer is 7,084.

The error: The student forgot to carry from the tens column. This mistake cascaded, missing the carry to hundreds meant the next column was also wrong. Always check: did I carry? Did I add the carry to the next column?
Task 2 · Conjecturing
Always, Sometimes, or Never?
Test a mathematical claim about estimation.

Claim: "If your estimate is close to your actual answer, the calculation must be correct."

Is this Always true, Sometimes true, or Never true?

Answer: SOMETIMES

An estimate being close to your actual answer is a good sign, but it's not proof of correctness.

Counter-example 1: 456 + 344. Estimate: 500 + 300 = 800. Actual: 800. The estimate matches perfectly! But that's lucky, it doesn't mean all our calculations are correct.

Counter-example 2: A student calculates 456 + 344 = 790 (wrong!). But their estimate is 500 + 300 = 800. They might think "close enough, must be right", but they made an error.

When it works: A close estimate is a useful check. If your estimate is far from your answer, you should recheck. But being close isn't a guarantee, you still need to verify the calculation itself.

Key insight: The Radar Estimate is a sanity check, not a proof. Use it to spot suspicious answers. If they're far apart, something is probably wrong. But even if they're close, always double-check your carrying and place values!
Task 3 · Justifying
Create Your Own Error Scenario
Design problems where a specific mistake produces an exact result.

Write an addition of two 3-digit numbers where a student who forgets to carry from the tens column would get an answer that is exactly 100 less than the correct answer. Find three different examples.

Hint: Think about what must be true of the tens digits for a carry to happen. When the tens add up to 10 or more, a carry goes to the hundreds. If that carry is forgotten, the hundreds digit will be 1 too small, that's 100 less in value.

Three examples

Example 1: 156 + 283 = 439 (correct). Tens: 5 + 8 = 13, so carry 1. Without carry: 1__ + 2__ = 3__ instead of 4__. Student gets 339. Difference: 439 - 339 = 100. ✓

Example 2: 275 + 164 = 439 (correct). Tens: 7 + 6 = 13, so carry 1. Without carry, student gets 339. Difference: 100. ✓

Example 3: 347 + 291 = 638 (correct). Tens: 4 + 9 = 13, so carry 1. Without carry, student gets 538. Difference: 638 - 538 = 100. ✓

The pattern: For a difference of exactly 100, the tens digits must add to at least 10 (so a carry is generated and "lost"). Can you find more examples? Try starting with numbers where the tens add to 10, 11, 12, etc.
