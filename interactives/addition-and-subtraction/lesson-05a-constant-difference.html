🎯 The Core Concept

Subtraction is just finding the distance between two numbers. If you move both numbers by the same amount, the distance stays exactly the same.

This is Constant Difference, and it eliminates every single zero in one strategic move.

Phase 2 · Explicit Instruction (I Do), 15 mins
Constant Difference: The Strategy
1
The Problem

Let us look at a tricky subtraction problem:

10,000 − 4,876

What makes this hard? We have zeros in the top number. If we try the standard column method, we would need to trade across all those zeros, which is slow and confusing.

2
The Big Idea
Constant Difference

Subtraction is really just finding the distance between two numbers on a number line. If you move both numbers by the same amount, the distance between them stays exactly the same.

This is the secret to avoiding all that trading! We can shift both numbers by any amount we choose, as long as we shift them both by the same amount.

Worked Example: Step by Step

Problem: 10,000 − 4,876

Step 1: Identify the problem

We have zeros in 10,000. This will need trading if we use the standard method.

Step 2: Shift both numbers

Subtract 1 from both numbers to eliminate the zeros:

10,000 − 1 = 9,999
4,876 − 1 = 4,875
Step 3: Solve (no trading needed!)

Now we solve 9,999 − 4,875:

9 9 9 9
− 4 8 7 5
5 1 2 4

Notice: no trading, no crossing out, no confusion!

The Answer

5,124, and we got there cleanly and efficiently.

6
Key Points to Remember
  • Why it works: Moving both numbers the same amount preserves the distance between them.
  • When to use it: Any time the larger number ends in zeros (like 100, 1,000, 10,000, or even $50.00).
  • How much to shift: Usually subtract 1 from both numbers, but you can shift by any amount that eliminates the zeros.
  • The advantage: One simple shift beats multiple trading steps, cleaner, faster, less error-prone.

💬 Discussion Questions ▾
  • 🤔 Why does this strategy work? (The distance between two numbers stays constant when you shift both by the same amount)
  • 🤔 When is this easier than the standard algorithm? (When the top number has trailing zeros, 1,000, 5,000, $100.00, because every zero causes a trading cascade)
  • 🤔 Could you shift by 2 instead of 1? (Yes! Any constant shift preserves the difference, shift by whatever removes the most zeros most efficiently)
  • 🤔 What if there are no zeros in the top number? (The standard algorithm is probably the better choice, always pick the strategy that suits the numbers)

Question 14
Spot the Error: A student used Constant Difference on 4,000 − 1,567. They shifted both numbers UP by 3 and got 4,003 − 1,570 = 2,433. What went wrong?
The answer (2,433) is actually correct! But think about WHY they shifted UP. Would that help? What direction usually works best?
The error is the direction.
Shifting UP by 3 gives 4,003 − 1,570, which still has a zero and is harder to calculate. They should have shifted DOWN by 3 to get 3,997 − 1,564, which removes the zero from 4,000. Constant Difference works best when you shift to remove zeros, not to create more work!
Question 15
Explain Why: Why does shifting both numbers by the same amount give you the same answer? (Use the idea of a gap or distance on a number line.)
Think of two dots on a number line. If you slide BOTH dots the same distance to the left or right, what happens to the gap between them?
The gap stays the same!
Imagine two dots on a number line: one at 5,000 and one at 2,347. The gap between them is 2,653. Now slide BOTH dots left by 1 (to 4,999 and 2,346). The gap is still 2,653! The distance doesn't change just because you moved both numbers. That's why 5,000 − 2,347 = 4,999 − 2,346.

Question 15
Explain Why: Explain in your own words why it's okay to add the same decimal amount to both numbers if you're subtracting. (Hint: think about money in your pocket.)
If you have $50 and owe someone $18.32, and the price goes UP by $0.50 for everything, you now have $50.50 and owe $18.82. Did the difference (how much extra money you have left) change?
The gap doesn't change when both sides change equally.
Imagine you have $50 and owe someone $18.32. You'd have $50 − $18.32 = $31.68 left over. Now suppose everything costs $0.32 more: you have $50.32 and owe $18.64. But your extra money is still $50.32 − $18.64 = $31.68! Adding the same amount to both numbers is like saying "everything got more expensive by the same amount", but the difference between what you have and what you owe stays the same.

Task 2: Conjecturing
The constant difference strategy works just as well for addition as it does for subtraction.
Is this always true, sometimes true, or never true? Make a conjecture and test it with an example.

Try this example:
Original: 45 + 30 = 75 If I add 5 to both numbers: (45 + 5) + (30 + 5) = 50 + 35 = ? What happened to the answer? Did it stay the same?

Mathematician's Thinking

My conjecture: The strategy only works for subtraction, not for addition. Never for addition in the same way.

Let me test: 45 + 30 = 75

If I add 5 to both: 50 + 35 = 85

Wait! The answer changed! It went from 75 to 85, which is 10 more. That's double the 5 I added!

Why does this happen? When you add the same number to both addends, you're adding that number TWICE to the total. In subtraction, adding to both numbers cancels out because one number is being subtracted. But in addition, both changes make the total grow.

Key insight: The constant difference strategy works for subtraction because it measures the gap between numbers. In addition, there is no gap, we're combining quantities. Adding a constant to both makes the total increase, not stay the same.
Task 3: Justifying
A student tried to use constant difference on an addition problem. What went wrong?
Find and explain the error in their working.

Student's work:
"I solved 397 + 248 by adding 3 to 397 to get 400, and adding 3 to 248 to get 251. So 397 + 248 = 400 + 251 = 651."

The correct answer is 397 + 248 = 645. But the student got 651.

Mathematician's Thinking

What went wrong: The student added 3 to BOTH numbers. That means they added 3 twice to the total!

Correct answer: 397 + 248 = 645

Student's answer: 400 + 251 = 651

Difference: 651 - 645 = 6 (which is 3 + 3, the amount added twice)

How to fix it: In addition, you can't use constant difference the same way. Instead, you must balance it: if you add to one number, you must SUBTRACT from the other to keep the total the same.

Correct method: 397 + 248 → Add 3 to 397 to get 400, subtract 3 from 248 to get 245. Then 400 + 245 = 645. The answer stays the same!
Phase 5 · Plenary (Wrap Up), 5 mins
When is the standard algorithm the worst choice?

💬 Discussion Question

A student says: "I always use the standard algorithm because I was taught it and it always works."

When would you tell them not to use it?

✅ The Answer

The standard algorithm becomes the worst choice when the top number has lots of zeros (like 10,000, 5,000, or $100.00). Every zero forces a trading cascade with multiple opportunities for error. Constant Difference eliminates this completely by shifting both numbers to remove the zeros.

📐 The General Rule

An efficient mathematician chooses their strategy based on the numbers. The standard algorithm is powerful and has its place, but it is not always the most efficient tool. Recognising when to switch is a mark of genuine mathematical thinking.

📋 Today's Strategy, Quick Reference

Constant Difference
  1. Subtract 1 from both numbers (or any constant amount)
  2. Repeat until zeros are gone
  3. Solve with standard algorithm, no trading needed

Best for: Numbers with trailing zeros (1,000, 5,000, 10,000, $50.00, 100.0, etc.)
