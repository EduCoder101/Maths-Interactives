Learning Intention

We are learning to plan a budget using our addition and subtraction strategies, proving that our calculations are reasonable and accurate.

  • I can estimate the cost of multiple items before calculating the exact amount
  • I can explain why I chose to calculate a certain way (mental, written, calculator)

Strategy Justification Sentence Starters

Students must use one of these to explain their method choice.

"I used a written algorithm because..."
e.g. "...I had to add several decimals with different numbers of decimal places."
"I used a mental strategy because..."
e.g. "...the numbers were whole hundreds and I could use levelling."
"I used a calculator because..."
e.g. "...there were 7 items with complex decimal prices and I wanted to make sure I didn't make an error."

Teacher Notes: Modelling the Task

  • Model the estimation step using the first two items from the relevant tier's price list. Show the rounding clearly on the board before revealing the calculation.
  • Explicitly model a justification sentence for each method type so students understand what "explain your strategy" actually looks like in practice.
  • Emphasise that the goal of this task is reasonableness: an exact answer that is wildly different from the estimate is not acceptable. They must go back and find the error.
  • Remind Tier 2/3 students that some items on their list have a hidden step (quantity multiplied by unit price). They must identify these before estimating.

Extension Tasks for Mathematicians

You have mastered the core task. These three challenges will push your thinking further. Each one asks you to conjecture, justify, and generalise, the marks of a true mathematician.

Extension Task 1: Conjecturing
When planning a budget, rounding every cost UP before adding gives you a safer estimate than rounding to the nearest hundred. Always, sometimes, or never?
Explain your thinking with an example or counterexample.
Mathematician's Response

Answer: ALWAYS for staying under budget, but with a caveat.

If you round every cost UP, your estimate will always be equal to or greater than the real total. This is mathematically guaranteed. So if your rounded total fits within the budget, the real total definitely will too.

For example:

  • Real costs: $450, $175, $380, $290, $350
  • Rounding UP: $500, $200, $400, $300, $400 = $1,800
  • Real total: $1,645

The estimate is $155 higher than reality, but you're safe.

But here's the downside: Rounding UP might make you think you can't afford something when actually you can. With $2,000 to spend, you'd estimate $1,800 left for decorations. But the real leftover is $355. So "safer" means "overcautious", but that's not always a bad thing in budgeting!

Extension Task 2: Justifying Your Thinking
A student planned a school fete budget with $2,000 to spend. Here are their costs: Bouncy castle $450, Face painting $175, Food stall supplies $380, Prizes $290, DJ $350. The student estimated by rounding each to the nearest hundred: $500 + $200 + $400 + $300 + $400 = $1,800. They said: "We have $200 left over for decorations." Is the student's conclusion correct? Explain why or why not.
Show the exact total and compare it to the student's estimate.
Mathematician's Response

Answer: No, the student's conclusion is wrong.

The student calculated correctly that the rounded estimate is $1,800, but they didn't calculate the real total.

Let me work it out:

  • $450 + $175 = $625
  • $625 + $380 = $1,005
  • $1,005 + $290 = $1,295
  • $1,295 + $350 = $1,645 (real total)

The real leftover is: $2,000 minus $1,645 = $355, not $200.

Here's what went wrong: The student's rounding overestimated the actual cost. They rounded each number up, so $1,800 (estimate) is higher than $1,645 (reality). This is good news: they actually have MORE money left than they think!

The lesson: If your exact answer is significantly different from your estimate, you must investigate. In this case, the student was lucky, but they didn't know why their estimate was high, and that's a problem.

Extension Task 3: Generalising Across All Problems
Think about all the strategies you have learned in this unit: Radar for Error, bar models, hidden steps, extraneous information, and strategy choice. Write 3 rules that a Year 4 student should follow when solving ANY multi-step word problem. Your rules should be general enough to work for any problem, not just budget problems.
Make them clear, memorable, and practical.
Mathematician's Response

Here are three essential rules:

Rule 1: Read and Prepare (Before You Calculate)

Read the whole problem twice. Cross out any information you don't need. Underline the question you're answering. Draw a bar model if the problem is tricky. If there's a hidden step (like multiplying first), identify it now, not halfway through your calculation.

Rule 2: Estimate First (Your Safety Net)

Round the numbers in a sensible way and work out an approximate answer. Write it down. This is your Radar for Error. If your final answer is wildly different, something has gone wrong and you need to find the mistake before you hand it in.

Rule 3: Break It Into Steps (One Step at a Time)

Don't try to do everything in one go. Write out each step clearly. After each step, check: "Does this number make sense?" If you're not sure which calculation tool to use (mental, written, or calculator), choose the one that matches the numbers you have. Always show your working so you can find any mistakes.

These three tasks will help you see how the strategies you learned today apply to every maths problem you solve, not just fete budgets.
