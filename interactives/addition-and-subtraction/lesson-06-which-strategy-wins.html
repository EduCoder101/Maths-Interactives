Learning Intention

We are learning to analyse an equation and choose the most efficient tool to solve it: our brain, our pen, or a calculator.

Success Criteria

  • I can scan an equation for friendly numbers or patterns that suggest a mental strategy
  • I can identify when a written algorithm or calculator is the better choice
  • I can explain why a calculator might be slower than a mental strategy for certain equations

🔑 The Big Idea

Every equation has a best tool. The skill isn't just being able to use each tool, it's knowing which to pick and why. That's what makes a mathematician efficient.

T3 · Q5, Always / Sometimes / Never
Open Reasoning

"A calculator is always the safest choice because it never makes mistakes."

Is this Always, Sometimes, or Never true? Justify your answer.

Model AnswerSometimes true. A calculator doesn't make arithmetic errors, but the person pressing the buttons can. If you mis-key a number, the calculator gives a wrong answer confidently. For simple equations with friendly numbers, a mental strategy is both faster and just as reliable, and you're less likely to make a keying error. The calculator is a powerful tool, but it doesn't replace the need for number sense.

Tier 1: Focus on the sort activity first. Circulate and guide students through the Mental column using bridging and levelling strategies they've practised in prior lessons. Resist the urge to give them the answer to the sorting, ask "What do you notice about the numbers?" instead.

Tier 2: The written justification for Calculator questions is the core challenge. Prompt with: "What property of those numbers makes a calculator better?" Accept reasonable answers, the goal is articulating mathematical reasoning, not a fixed script.

Tier 3: Q4 and Q5 are the extension targets. Q4 challenges the misconception that "big numbers = algorithm". Q5 tests metacognitive awareness about tool limitations. Both require multi-sentence reasoning, not just a number answer.

In this extension, you'll become a strategy analyst. Instead of just solving equations, you'll solve the same equation using multiple strategies and compare their efficiency. Then you'll make and test conjectures about when each strategy wins.

Task 1: Specialising
Solve each equation using TWO different strategies, then decide which was more efficient.
Below are four calculations. For each one, work it out in your head (mental strategy), then work it out using the written algorithm (pen and paper). Finally, decide which strategy was more efficient and why.

Equation 1: 4,002 - 3,998

Mathematician's Thinking

Mental strategy: I notice the numbers are very close. 3,998 is 2 away from 4,000, and 4,002 is 2 away from 4,000 on the other side. So the gap is just 4. Instant!

Written algorithm: I'd line them up and subtract column by column. But wait... the ones place: 2 - 8? I can't do that. I need to borrow from the tens. But the tens place is 0, so I need to borrow from the hundreds. But the hundreds is also 0. So I have to borrow all the way from the thousands. This takes 5 steps and is error-prone.

Winner: Mental strategy by miles. For numbers this close, counting on is efficient and reliable.

The insight: We assumed "subtract means write it down". But we didn't have to. When numbers are close together, your brain can do better than the algorithm.

Equation 2: 3,456 + 2,789

Mathematician's Thinking

Mental strategy: Hmm. 3,456 plus 2,789... I'd try breaking it into parts: 3,000 + 2,000 = 5,000, then 456 + 789. But 456 + 789 still feels messy. I have to remember to carry the hundred, then add... actually, this is getting complicated. I'd lose track.

Written algorithm: Line them up. Ones: 6 + 9 = 15, write 5 carry 1. Tens: 5 + 8 + 1 = 14, write 4 carry 1. Hundreds: 4 + 7 + 1 = 12, write 2 carry 1. Thousands: 3 + 2 + 1 = 6. Answer: 6,245. Clean, step-by-step, low error.

Winner: Written algorithm. The numbers are large and there's no obvious mental shortcut. The algorithm gives certainty.

The insight: Large numbers with no friendly bonds are exactly what the algorithm was designed for. Trust the structure.

Equation 3: 5,000 - 1

Mathematician's Thinking

Mental strategy: 5,000 - 1? That's just 4,999. Done. No thinking needed.

Written algorithm: Line them up. Ones: 0 - 1? I can't do that. Borrow from tens. But tens is 0, so borrow from hundreds. But hundreds is 0. Borrow from thousands. So thousands becomes 4, then I have to trade down through all three columns... it's a trading nightmare, and easy to make mistakes.

Winner: Mental strategy crushes it. When one number is a power of 10 and we're subtracting 1, the algorithm is painful.

The insight: Zeros are trouble for subtraction algorithms because they force trading chains. Your brain handles them instantly.

Equation 4: 248 + 252

Mathematician's Thinking

Mental strategy: Both numbers are near 250. 248 is 2 below, 252 is 2 above. So the answer is 250 + 250 = 500. Super fast.

Written algorithm: Line them up. Ones: 8 + 2 = 10, write 0 carry 1. Tens: 4 + 5 + 1 = 10, write 0 carry 1. Hundreds: 2 + 2 + 1 = 5. Answer: 500. Also pretty straightforward.

Winner: Either works, but mental strategy is faster because the numbers are friendly pairs.

The insight: Numbers near the same round value suggest a mental strategy. But the algorithm isn't bad here either. Sometimes both strategies are good.

Now, reflect on your answers:

Which calculations did the written algorithm win for you? Which did mental strategies win? What made the difference? Write 2-3 sentences exploring the pattern you notice.

Task 2: Conjecturing
Test a claim about strategy reliability.
A student says: "A written algorithm is always the safest strategy because it works for every calculation."

Is this claim ALWAYS, SOMETIMES, or NEVER true? Explain your reasoning.

Mathematician's Thinking

The student said "always": It's true that the written algorithm DOES work mechanically for every calculation. You can use it for 5,000 - 1, and it will give you the right answer eventually.

But "safest" means something different: "Safe" usually means "least likely to make an error". And here's the key: for calculations like 5,000 - 1, the algorithm requires trading across three columns. Trading is where most students make errors. One column gone wrong, and your answer is ruined.

Mental strategies can be SAFER: If you count on from 3,998 to 4,002, you take two steps. If you get one step wrong, you're only off by 1. With the algorithm, a single trading mistake cascades.

Verdict: SOMETIMES true. The algorithm works for every calculation mechanically, but it's not always the SAFEST choice. For numbers with lots of zeros or numbers very close together, mental strategies can be both faster AND less error-prone.

The insight: "Works" and "safest" are not the same thing. An algorithm that works mechanically can still be a risky choice for a particular set of numbers.
Task 3: Justifying
Evaluate a real student disagreement.
Here's what two real students said:

Student A: "I solved 4,002 - 3,998 using the written algorithm and got 4."

Student B: "I just counted on from 3,998 to 4,002 in my head. The answer is 4."

Student A then says: "You should always use the written algorithm because it's more reliable."

Is Student A right? Write a clear justification.

Mathematician's Thinking

Is Student A right? No. Here's why:

For THIS equation, Student B's strategy is both faster AND more reliable:

  • Student A's written algorithm for 4,002 - 3,998 requires trading across three columns (thousands, hundreds, tens). That's three places where an error can slip in.
  • Student B counts on: 3,998 to 4,000 is 2, then 4,000 to 4,002 is 2. Total: 4. Two simple steps.
  • If Student B gets one step wrong, they're off by 1. If Student A makes a single trading error, they could get 3 or 5 or worse.

Student A is confusing "always use the written algorithm" with "know how to use the written algorithm". Yes, you should know the algorithm. But knowing a strategy and using it blindly are different things. The best mathematicians pick the right tool for the job.

The bigger lesson: Student A had the right answer, but for the wrong reason. The lesson of this whole unit is that smart maths isn't about having one reliable tool. It's about having multiple tools and choosing wisely.
