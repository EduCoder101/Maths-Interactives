Learning Intention & Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning to mentally calculate answers by breaking numbers into their place value parts and jumping to friendly numbers.

Success Criteria

  • I can partition a number by its place value (e.g. thousands, hundreds, tens, ones)
  • I can use an open number line to bridge to the next 10, 100, or whole number
  • I can explain why my mental strategy was efficient

The Big Idea

Algorithms are reliable, but mental strategies are faster when numbers are close to friendly benchmarks like 10, 100, or 1,000.

Head to Key Concepts to learn the two strategies that make this possible.

Card 6 of 26
Partitioning by Place Value

Definition

Breaking a number into its thousands, hundreds, tens, and ones so you can work on each part one at a time.

Example: Break 347 into parts

300+ 40+ 7

Worked example: 562 - 347

562 - 300 = 262
262 - 40 = 222
222 - 7 = 215
Card 7 of 26
Bridging

Definition

Splitting a jump so you land exactly on a friendly number (a multiple of 10, 100...) before continuing.

Example: 38 + 5 bridging through 40

38 + 2 = 40 (friendly!) then 40 + 3 = 43

Card 8 of 26
Friendly Numbers

Definition

A number that is easy to work with, usually a multiple of 10, 100, or 1,000.

Examples of friendly numbers

10 50 100 500 1,000

The point of bridging is to land on one of these first, then continue.

Card 9 of 26
Open Number Line

Definition

A blank number line (not to scale) where you mark start, bridge, and end points to show your mental jumps.

Example layout

The gaps do not need to be to scale. It is a thinking tool, not a measuring tool.

Card 10 of 26

Choosing Your Strategy

Is one of the numbers close to a friendly number?

Yes
Use Bridging
Bridge to the nearby friendly number first, then deal with what is left.
Example
397 + 46

397 is 3 away from 400. Bridge there first. 397 + 3 = 400, then 400 + 43 = 443.

No
Use Partitioning
Split the number being added or subtracted into its place value parts and work through each one in turn.
Example
452 + 236

236 = 200 + 30 + 6. No number sits close to a round number. 452 + 200 = 652, + 30 = 682, + 6 = 688.

Card 11 of 26

The Clue to Look For

Scan the digits at the end of each number. If you spot a 9, 8, 1, or 2 in the ones (or tens) place, that is your signal. The number is almost certainly close enough to a friendly number to make bridging worthwhile.

Bridge, ends in...
...8 ...9 ...1 ...2
e.g. 38, 99, 201, 1,998
Partition, ends in...
...4 ...5 ...6 ...7
e.g. 34, 256, 1,467

There is no single right answer. Both strategies always work. This is about choosing the faster mental path for a given equation.

Example 2 Bridging
56 + 37

56 is 4 away from 60. Instead of partitioning 37 straight away, bridge to 60 first.

1
Find the bridge: how far to the next ten?

56 needs 4 more to reach 60.

56 + 4 = 60 (friendly!)
2
Split 37 to match

We used 4 to bridge. How much of 37 remains?

4+33

Note: this split is not by place value. It is by what we needed to reach 60.

3
Jump from the friendly number

Add the remaining 33 from 60.

60 + 33 = 93
56 + 37 = 93
