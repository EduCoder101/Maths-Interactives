Learning Intention and Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning to use estimation as a "Radar for Error" to check whether our calculations are reasonable, and to align numbers correctly in vertical algorithms.

Success Criteria

  • I can round numbers to get a quick estimate before calculating
  • I can set up a vertical algorithm with digits correctly aligned by place value
  • I can use my estimate to check whether my final answer is reasonable
Card 1 of 11

Introducing the Radar for Error

The greatest mathematicians don't just calculate perfectly, they know roughly what the answer should be before they even pick up a pen. That's your Radar for Error.

Today we're going to build that radar, then use it to check our formal algorithms.

Card 2 of 11

Spot the Error

A school canteen made $4,562 on Monday and $35 on Tuesday. How much did it make altogether?

Someone has set up the algorithm below. The digits have all been added correctly. But the answer is completely wrong. What has gone wrong?

Wrong
What was written:
4,562
+3,5
8,062
The 3 and 5 have been placed in the wrong columns
Correct alignment:
4,562
+35
4,597
$35 sits in the tens and ones columns
Think about it

Every digit was added correctly, 4+3=7, 5+5=10, so why is $8,062 the wrong answer?

Card 3 of 11

Could You Have Spotted This Sooner

Think about it

If you had used your Radar for Error, you would have known $8,062 was impossible, before you even checked the working. How?

$4,562 is already more than $4,000. Adding just $35 should give something close to $4,600, so an answer of $8,062 is clearly wrong.

That's the Radar for Error. Estimate first, calculate, then check your answer makes sense.
