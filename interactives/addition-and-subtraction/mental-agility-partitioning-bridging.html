🔑 The Big Idea

Algorithms are reliable, but mental strategies are faster when numbers are close to "friendly" benchmarks like 10, 100, or 1,000.

Head to Key Concepts to learn the two strategies that make this possible.

Key Concepts
The Two Strategies

Click See an example on each card to see the strategy in action.

Partitioning by Place Value

Breaking a number into its thousands, hundreds, tens, and ones so you can work on each part one at a time.

Break 347 into its place value parts:

300+ 40+ 7

Now subtract each part from 562 one at a time:

562 − 347

562 − 300 = 262 → 262 − 40 = 222 → 222 − 7 = 215

Bridging

Splitting a jump so you land exactly on a friendly number (a multiple of 10, 100…) before continuing.

Add 38 + 5 by bridging through 40:

Why bridge?

38 + 2 = 40 (friendly!) → 40 + 3 = 43. Landing on 40 makes the next step trivial.

Friendly Number

A number that is easy to work with, usually a multiple of 10, 100, or 1,000.

These are all friendly numbers:

10 50 100 500 1,000

The point of bridging is to land on one of these first, then continue.

Open Number Line

A blank number line with no fixed scale, you place numbers where they make sense for the jumps you are making.

Gaps don't have to be to scale, it's a thinking tool, not a measuring tool. Draw a long line, mark your start number, then make your jumps:

Choosing Your Strategy
Partitioning or Bridging? 🤔

Ask yourself one question first:

Is one of the numbers close to a friendly number?

(within about 5–10 of a multiple of 10, 100, or 1,000)

✅ Yes

Use Bridging

Bridge to the nearby friendly number first, then deal with what's left. The closer a number is to a round number, the more time you save.

Example

397 + 46

397 is only 3 away from 400 → bridge there first.
397 + 3 = 400 → 400 + 43 = 443

❌ No

Use Partitioning

Split the number being added or subtracted into its place value parts (hundreds, tens, ones) and work through each part in turn.

Example

452 + 236

236 = 200 + 30 + 6, no number sits close to a round number. Partition it.
452 + 200 = 652 → + 30 = 682 → + 6 = 688

🔍 The clue to look for

Scan the digits at the end of each number. If you spot a 9, 8, 1, or 2 in the ones (or tens) place, that's your signal, the number is almost certainly close enough to a friendly number to make bridging worthwhile.

🟢 Bridge, ends in…

…8 …9 …1 …2

e.g. 38, 99, 201, 1,998

🔵 Partition, ends in…

…4 …5 …6 …7

e.g. 34, 256, 1,467

⚠️ Remember

There's no single right answer, both strategies always work. This is about choosing the faster mental path for a given equation. With practice, you'll spot it instantly.

These tasks go beyond practising strategies. You will investigate when each strategy works best and why.

Task 1: Specialising
Solve 67 + 28 using partitioning. Then solve it again using bridging. Write out every step for both methods. Which one took fewer steps?
Now try the same with 45 + 99. Which strategy was quicker this time?
What is it about the numbers that makes one strategy better than the other?
Model response

For 67 + 28 by partitioning: 60 + 20 = 80, 7 + 8 = 15, 80 + 15 = 95. Three steps.

By bridging: 67 + 3 = 70, 70 + 25 = 95. Two steps. Bridging was quicker because 67 is close to 70.

For 45 + 99 by partitioning: 40 + 90 = 130, 5 + 9 = 14, 130 + 14 = 144. Three steps.

By bridging: 45 + 100 = 145, 145 − 1 = 144. Two steps. Bridging was quicker again because 99 is close to 100.

I first thought partitioning would always be about the same number of steps. But bridging is especially quick when a number is close to a friendly number (a multiple of 10 or 100).

Task 2: Conjecturing (Always/Sometimes/Never)
"Bridging is always quicker than partitioning." Always, sometimes, or never?
Back up your answer with examples.
Model response

Answer: SOMETIMES. Bridging is great when one number is near a friendly number (like 99, 198, 302).

But for something like 45 + 32, neither is close to a friendly number. Partitioning gives 40 + 30 = 70, 5 + 2 = 7, total 77. Bridging: 45 + 5 = 50, 50 + 27 = 77. Both take about the same effort.

And for something like 350 + 240 where both numbers are already friendly, partitioning is instant: 300 + 200 = 500, 50 + 40 = 90, total 590.

After Task 1 I was ready to say "always", but then I tried some numbers where neither was near a friendly number, and I realised I had missed something important.

Task 3: Justifying (Reverse question/constraint)
The answer to an addition is 100. One of the two numbers is better solved by bridging, the other is better solved by partitioning. Find three different pairs of numbers that add to 100 where you can explain why one number suits bridging and the other suits partitioning.
For each pair, explain which number you would bridge from and why.
Model response

Pair 1: 99 + 1. Bridge from 99 (it is 1 away from 100).

Pair 2: 38 + 62. Bridge from 38 to 40, then add 60. The 38 suits bridging (close to 40) while 62 is already a neat number.

Pair 3: 47 + 53. Bridge from 47 to 50, add 50. Each time, the number close to a multiple of 10 suits bridging.

Mathematician's note: I first tried 99 + 1 and 2 + 98, but both numbers in those pairs are close to friendly numbers. The clearest examples are when one number is clearly near a multiple of 10 (suits bridging) and the other is not (neither strategy is obviously better, so either works). That is what makes a good answer to this constraint problem.
