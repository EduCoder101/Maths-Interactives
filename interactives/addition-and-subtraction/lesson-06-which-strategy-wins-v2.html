Learning Intention and Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning to analyse an equation and choose the most efficient tool to solve it: our brain, our pen, or a calculator.

Success Criteria

  • I can scan an equation for friendly numbers or patterns that suggest a mental strategy
  • I can identify when a written algorithm or calculator is the better choice
  • I can explain why a calculator might be slower than a mental strategy for certain equations

T3 · Q5: Always / Sometimes / Never
Open

"A calculator is always the safest choice because it never makes mistakes."

Is this Always, Sometimes, or Never true? Justify your answer.

ModelSometimes true. A calculator doesn't make arithmetic errors, but the person pressing buttons can. If you mis-key a number, the calculator gives a wrong answer confidently. For simple equations with friendly numbers, a mental strategy is both faster and just as reliable.

Equation 1: 4,002 - 3,998

Mathematician's Thinking

Mental strategy: I notice the numbers are very close. 3,998 is 2 away from 4,000, and 4,002 is 2 away on the other side. So the gap is just 4. Instant!

Written algorithm: I'd need to borrow all the way from the thousands through three zero columns. This takes multiple steps and is error-prone.

Winner: Mental strategy by miles.

Equation 2: 3,456 + 2,789

Mathematician's Thinking

Mental strategy: Large numbers with no friendly shortcut. Gets complicated fast.

Written algorithm: Line them up and follow the process step by step. Clean and structured. Answer: 6,245.

Winner: Written algorithm.

Now reflect:

Which calculations did the written algorithm win for you? Which did mental strategies win? What made the difference?

Task 2: Conjecturing
Test a claim about strategy reliability.
A student says: "A written algorithm is always the safest strategy because it works for every calculation."

Is this ALWAYS, SOMETIMES, or NEVER true? Explain your reasoning.

Mathematician's Thinking

Verdict: SOMETIMES true. The algorithm works mechanically, but it's not always the SAFEST choice. For numbers with zeros or numbers very close together, mental strategies can be faster AND less error-prone.

The insight: "Works" and "safest" are not the same thing.
