Learning Intention & Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning to use the constant difference strategy to make subtraction easier, especially when numbers contain zeros.

Success Criteria

  • I can shift both numbers in a subtraction by the same amount to remove zeros
  • I can explain that the difference between two numbers stays the same when both are shifted equally
  • I can use constant difference to avoid messy trading across zeros

The Big Idea (Reveal After Struggle)

The Promise

What if I told you we never have to trade across zeros again?

The Core Concept

Subtraction is just finding the distance between two numbers. If you move both numbers by the same amount, the distance stays exactly the same.

This is Constant Difference, and it eliminates every single zero in one strategic move.

Step 2: The Big Idea

Constant Difference

Subtraction is really just finding the distance between two numbers on a number line. If you move both numbers by the same amount, the distance between them stays exactly the same.

This is the secret to avoiding all that trading! We can shift both numbers by any amount we choose, as long as we shift them both by the same amount.

Step 5: Worked Example

Let us solve the original problem step by step.

10,000 − 4,876
Step 1: Spot the problem

10,000 is full of zeros. If we use the standard algorithm, we will need to trade across every single column. That is where errors happen.

Step 2: Shift both numbers down by 1

10,000 − 1 = 9,999
4,876 − 1 = 4,875

Step 3: Solve the new equation (no trading needed!)

Now we solve 9,999 − 4,875. Look how clean this is:

9 , 9 9 9
− 4 , 8 7 5
5 , 1 2 4

No trading, no crossing out, no confusion!

The Answer

10,000 − 4,876 = 5,124, and we got there cleanly and efficiently.

Card 9 of 19

Step 6: Key Points

  • Constant Difference works because subtraction is finding distance between numbers
  • When you shift both numbers by the same amount, the distance never changes
  • Use this strategy when the top number has zeros (1,000, 5,000, 10,000, etc.)
  • Choose a shift that removes the zeros, making the problem easier

Card 16 of 19

We Do: Discussion Questions

Show discussion questions and answers ▼

Question 1: Why does constant difference work?

Answer: Subtraction measures the distance between two numbers. If you move both numbers the same distance on a number line, the gap stays the same.

Question 2: What if I shift by 10 instead of 1?

Answer: It still works! Constant Difference works with any shift amount. Choose a shift that makes the problem easier (removing zeros).

Question 3: Is this method faster than the standard algorithm?

Answer: Yes! When the top number has zeros, constant difference eliminates the need for messy trading. The problem becomes much simpler.

Question 4: When should I use this strategy instead of the standard algorithm?

Answer: Use constant difference when you see zeros in your numbers (10,000, 5,000, $100.00, etc.). For other numbers without zeros, the standard algorithm works fine.

Card 18 of 19

When is the standard algorithm the worst choice?

A student says: I always use the standard algorithm because I was taught it and it always works.

When would you tell them not to use it?

The Answer

The standard algorithm becomes the worst choice when the top number has lots of zeros (like 10,000, 5,000, or $100.00). Every zero forces a trading cascade with multiple opportunities for error. Constant Difference eliminates this completely by shifting both numbers to remove the zeros.

The General Rule

An efficient mathematician chooses their strategy based on the numbers. The standard algorithm is powerful and has its place, but it is not always the most efficient tool. Recognising when to switch is a mark of genuine mathematical thinking.

Today's Strategy, Quick Reference

Constant Difference
  1. Subtract 1 from both numbers (or any constant amount)
  2. Repeat until zeros are gone
  3. Solve with standard algorithm, no trading needed

Best for: Numbers with trailing zeros (1,000, 5,000, 10,000, $50.00, 100.0, etc.)
