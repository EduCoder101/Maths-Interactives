Mental Calculation Strategies

Lesson 5B: Addition for Subtraction

Reframe subtraction as counting up, eliminate borrowing entirely

⬆️

Learning Intention & Success Criteria ▼

Learning Intention

We are learning to use addition for subtraction by counting up from the smaller number to the larger number to find the difference.

Success Criteria

  • I can count up from the smaller number to the larger number using an open number line
  • I can break the counting-up jumps into efficient steps (e.g. jumping to the nearest whole number first)
  • I can add my jumps together to find the total difference
Phase 1 · The Hook, 10 mins
Making Change: The Natural Way to Subtract

🎯 Real-World Scenario

The Scenario: You buy something for $4.60 and hand the cashier a $20 note. How much change should you get?

How would you work this out in your head?

📋 What Happens (Teacher Notes) ▼

Natural approach, counting up:

  • "Count: $4.60 to $5.00 is 40 cents"
  • "Then $5.00 to $20.00 is $15"
  • "So it's $15.40 altogether"
💡 The Key Insight

This natural approach is a legitimate mathematical technique! This is Addition for Subtraction, and it's incredibly powerful for mental math.

💡 The Big Idea (Reveal After Discussion) ▼
✨ The Core Concept

Subtraction is just finding the difference. Instead of taking away, you can count up from the smaller number to the larger number. The total distance you traveled is the answer.

Instead of subtracting, we add up from 4.60 to 20.00

Phase 2 · Explicit Instruction (I Do), 15 mins
Addition for Subtraction: The Strategy
1
The Problem

Let's work through the Hook problem together:

$20.00 − $4.60 = ?

Instead of counting backwards with subtraction, what if we thought about it differently? What if we counted UP from $4.60 to $20.00?

2
Identify the Numbers

The first step is always to recognise where we start and where we finish.

Where We Start

$4.60

This is the smaller number, the amount you spent

Where We End

$20.00

This is the larger number, the note you gave

Our job: Count up from $4.60 to $20.00 and keep track of how much we add.

3
Make Smart Jumps

Instead of counting on one by one, we make "smart jumps" to friendly numbers. Friendly numbers are easy to work with:

  • Whole dollar amounts: $5, $10, $20, $50
  • Whole number decimals: 5.0, 10.0
  • Multiples of 5 or 10

Why? Jumping to round numbers makes the mental maths much easier. Instead of adding 0.40 and 15.00 separately, we use numbers our brains find simple.

4
Worked Example: $20.00 − $4.60

Now let's apply the strategy. Here are the jumps we make:

Start:
$4.60
Jump 1:
+$0.40 → get to $5.00
Jump 2:
+$15.00 → get to $20.00
Total:
$0.40 + $15.00 = $15.40
✅ The Answer

$15.40

No subtraction. No borrowing. Just pure addition!

5
Another Example & Key Points

Let's see the strategy on a number line with a decimal problem:

13.2 − 4.6 = ?

13.2 − 4.6 = 8.6 ✓

🔑 Key Teaching Points

  • Why it works: Addition and subtraction are inverse operations, the gap is the same whether you subtract down or count up
  • When to use it: Decimals, money, or whenever mental calculation is needed
  • Smart jumps: Always jump to friendly numbers (5, 10, 50, 100, whole numbers)
  • No borrowing: This completely eliminates regrouping and trading, it's pure addition!

You have mastered the addition-for-subtraction strategy. Now it is time to investigate when and why it works best. These tasks ask you to explore, make conjectures, and reason like a mathematician.

Task 1 · Specialising
Solve each calculation TWO ways
Try both: subtracting directly AND adding on from the smaller number. Notice which method was easier for each one.

Example calculations:

  • 1,000 − 637
  • 845 − 399
  • 752 − 348
A mathematician's thinking:

Let me try 1,000 − 637 both ways.

Subtracting directly: 1,000 − 637 = 363. This feels a bit annoying because I have to borrow from all those zeros!

Adding on: I'll jump from 637 up to 1,000.
637 + 3 = 640
640 + 60 = 700
700 + 300 = 1,000
Total added: 3 + 60 + 300 = 363. Much easier! No borrowing needed.

For 845 − 399, adding on is great because 399 is close to 400:
399 + 1 = 400, then 400 + 445 = 845. Answer: 1 + 445 = 446.

For 752 − 348, again the subtrahend is close to 350:
348 + 2 = 350, then 350 + 402 = 752. Answer: 2 + 402 = 404.

Pattern I noticed: Adding on tends to need fewer steps when the smaller number is close to a round number. The method you choose really does depend on the numbers!
Task 2 · Conjecturing
Is this always, sometimes, or never true?
When you use addition for subtraction, you always need fewer steps than when you subtract directly.
A mathematician's thinking:

Hmm, let me test this claim. Is it true that adding on always needs fewer steps?

I already saw that 1,000 − 637 takes 3 steps of adding, but the direct subtraction is messier with all the borrowing. So far, adding on seems better for that one.

But wait... let me try 750 − 250:

Subtracting directly: 750 − 250 = 500. Done instantly!

Adding on: 250 + 500 = 750. Also just one step.

So for this one, both methods need the same number of steps. That means the answer is SOMETIMES, not always. It really depends on which numbers you're working with. Adding on tends to need fewer steps when the smaller number is close to a round number, but not always.

The real wisdom: Different strategies work better for different numbers. Being flexible is more powerful than always using the same method!
Task 3 · Justifying
Explain why this works, not just that it works
A student solved 1,000 − 637 by adding on: 637 + 3 = 640, then 640 + 60 = 700, then 700 + 300 = 1,000. Total added: 3 + 60 + 300 = 363. Why does adding these jumps give the same answer as subtracting?
A mathematician's thinking:

Why does adding the jumps (3 + 60 + 300 = 363) give the same answer as 1,000 − 637?

Think about what subtraction actually means. When I ask "what is 1,000 − 637?", I'm really asking: "What is the gap between 637 and 1,000?"

Now, when I add on from 637 to 1,000, I'm measuring that exact same gap by building it up in pieces. My three jumps (3, 60, and 300) together make up the entire distance from 637 to 1,000.

Whether I measure the gap by going down from the top (1,000) or building up from the bottom (637), I get the same distance. The gap is the gap, no matter which direction I measure it from!

The big idea: Subtraction measures the distance (or gap) between two numbers. Adding on from the smaller number measures that exact same distance. They're different paths to the same answer.
Phase 5 · Plenary (Wrap Up), 5 mins
When is Addition for Subtraction most powerful?

💬 Discussion

We've learned that subtraction can be reframed as addition. Let's think about when this strategy shines:

✅ Best Use Cases

Addition for Subtraction is most powerful for:

  • Mental math, no paper needed, easy to track jumps in your head
  • Money calculations, making change, budgeting, shopping
  • Decimal subtraction, eliminates complex borrowing across decimal points
  • Real-world contexts, finding differences in measurements, times, distances
🎯 The Key Advantage

This strategy completely eliminates borrowing and regrouping. You never cross out numbers, never carry, never trade. It's pure, simple addition, which our brains find much easier than subtraction.

📐 Mathematical Thinking

Recognizing that subtraction and addition are inverse operations, and that either can be used to find a difference, is sophisticated mathematical understanding. The efficient mathematician chooses the path that's clearest and simplest for the numbers at hand.

📋 Today's Strategy, Quick Reference

Addition for Subtraction
  1. Start at the smaller number
  2. Jump to friendly numbers (whole dollars, round numbers, multiples of 5 or 10)
  3. Add all the jumps together

Best for: Mental math, money, decimals, finding differences

Advantage: No borrowing, no regrouping, just addition!
