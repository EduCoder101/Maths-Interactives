Levelling (Compensation)

Adjusting numbers to create friendly calculations, without changing the answer.

Learning Intention & Success Criteria ▼

Learning Intention

We are learning to adjust numbers to make them easier to calculate in our heads, using a strategy called levelling or compensation.

Success Criteria

  • I know that levelling and compensation mean the same thing
  • I can take an amount from one number and give it to another to make a friendly number (like a multiple of 10 or 100)
  • I can use this strategy to solve addition and subtraction problems without a written algorithm

🔑 The Big Idea

You can move an amount from one number to another without changing the total. If you move the right amount, you create a friendly number, a multiple of 10, 100, or 1,000, that makes mental calculation trivial.

This strategy has two names: Levelling and Compensation. They mean exactly the same thing.

Compensation

The twin word, same strategy, different name

Exactly the same strategy as levelling. If a test says "compensate", it means "level the numbers". These are twin words.

If a test question asks you to use "compensation" to solve a problem, it wants you to level the numbers. Don't be tricked by the different name, it's the same thing.

Concept 3 of 3
Friendly Number

The target, what you're trying to create

A number that is easy to work with, usually a multiple of 10, 100, or 1,000. The goal of levelling is to create one.

These are all friendly numbers:

10 50 100 200 1,000 5,000 6,000

The point of levelling is to turn one of the numbers into one of these, making the rest of the calculation trivial.

💡 Remember

You're scanning for numbers that are close to a friendly number, 197 is 3 away from 200, 4,997 is 3 away from 5,000, $9.98 is $0.02 away from $10.00.

Concept 1 of 3
The Rules
How the adjustment works
⚖️ Levelling Rule, Addition
➕ Addition Add to one number, subtract the same amount from the other.
The total stays the same.
📘 What about subtraction?

Subtraction uses a related but different method called Constant Difference, covered in its own lesson. In this lesson we focus on addition only.

🔍 The clue to look for

Scan for numbers that are close to a round number. If you see 197, 398, 5,983, 4.9, or $9.98, that's your signal to level.

These tasks go beyond practising the levelling strategy. You will investigate why it works and when it is most useful.

Task 1, Specialising
Try using the levelling strategy on these three calculations. For which one did levelling help the most? For which one was it least useful? Why?
(a) 199 + 356
(b) 432 + 251
(c) 398 + 407
What makes a calculation "good" for levelling? Can you describe the kind of numbers where levelling really helps?
Model Response

For (a) 199 + 356, I levelled to 200 + 355 = 555. That made it really easy because 199 is so close to 200.

For (b) 432 + 251, I could level to 430 + 253 or 440 + 243... but none of these feel much easier than the original. Levelling didn't help much here because neither number is close to a friendly number. The digits in the tens and units places aren't "nudging" either number towards a round number.

For (c) 398 + 407, I levelled to 400 + 405 = 805. That was helpful because 398 is very close to 400.

Key insight: I initially thought levelling always helps, but now I see it is most powerful when at least one number is close to a multiple of 10 or 100. If both numbers are already in the middle of a range (like 432), there is no obvious "friendly" number to level towards. Levelling shines when you spot a number that is just a small jump away from something round.
Task 2, Conjecturing
"The levelling strategy works for subtraction in exactly the same way as it works for addition." Is this always true, sometimes true, or never true?
Model Response

First, my initial thought: Sometimes. I thought addition and subtraction might follow different rules, so maybe levelling works differently...

But then I worked it out properly: It is always true that levelling works for both operations. However, the rule for how you adjust is different.

For addition: You adjust in opposite directions. If you add to one number, you subtract from the other. For example: (199 + 1) + (356 - 1) = 200 + 355 = 555, which equals the original 199 + 356. The total stays the same.

For subtraction: You adjust in the same direction. You add (or subtract) the same amount to both numbers. For example: (305 + 2) - (198 + 2) = 307 - 200 = 107, which equals the original 305 - 198. The difference stays the same.

Why is this? Mathematically, for addition: (a + n) + (b − n) = a + b. For subtraction: (a + n) − (b + n) = a − b. When you shift both numbers by the same amount in subtraction, the gap between them does not change. Think of it on a number line: if you slide both numbers 2 to the right, the distance between them stays the same.
Task 3, Justifying
A student uses levelling on 305 − 198. They say: "I will add 2 to 198 to make it 200, and subtract 2 from 305 to make it 303. So the answer is 303 − 200 = 103."

Is their answer correct? Is their method correct? Explain what went wrong.

Model Response

The answer is incorrect. 305 − 198 = 107, not 103. So the student made a significant mistake.

What went wrong: The student used the addition rule (adjust in opposite directions) instead of the subtraction rule (adjust in the same direction). For subtraction, you need to add 2 to both numbers, not just one.

The correct method: (305 + 2) − (198 + 2) = 307 − 200 = 107. By adding 2 to both numbers, the difference between them stays the same.

The confusion: The student adjusted 305 down by 2 and 198 up by 2, which makes the gap smaller! If one number goes up and the other goes down, the difference shrinks. For subtraction levelling to work, both numbers must move in the same direction. This is the opposite of addition, where you move them in opposite directions.
