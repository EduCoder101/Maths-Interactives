Learning Intention and Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning to explore how the base-ten system works, where each place value is ten times the previous place.

Success Criteria

  • I can explain that each place value column is ten times bigger than the one to its right.
  • I can explain that each place value column is ten times smaller than the one to its left.
  • I can multiply a number by 10, 100, or 1,000 by shifting digits to the left.
  • I can divide a number by 10, 100, or 1,000 by shifting digits to the right.
Card 1 of 10

The Special Number

Look at this number. What do you notice?

1,111,111

Can you write down in words the value of this number?

One million, one hundred and eleven thousand, one hundred and eleven.

Key Observation

Every column has the same digit (1), but every column has a DIFFERENT value. The same digit 1 is worth 1, or 10, or 100, or 1,000, or 10,000, or 100,000, or 1,000,000.

What makes each 1 different? Its POSITION.

Card 2 of 10

The Big Idea

In our number system, every time you move one place to the LEFT, the value gets TEN TIMES BIGGER.

Every time you move one place to the RIGHT, the value gets TEN TIMES SMALLER.

Card 4 of 10

Moving Right (÷10)

Now let's use 7,000 and divide by powers of 10.

Step 17,000 means 7 thousands
Step 2Divide by 10: 7,000 ÷ 10 = 700

The 7 moves from thousands to hundreds. Every digit shifts one place to the RIGHT.

Step 3Divide by 10 again: 700 ÷ 10 = 70

The 7 moves from hundreds to tens.

Step 4And again: 70 ÷ 10 = 7

The 7 moves from tens to ones.

Step 5One more: 7 ÷ 10 = 0.7

The 7 moves from ones to tenths (a decimal place).

Misconception

Students often say "take away a zero" when dividing by 10. But the digit is MOVING to the right, not losing a zero.

This matters especially with decimals: 70 ÷ 10 = 7, but 7 ÷ 10 = 0.7. There is no zero to "take away", so the digit must move into the decimal place.

Go Deeper: Powers of Ten Investigations

These tasks will push your thinking beyond finding answers. You will explore, make conjectures, and reason like a mathematician.

Specialising
Start with the number 5. Multiply by 10 five times in a row, writing each result. Then start with your final answer and divide by 10 five times.
What do you notice? Does the journey up always perfectly undo the journey down?
A mathematician's response

I started with 5 and multiplied by 10 five times:

5 × 10 = 50

50 × 10 = 500

500 × 10 = 5,000

5,000 × 10 = 50,000

50,000 × 10 = 500,000

My final answer was 500,000. Now I divided by 10 five times:

500,000 ÷ 10 = 50,000

50,000 ÷ 10 = 5,000

5,000 ÷ 10 = 500

500 ÷ 10 = 50

50 ÷ 10 = 5

I landed back on 5. The journey down perfectly undid the journey up!

This works because multiplying by 10 and dividing by 10 are inverse operations. Each multiplication and division by 10 shifts a digit one place. Five multiplications move it 5 places left. Five divisions move it 5 places right. Perfect balance.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. You tried a specific example and looked for a pattern in the results.
Conjecturing
A student says: "Multiplying by 100 is the same as multiplying by 10 twice."
Is this always true, sometimes true, or never true? Test with at least 5 different numbers before you decide.
A mathematician's response

I decided to test this conjecture with several numbers. Let me start by thinking about why this might be true. 100 = 10 × 10, so multiplying by 100 should be the same as multiplying by 10 twice.

Test 1: 5

5 × 100 = 500

5 × 10 = 50, then 50 × 10 = 500

Same result. The statement is TRUE for 5.

Test 2: 23

23 × 100 = 2,300

23 × 10 = 230, then 230 × 10 = 2,300

Same result. TRUE for 23.

Test 3: 7

7 × 100 = 700

7 × 10 = 70, then 70 × 10 = 700

Same result. TRUE for 7.

Test 4: 164

164 × 100 = 16,400

164 × 10 = 1,640, then 1,640 × 10 = 16,400

Same result. TRUE for 164.

Test 5: 99

99 × 100 = 9,900

99 × 10 = 990, then 990 × 10 = 9,900

Same result. TRUE for 99.

My conclusion: ALWAYS TRUE. Because 100 = 10 × 10, multiplying by 100 is mathematically the same as multiplying by 10 twice, no matter which number you start with.

Mathematician's thinking: You are conjecturing. You made a claim based on evidence from multiple tests. The evidence strongly suggests the claim is always true.
Generalising
Write a rule that connects multiplying by 1,000 to multiplying by 10. Can you extend your rule to multiplying by 10,000 or 100,000?
Look for a pattern in the number of zeros.
A mathematician's response

I noticed something about how many times I need to multiply by 10.

When I multiply by 10, I multiply by 10 once. The number has one zero.

When I multiply by 100, I multiply by 10 twice. The number has two zeros.

When I multiply by 1,000, I should multiply by 10 three times. The number has three zeros.

My rule: Multiplying by 1,000 is the same as multiplying by 10 three times.

I can extend this pattern:

Multiplying by 10,000 means multiplying by 10 four times (four zeros).

Multiplying by 100,000 means multiplying by 10 five times (five zeros).

General rule: The number of zeros tells you how many times to multiply by 10. 10^n means multiply by 10, n times.

Mathematician's thinking: You are generalising. You found a pattern and wrote a rule that works for many cases, not just one example.
