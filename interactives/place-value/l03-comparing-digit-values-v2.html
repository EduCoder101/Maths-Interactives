Learning Intention and Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning to compare the value of the same digit in different positions within a number.

Success Criteria

  • I can identify the value of a digit based on its position in a number.
  • I can compare the value of the same digit in two different positions.
  • I can calculate how many times bigger or smaller one digit's value is compared to another.
  • I can explain the relationship between two positions using powers of ten.

The Question That Matters

Big Idea

If the same digit can have different values, how do we work out exactly HOW MUCH bigger or smaller one value is compared to another? That's what this lesson is about.

Card 3 of 10

Comparing Within 5,500

Let's work out how many times bigger the thousands place is compared to the hundreds place.

Step 1The 5 in the thousands place is worth 5,000.
Step 2The 5 in the hundreds place is worth 500.
Step 3How many times bigger is 5,000 than 500? We divide: 5,000 ÷ 500 = 10.
Step 4So the 5 in the thousands place is 10 times bigger. That makes sense: thousands is ONE column to the left of hundreds, and each column is 10 times bigger.
Pattern

1 column apart = 10 times. 2 columns apart = 100 times. 3 columns apart = 1,000 times. Each extra column multiplies by another 10.

Card 4 of 10

Working the Other Way

What if we compare the smaller value to the bigger one? Let's use 3,033.

Step 1The 3 in the ones place is worth 3.
Step 2The 3 in the thousands place is worth 3,000.
Step 3How many times SMALLER is 3 compared to 3,000? We can say: 3,000 is 1,000 times bigger than 3.
Step 4Or we say: the 3 in the ones place is 1,000 times SMALLER than the 3 in the thousands place.
Key Method

You can always work it out by dividing the bigger value by the smaller value. The answer tells you the factor: 10, 100, 1,000, etc.

Go Deeper: Comparing Digit Values Investigations

These tasks will push your thinking beyond finding answers. You will explore, make conjectures, and reason like a mathematician.

Specialising
Pick any digit from 1-9. Write a number where that digit appears in the ones, tens, hundreds, and thousands places. Work out the value of the digit in each position. What pattern do you see in the values?
Try this with at least 2 different digits. Is the pattern always the same?
A mathematician's response

I chose the digit 3. I wrote the number 3,333 where the digit 3 appears in all four positions.

The value in the thousands place: 3,000. In the hundreds place: 300. In the tens place: 30. In the ones place: 3.

I notice that each value is exactly 10 times the one to its right. 3,000 = 10 × 300. 300 = 10 × 30. 30 = 10 × 3.

I tried with digit 7 (writing 7,777) and the same pattern held: 7,000, 700, 70, 7 each 10 times the previous.

The pattern is always the same because place value itself is built on powers of 10. Whatever digit you choose, moving left multiplies by 10.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. That means you are trying specific examples to gather evidence, looking for a pattern before you make any claims.
Conjecturing
"Moving a digit one place to the left always makes its value exactly 10 times bigger."
Is this always true, sometimes true, or never true? Test it with at least 4 different digits in different positions.
A mathematician's response

Let me test this systematically. I'll start with different digits in the ones place, then move them one place left to the tens place.

Test 1: 4 in ones (worth 4) versus 4 in tens (worth 40). Is 40 ÷ 4 = 10? Yes, always true here.

Test 2: 9 in tens (worth 90) versus 9 in hundreds (worth 900). Is 900 ÷ 90 = 10? Yes.

Test 3: 2 in hundreds (worth 200) versus 2 in thousands (worth 2,000). Is 2,000 ÷ 200 = 10? Yes.

Test 4: 7 in tens (worth 70) versus 7 in hundreds (worth 700). Is 700 ÷ 70 = 10? Yes.

My conjecture: ALWAYS TRUE. Each column is defined as exactly 10 times the column to its right. This is the foundation of our base-ten number system.

Mathematician's thinking: You are conjecturing. That means you are making a claim that you believe is true, based on your evidence, but you know it still needs to be proved.
Justifying
A student says: "In 3,300, the first 3 is worth more because it comes first in the number." Is the student's reasoning correct? What is the real reason the first 3 is worth more?
Can you find a number where a digit that comes first is actually worth LESS than the same digit later in the number?
A mathematician's response

First, I need to check if the student's conclusion is correct. In 3,300, the first 3 is in the thousands place (worth 3,000) and the second 3 is in the hundreds place (worth 300). Yes, 3,000 is worth more than 300. The conclusion is correct.

But the student said the reason is "because it comes first in the number." Is that the right reason? Let me think about different digits. Look at 1,900. The 1 comes first, but the 9 (which comes second) is worth much more (900 versus 1,000... wait, that's not right. 1 is in thousands worth 1,000, and 9 is in hundreds worth 900). The 1 IS worth more even though it comes first.

Let me look at 0.33 (decimal). The first 3 is in the tenths place (worth 0.3) and the second 3 is in the hundredths place (worth 0.03). Here, the digit that comes FIRST is worth MORE, but for the same reason: position.

For same-digit numbers in standard whole numbers, the student's pattern happens to be true: the digit on the left is always in a higher place value. But the REASON is not "it comes first." The real reason is POSITION and PLACE VALUE. Left is higher place value, so left is worth more. A right answer with a wrong reason is still a problem in mathematics.

Mathematician's thinking: You are justifying. That means you are not just checking whether an answer is right, you are examining whether the reason given is correct. A right answer with a wrong reason is still a problem in mathematics.
