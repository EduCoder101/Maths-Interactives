Learning Intention and Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning to round numbers to a specified place value.

Success Criteria

  • I can identify the two multiples a number sits between when rounding.
  • I can find the halfway point and use it to decide whether to round up or down.
  • I can round numbers to the nearest ten, hundred, thousand, or ten-thousand.
  • I can explain why rounding is useful for estimation.

Most people don't add $6.95 + $2.15 exactly. They round: $7 + $2 = $9. That leaves about $3. Rounding makes numbers friendly.

How Do We Decide: Up or Down?

First example: $6.95

$7
$6.50
$6

$6.95 is above the halfway mark ($6.50), so it rounds UP to $7.

Second example: $2.15

$3
$2.50
$2

$2.15 is below the halfway mark ($2.50), so it rounds DOWN to $2.

The Rule

At the halfway point or above, round up. Below the halfway point, round down.

Card 3 of 10

Round 10,342 to the Nearest Thousand

Step 1Find the two thousands: 10,342 sits between 10,000 and 11,000.
Step 2Find the halfway point: 10,500.
Step 3Is 10,342 above or below 10,500? Below.
Step 4So 10,342 rounds DOWN to 10,000.
Step 5Quick check: look at the hundreds digit (3). Since 3 < 5, round down. Confirmed!
Shortcut

Just look at the digit immediately to the right of the rounding place. If it's 0, 1, 2, 3, or 4, round down. If it's 5, 6, 7, 8, or 9, round up.

Card 4 of 10

Round 467,800 to the Nearest Ten-Thousand

Step 1The rounding place is ten-thousands. The digit there is 6 (in 467,800).
Step 2The two ten-thousands it sits between: 460,000 and 470,000.
Step 3Look at the digit to the right of ten-thousands: that's 7 (the thousands digit).
Step 47 >= 5, so round UP.
Step 5467,800 rounds to 470,000.
Key Idea

When rounding, look at the digit one place to the right. That digit is the "decider".

Go Deeper: Rounding Investigations

These tasks will push your thinking beyond finding answers. You will explore, make conjectures, and reason like a mathematician.

Specialising
Find three different numbers that all round to 7,000 when rounded to the nearest thousand. What is the smallest possible number that rounds to 7,000? What is the largest?
Think carefully about the boundaries. What number is exactly halfway?
A mathematician's response

I need numbers where the thousands digit rounds to 7. That means they must be between 6,500 and 7,499 (inclusive).

Let me try: 6,500 rounds up to 7,000. 7,000 stays at 7,000. 7,499 rounds down to 7,000. What about 7,500? That rounds UP to 8,000.

So the range is 6,500 to 7,499. Three examples: 6,723, 7,000, 7,321. The smallest is 6,500 and the largest is 7,499. I first thought the largest might be 7,500, but 7,500 rounds to 8,000, not 7,000.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. That means you are trying specific examples to gather evidence, looking for a pattern before you make any claims.
Conjecturing
"When you round a number twice (e.g. first to the nearest ten, then to the nearest hundred), you always get the same result as rounding directly to the nearest hundred." Is this always true, sometimes true, or never true?
Try it with numbers like 449, 450, 451, and 350.
A mathematician's response

Let me test this systematically with the numbers suggested.

Test 1: 449. Round to nearest ten: 450. Round 450 to nearest hundred: 500. Round 449 directly to nearest hundred: 400 (since 4 < 5). DIFFERENT!

Test 2: 350. Round to nearest ten: 350 (already exact). Round to nearest hundred: 400. Directly: 400. Same.

Test 3: 451. Round to nearest ten: 450. Round to nearest hundred: 500. Directly: 500 (5 >= 5). Same.

Test 4: 245. Round to nearest ten: 250. Round to nearest hundred: 300. Directly: 200 (4 < 5). DIFFERENT again!

So this is SOMETIMES TRUE. Double rounding can give a different answer when the first rounding pushes a digit from below 5 to exactly 5.

Mathematician's thinking: You are conjecturing. That means you are making a claim that you believe is true, based on your evidence, but you know it still needs to be proven.
Generalising
How many whole numbers round to 500 when rounded to the nearest hundred? How many round to 5,000 when rounded to the nearest thousand? Can you write a general rule?
Count carefully. Don't forget to check both boundaries.
A mathematician's response

For 500 (nearest hundred): the range is 450 to 549. Let me count: 549 - 450 + 1 = 100 numbers.

For 5,000 (nearest thousand): the range is 4,500 to 5,499. That's 5,499 - 4,500 + 1 = 1,000 numbers.

I see a pattern! For rounding to nearest hundred: 100 numbers map to each result. For rounding to nearest thousand: 1,000 numbers. My rule: when rounding to the nearest [power of ten], exactly [that power of ten] different whole numbers will round to any given result.

Rounding to nearest 10 results in 10 numbers each. Rounding to nearest 100 results in 100 numbers each. Rounding to nearest 1,000 results in 1,000 numbers each.

Note: The number 0 is a special case (only 50 numbers round to 0), but for all other rounding targets in the middle, the rule holds perfectly.

Mathematician's thinking: You are generalising. That means you are looking at several specific examples to discover a pattern or rule that works beyond those examples.
