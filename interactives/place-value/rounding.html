Learning Intention & Success Criteria ▼

Learning Intention

We are learning to round numbers to a given place value using the midpoint rule.

Success Criteria

  • I can identify the midpoint between two benchmark numbers
  • I can determine whether to round up or round down based on the midpoint
  • I can round numbers to the nearest ten, hundred, thousand, or beyond

What is Rounding?

Rounding is a way to simplify numbers by replacing them with nearby benchmark numbers that are easier to work with. We use rounding when we need a quick estimate rather than an exact answer.

Example: If you have $24.75 and want to know roughly how much that is, you could round to $25, it's close enough for quick thinking!

The Midpoint Rule

  • If a number is below the midpoint, round down to the lower benchmark
  • If a number is at or above the midpoint, round up to the higher benchmark
  • Example: Rounding 24 to tens → midpoint is 25, so 24 rounds down to 20
  • Example: Rounding 57 to tens → midpoint is 55, so 57 rounds up to 60

Worked Example

Let's round a number step by step.

Round 367 to the nearest hundred

Step 1: Find the two benchmarks (the hundreds on either side):
300 ←── 367 ──→ 400
Step 2: Find the midpoint between the benchmarks:
Midpoint = 350
Step 3: Is 367 below or at/above the midpoint?
367 is above 350, so we round up.
Answer: 367 rounded to the nearest hundred is 400

Key Vocabulary ▼

Benchmark numbers are the "friendly" numbers you round to, like 10, 20, 30 or 100, 200, 300.

Midpoint is the number exactly halfway between two benchmarks. For example, the midpoint between 20 and 30 is 25.

Go Deeper: Rounding Investigations

You've mastered the skills. Now think like a mathematician. These tasks are not about right or wrong answers. They are about exploring, reasoning, and convincing others.

Conjecturing
Read this statement carefully:
"When you round a number, the answer is always smaller than the original number."

Is this always true, sometimes true, or never true?
Find examples to support your decision. Can you find a case where it breaks?
A mathematician's response

This is sometimes true.

When I round 13 to the nearest ten, I get 10. The rounded answer is smaller. But when I round 17 to the nearest ten, I get 20. The rounded answer is bigger!

And there is a special case: 20 rounded to the nearest ten stays as 20. The answer is the same, not smaller.

So rounding can make a number smaller, bigger, or keep it the same. It depends which benchmark the number is closer to.

Mathematician's thinking: You are conjecturing and justifying. That means making a claim and testing it with evidence. Mathematicians do not just guess. They try lots of examples, including tricky ones, before they decide.
Specialising
A number rounds to 350 when rounded to the nearest ten.
It also rounds to 300 when rounded to the nearest hundred.
How many whole numbers could it be? List them all. How do you know you have found every one?
A mathematician's response

First, which numbers round to 350 when rounded to the nearest ten? They must be from 345 to 354.

Now, which of those also round to 300 (nearest hundred)? To round to 300, a number must be less than 350. That means 345, 346, 347, 348, and 349.

That is 5 numbers. I know I have found them all because I worked systematically through every number between 345 and 354, and checked each one against both conditions.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. That means working through specific examples carefully, one by one, to spot a pattern. Being systematic (not just guessing randomly) is what helps you find every possibility.
Generalising
Pick any 3-digit number. Round it to the nearest 10. Now round it to the nearest 100.

Do this with at least 5 different numbers. What do you notice about how far apart the two rounded answers are?
Can you write a rule that describes what you found? Does your rule always work?
A mathematician's response

267 rounds to 270 (nearest 10) and 300 (nearest 100). Difference: 30.

143 rounds to 140 (nearest 10) and 100 (nearest 100). Difference: 40.

850 rounds to 850 (nearest 10) and 900 (nearest 100). Difference: 50.

The difference between rounding to the nearest 10 and rounding to the nearest 100 is always a multiple of 10, and it is never more than 50. This makes sense because rounding to the nearest 10 can only change a number by up to 5, and rounding to the nearest 100 can change it by up to 50. The gap between the two rounded answers depends on the tens digit.

Mathematician's thinking: You are generalising. That means looking across lots of examples to find a pattern that always works, then writing it as a rule. The best generalisations come from testing tricky cases, not just easy ones.
Justifying
Sam says: "I rounded 4,550 to the nearest thousand and got 4,000. Then I rounded 4,550 to the nearest hundred and got 4,600. So if I round 4,600 to the nearest thousand, I should get the same answer as before: 4,000."

Is Sam right? Explain why or why not.
What mistake is Sam making about how rounding works? Can you convince a friend using a clear explanation?
A mathematician's response

Sam is wrong. Let me work through each step.

4,550 rounded to the nearest thousand is 5,000 (not 4,000!). The midpoint between 4,000 and 5,000 is 4,500, and 4,550 is above it, so it rounds up.

4,550 rounded to the nearest hundred is 4,600. That is correct.

But 4,600 rounded to the nearest thousand is 5,000, not 4,000. Rounding in two steps can give a different result than rounding in one step because the first round changes the number, and the new number might be on the other side of a midpoint.

Mathematician's thinking: You are justifying. That means explaining why something is true or false, not just saying the answer. A strong justification walks through the reasoning step by step so that someone else could follow it.
