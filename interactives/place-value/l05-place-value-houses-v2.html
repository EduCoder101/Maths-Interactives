Learning Intention

We are learning to use place value houses to read and name numbers into the millions.

Success Criteria

  • I can explain that place values are grouped in threes (ones, tens, hundreds) inside each house.
  • I can name the ones house, the thousands house, and the millions house.
  • I can read a number up to millions using the house structure.
  • I can identify the value of any digit in a number up to millions.

Why Do We Group Numbers in Threes?

Can you read this number easily?

398401

Probably not. Now watch what happens when we group it.

398,401

That's three hundred and ninety-eight thousand, four hundred and one.

Key Idea

Every group of three digits follows the same pattern: hundreds, tens, ones. We call these groups "houses".

Thousands
H 4
T 3
O 8

H 3

Step 1Start with the millions house. There's just a 2 here, so we say "two million..."
Step 2Move to the thousands house. We read 438 just like a normal three-digit number: "four hundred and thirty-eight thousand..."
Step 3Finally the ones house. Again, read it normally: "three hundred and ninety-five."
Step 4Put it together: two million, four hundred and thirty-eight thousand, three hundred and ninety-five
Pattern

Inside each house, you just read a normal three-digit number. The house name goes at the end.

Thousands
H 0
T 3
O 0

H 0
T 7

Step 1Millions house: just 5. So "five million..."
Step 2Thousands house: 030. The 0 in hundreds and 0 in ones are skipped. We say "thirty thousand..." (not "zero hundred and thirty-zero thousand").
Step 3Ones house: 072. Skip the hundreds 0. We say "and seventy-two."
Step 4Put it together: five million, thirty thousand and seventy-two
Key Idea

Zeros are still important. They hold places inside the house, but we skip them when reading aloud.

Go Deeper

What can we discover about how houses help us understand really big numbers?

Task 1: Specialising
Write the number that has: 7 in the millions, 0 in the hundred-thousands, 4 in the ten-thousands, 0 in the thousands, 0 in the hundreds, 6 in the tens, and 3 in the ones. Now read it aloud. Which houses have zeros you need to skip?
Tip: Draw the houses and fill in the digits to help.
Mathematician's Response

I placed each digit: 7,040,063. Let me put that in houses. Millions: 7. Thousands: 0, 4, 0. Ones: 0, 6, 3. Reading: "seven million, forty thousand and sixty-three." In the thousands house I had to skip the hundreds (0) and the ones (0), reading just "forty thousand." In the ones house I skipped the hundreds (0). Three of the seven digits are zeros, but the number still reads fairly smoothly because of the house structure.

Mathematician's Box: Specialising.
Task 2: Conjecturing
"Every house in a number must have exactly three digits." Is this always true, sometimes true, or never true?
Tip: Think about the leftmost house. Does it always have three digits?
Mathematician's Response

Let me test. 2,438,395 has houses of 2 | 438 | 395. The millions house only has 1 digit. What about 45,200? Houses: 45 | 200. The thousands house has 2 digits. And 700? That's just 700 in the ones house, 3 digits. So the rightmost house always has 3 digits and so does the middle house when it exists. But the leftmost house can have 1, 2, or 3 digits. 2,438,395 has 1 in millions. 45,200 has 2 in thousands. 398,401 has 3 in thousands. So this is SOMETIMES TRUE. Every house except the leftmost must have exactly three digits (otherwise digits would be in the wrong place). But the leftmost house can have 1, 2, or 3.

Mathematician's Box: Conjecturing.
Task 3: Generalising
Can you write a rule connecting the number of digits in a whole number to how many houses it needs? Test your rule with numbers that have 1 digit, 3 digits, 5 digits, 7 digits, and 9 digits.
Tip: Is there a pattern you can describe using division?
Mathematician's Response

1 digit (e.g. 5): 1 house (ones). 3 digits (e.g. 842): 1 house (ones). 5 digits (e.g. 45,200): 2 houses (thousands + ones). 7 digits (e.g. 2,438,395): 3 houses (millions + thousands + ones). 9 digits (e.g. 123,456,789): 3 houses, all full.

I first thought it might be: houses = digits ÷ 3. But 5 ÷ 3 = 1.67, and we need 2 houses. Let me try rounding up. Ceiling of 1/3 = 1. Ceiling of 3/3 = 1. Ceiling of 5/3 = 2. Ceiling of 7/3 = 3. Ceiling of 9/3 = 3. That works!

My rule: The number of houses equals the number of digits divided by 3, rounded up. Or: you get a new house every time you pass a multiple of 3 digits. The ones house holds up to 3 digits, then you need the thousands house for digits 4–6, then the millions house for digits 7–9.
