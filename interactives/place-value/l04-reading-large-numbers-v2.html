Learning Intention and Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning to read and represent numbers up to tens of thousands.

Success Criteria

  • I can read a five-digit number using place value to say it correctly.
  • I can write a number in words when given the numeral.
  • I can write a number as a numeral when given the words.
  • I can identify and correct errors in how numbers are read.

How Do We Read This Number?

47,382
Step 1Start from the left. The 4 is in the ten-thousands place, so we begin with "forty..."
Step 2The 7 is in the thousands place. Together with the 4, we say "forty-seven thousand..."
Step 3Now the hundreds: the 3 means "three hundred..."
Step 4The 8 is in tens and the 2 is in ones: "eighty-two"
Step 5Put it together: forty-seven thousand, three hundred and eighty-two
Card 4 of 10

What Happens When There Are Zeros?

60,508
Step 1The 6 is in ten-thousands, the 0 is in thousands. We say "sixty thousand" (not "sixty-zero thousand").
Step 2The 5 is in hundreds: "five hundred..."
Step 3The 0 is in tens. We skip it. We don't say "zero tens".
Step 4The 8 is in ones: "and eight"
Step 5Put it together: sixty thousand, five hundred and eight
Key Idea

When a column has zero, we skip it when reading. Zero holds the place, but we don't say it.

Go Deeper: Reading Large Numbers Investigations

These tasks will push your thinking beyond finding answers. You will explore patterns, make conjectures, and reason like a mathematician.

Specialising
Write three different five-digit numbers that each have exactly two zeros. Read each number aloud. How many words does each number use?
Do numbers with zeros in different positions use the same number of words?
A mathematician's response

I tried 30,040. Read aloud: "thirty thousand and forty". That's 5 words.

Then 10,500. Read aloud: "ten thousand, five hundred". That's 4 words.

Then 20,007. Read aloud: "twenty thousand and seven". That's 4 words.

I also tried 50,600. Read aloud: "fifty thousand, six hundred". That's 4 words.

Interesting! 30,040 used more words (5) because the zero splits the non-zero digits apart with an "and". When both zeros are adjacent (like in 10,500 where they're in tens and ones), I get fewer words. When zeros separate non-zero digits (like in 30,040), I need more connecting words.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. You are trying specific examples to gather evidence and look for a pattern before making any claims.
Conjecturing
"A number with more zeros is always shorter to say than a number with fewer zeros." Is this always true, sometimes true, or never true?
Compare numbers with 1 zero, 2 zeros, and 3 zeros. Count the words each time.
A mathematician's response

Let me test systematically.

1 zero: 47,082 = "forty-seven thousand and eighty-two" (6 words)

2 zeros: 40,082 = "forty thousand and eighty-two" (5 words). Fewer words! The claim holds so far.

3 zeros: 40,002 = "forty thousand and two" (4 words). Even fewer!

But wait: 10,000 has 4 zeros = "ten thousand" (2 words). And 90,034 has 1 zero = "ninety thousand and thirty-four" (5 words). Hmm, that's fewer words than 47,082 with 1 zero.

Actually, I think this is SOMETIMES TRUE. More zeros generally means fewer words because we skip those places. But the position of the zeros matters. The pattern isn't absolute.

Mathematician's thinking: You are conjecturing. You are making a claim based on evidence, but you know you need to test it further.
Generalising
Can you write a rule for when you need the word "and" when reading a number? Test your rule with at least 5 different numbers.
When does "and" appear, and when is it left out?
A mathematician's response

I started by listing some numbers and whether they use "and":

47,382 = "forty-seven thousand, three hundred AND eighty-two" (has "and")

60,508 = "sixty thousand, five hundred AND eight" (has "and")

40,000 = "forty thousand" (no "and")

93,041 = "ninety-three thousand AND forty-one" (has "and")

20,300 = "twenty thousand, three hundred" (no "and")

Wait, that last one surprised me. Why is there no "and"? Let me think. 20,300 ends in the hundreds exactly. There are no tens or ones, so no "and."

My rule: Use "and" to connect the tens and ones part only when there are tens or ones to read. If the number ends in the hundreds exactly (like 20,300), there is no "and". If there are tens or ones after the hundreds, use "and" before them.

Testing: 15,420 = "fifteen thousand, four hundred AND twenty" (ones/tens exist, so "and" appears). 30,700 = "thirty thousand, seven hundred" (no tens or ones, so no "and"). My rule works!

Mathematician's thinking: You are generalising. You are finding the rule that explains the pattern across many examples.
