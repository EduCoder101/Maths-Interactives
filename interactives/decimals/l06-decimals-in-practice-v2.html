Reaction Time: The Decimal Challenge

A reaction time measures how quickly you respond to something. It is measured in seconds, and it's always a decimal.

Go Deeper: Decimals Investigations

These tasks will push your thinking beyond finding answers. You will explore, make conjectures, and reason like a mathematician.

Specialising
A student tested their reaction time 5 times and got: 0.234, 0.219, 0.241, 0.228, 0.235. Order these from fastest to slowest. Then find the difference between their fastest and slowest times.
A mathematician's response

First, I'll order from fastest to slowest by comparing the decimals. I look at the tenths place first: all have 2, so I move to hundredths. I see: 2, 3, 3, 4, 4. Wait, that's not in order. Let me list them: 0.234, 0.219, 0.241, 0.228, 0.235.

Ordering by hundredths: 1 (0.219), 2 (0.228), 3 (0.234, 0.235), 4 (0.241). Within 0.234 and 0.235, I compare thousandths: 4 < 5, so 0.234 < 0.235.

Fastest to slowest: 0.219, 0.228, 0.234, 0.235, 0.241.

Fastest: 0.219. Slowest: 0.241. Difference: 0.241 - 0.219 = 0.022 seconds.

That's 22 thousandths of a second between best and worst. The student is fairly consistent.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. That means you work with specific examples, comparing decimals step-by-step, looking for patterns.
Conjecturing
'Rounding reaction times to the nearest tenth always keeps the same ranking (fastest to slowest order).' Always, sometimes, or never true?
A mathematician's response

I need to test this with examples. Let me take Mia (0.234) and Liam (0.245). Mia is faster (smaller). Round both to nearest tenth: 0.2 and 0.2. They become EQUAL when rounded! We can't tell who's faster.

Let me try Zara (0.189) and Jack (0.198). Zara is faster. Round: 0.2 and 0.2. Again equal!

But try Zara (0.189) and Ruby (0.312). Round: 0.2 and 0.3. Order preserved.

SOMETIMES TRUE. Rounding can make different times look equal, which destroys the ranking. The closer two times are, the more likely rounding will make them appear equal.

Mathematician's thinking: You are conjecturing. You test cases, find counterexamples, and decide if your claim is always, sometimes, or never true.
Generalising
In 100m sprinting, a 'false start' is recorded when a runner reacts in under 0.100 seconds. Why do you think scientists chose this specific number? Write a rule explaining what 0.100 seconds means in terms of place value, and why the zeros matter.
A mathematician's response

0.100 seconds = 100 thousandths of a second = one tenth of a second. The zeros matter because 0.1 (one decimal place) suggests precision only to the nearest tenth. But 0.100 (three decimal places) tells us the measurement is precise to the nearest thousandth.

In science, 0.100 means 'exactly one hundred thousandths, measured to three decimal places.' The trailing zeros communicate precision. Scientists chose 0.100 because human reaction time cannot physically be faster than about 0.1 seconds. So any recorded time below 0.100 must be a false start (the runner anticipated rather than reacted).

My rule: Trailing zeros in a measurement show how precisely it was measured. 0.1, 0.10, and 0.100 are equal in value but different in precision.

Mathematician's thinking: You are generalising. You use specific facts (reaction times, rounding) to discover broader rules (precision and place value are linked).
