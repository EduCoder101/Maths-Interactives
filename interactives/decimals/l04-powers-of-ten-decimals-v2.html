Learning Intention and Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning to multiply and divide decimals by 10 and 100 by shifting digits along the place value columns.

Success Criteria

  • I can explain that multiplying by 10 moves every digit one place to the left.
  • I can explain that dividing by 10 moves every digit one place to the right.
  • I can multiply and divide decimals by 10 and 100.
  • I can explain that the decimal point stays fixed while the digits move.

Card 2 of 10

Connecting to What We Know

Remember from whole numbers: multiplying by 10 makes each digit 10 times bigger. The same rule works for decimals.

Show a visual: 0.003 → ×10 → 0.03 → ×10 → 0.3 → ×10 → 3 → ×10 → 30

0.003   →   0.03   →   0.3   →   3   →   30

Each ×10 shifts the digit one column to the left.

Big Idea

It's all one system. The same ×10 rule works on both sides of the decimal point.

Go Deeper: Powers of Ten with Decimals

These tasks will push your thinking beyond finding answers. You will explore, make conjectures, and reason like a mathematician.

Specialising
Start with 0.008. Multiply by 10 four times in a row, writing each result. Then divide your final answer by 10 four times. Do you get back to where you started?
A mathematician's response

0.008 × 10 = 0.08. × 10 = 0.8. × 10 = 8. × 10 = 80. Now reverse: 80 ÷ 10 = 8. ÷ 10 = 0.8. ÷ 10 = 0.08. ÷ 10 = 0.008. Yes! I got back to 0.008. Multiplying and dividing by the same amount undoes the operation. The 8 travelled from thousandths all the way to tens, then back again.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. That means you are trying specific examples to gather evidence about how operations work.
Conjecturing
"Multiplying a decimal by 10 always makes the number have one fewer decimal place."
Always, sometimes, or never true?
A mathematician's response

0.456 × 10 = 4.56 (3 places → 2 places). 0.50 × 10 = 5.0 (2 places → 1 place, or 0 places if we drop the trailing zero). 0.100 × 10 = 1.00 (3 places → 2 places, or 0). Hmm, what about 0.005 × 10 = 0.05 (3 places → 2 places). What about 1.0 × 10 = 10 (1 place → 0 places). SOMETIMES TRUE. When the last decimal digit is non-zero, it always loses one decimal place. But if there are trailing zeros, you might lose more. And if the number was already a whole number with no decimal places, it doesn't apply.

Mathematician's thinking: You are conjecturing. That means you are making a claim based on evidence, but recognising that a pattern may not hold in all cases.
Generalising
Write a rule that connects "multiply by 10", "multiply by 100", and "multiply by 1,000" to the number of places each digit shifts.
A mathematician's response

× 10 = shift 1 place left. × 100 = shift 2 places left. × 1,000 = shift 3 places left. The pattern: the number of zeros in the multiplier tells you how many places to shift. 10 has 1 zero → 1 shift. 100 has 2 zeros → 2 shifts. 1,000 has 3 zeros → 3 shifts. Same for division but shifting right. My rule: count the zeros in 10, 100, or 1,000. That's how many columns every digit moves.

Mathematician's thinking: You are generalising. That means you are looking for a rule or pattern that works across many examples, and expressing it in a concise way.
