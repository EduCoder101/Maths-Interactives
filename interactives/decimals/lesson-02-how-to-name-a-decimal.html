🏷️ The "Last Name" Rule

Just like people have a first and last name, decimals have one too. The "last name" tells you what kind of parts it is made of.

To find the last name, look at the final digit, whichever place value column it sits in gives the decimal its name.

⭐ The Rule

Read the digits after the decimal point as a number, then give it the "last name" of the column where the final digit lives.

3

Let's apply the rule to 0.321

0.321
Tenths 3
Hundredths 2
Thousandths 1 ← last digit!
📢 The Answer

The last digit (1) sits in the thousandths column.
So the "last name" is thousandths.

0.321 = "three hundred and twenty-one thousandths"

Read all the digits after the decimal point as a whole number, then add the "last name" of the final digit's column.

Deep Dive

Why is 0.04 called "4 hundredths" and not "4 tenths"?

0.04
Ones 0
Point .
Tenths 0 placeholder
Hundredths 4 ← last digit!
✅ The Answer

The digit 4 sits in the hundredths house, not the tenths house. The zero in the tenths column is just a placeholder, it tells us the tenths seat is empty. The last digit's position determines the name.

Sam is incorrect
✅

The digit 4 in 0.04 is in the hundredths column, not the tenths column. The zero in the tenths place is a placeholder. The correct name is "4 hundredths". If it were "4 tenths", the decimal would be written as 0.4.
