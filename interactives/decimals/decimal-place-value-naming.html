Decimal Place Value Naming

Name the value of each decimal, from tenths to hundred-thousandths!

Learning Intention & Success Criteria ▼

Learning Intention

We are learning to practise naming decimals accurately using the Last Name Rule.

Success Criteria

  • I can read a decimal and identify its correct name based on the final digit's place value
  • I can match a decimal to its word form
  • I can distinguish between similar-sounding names (e.g. four tenths vs four hundredths)

Worked Example

Let's name a decimal step by step using the Last Name Rule.

What is the value of 0.25?

Step 1: Read the digits after the decimal point: 25
Step 2: Find the last digit's column. The 5 is in the hundredths column.
Step 3: Use the last digit's column as the "surname" for the whole number: twenty-five hundredths.
Answer: 0.25 = twenty-five hundredths

Key Vocabulary ▼

Tenths are the first column after the decimal point. In 0.3, the 3 is in the tenths column.

Hundredths are the second column after the decimal point. In 0.25, the 5 is in the hundredths column.

Thousandths are the third column after the decimal point. In 0.125, the 5 is in the thousandths column.

Decimal Place Value Investigations

You've mastered the names of decimal place value columns. Now let's investigate the patterns and connections that make decimals work.

Task 1: Specialising
In whole numbers, each column is 10 times bigger than the one to its right. Ones, tens, hundreds, thousands... Now look at decimal columns going the other way from the decimal point: tenths, hundredths, thousandths. Write down what each column is worth as a fraction AND as a decimal. What pattern connects the whole number columns to the decimal columns?
Can you describe the pattern in your own words? What happens each time you move one column to the right of the decimal point?
A mathematician's response

Each column to the right is 10 times SMALLER (or you divide by 10). Let me show this:

Ones = 1

Tenths = 0.1 (which is 1/10)

Hundredths = 0.01 (which is 1/100)

Thousandths = 0.001 (which is 1/1000)

Ten-thousandths = 0.0001 (which is 1/10000)

Hundred-thousandths = 0.00001 (which is 1/100000)

The pattern is: moving right always divides by 10, whether you are in the whole number side or the decimal side. The decimal point is not a wall; it is just a marker for the ones column. At first I thought the pattern might be different for decimals because they are smaller numbers, but the division pattern is exactly the same!

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. That means you are trying specific examples to gather evidence, looking for a pattern before you make any claims.
Task 2: Always, Sometimes, or Never?
"A decimal with more digits after the decimal point is always a smaller number."
Is this always true, sometimes true, or never true? Test with examples and explain your thinking.
A mathematician's response

This is SOMETIMES true. Let me test some examples:

0.5 (one digit) compared to 0.25 (two digits): 0.5 > 0.25, so more digits IS smaller here.

But 0.5 (one digit) compared to 0.75 (two digits): 0.75 > 0.5, so more digits is BIGGER in this case!

And 0.9 (one digit) compared to 0.123 (three digits): 0.9 > 0.123, so one digit is bigger.

At first I was tempted to say "always" after the first example, but that was too quick! The number of decimal digits tells you nothing about the size. The actual VALUES in those columns matter much more.

Mathematician's thinking: You are conjecturing. That means you are making a claim that you believe is true, based on your evidence, and then testing it carefully. Finding one counterexample is enough to prove an "always" statement false.
Task 3: Justifying
A student says: "Hundredths are bigger than tenths because a hundred is bigger than ten." Explain carefully why this student's thinking is wrong. Use examples to prove it.
Think about what the "-ths" ending means. What happens when you split something into more pieces?
A mathematician's response

The student is confusing the NAME of the column with its VALUE. You are right that a hundred is bigger than ten, so that part of their thinking is correct. But hundredths (1/100 = 0.01) are SMALLER than tenths (1/10 = 0.1).

The "-ths" ending flips it around! When you add "-ths" to a number, it means you have split 1 into that many pieces. So:

Tenths = split 1 into 10 pieces = each piece is 0.1

Hundredths = split 1 into 100 pieces = each piece is 0.01

It is like sharing a pizza: if you share it among 100 people, you give each person much less than if you share among just 10 people!

Mathematician's thinking: You are justifying. That means you are not just saying the student is wrong. You are explaining why their reasoning does not work, using clear examples and logic.
