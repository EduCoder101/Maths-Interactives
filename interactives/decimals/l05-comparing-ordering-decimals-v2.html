Learning Intention and Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning to compare and order decimals using place value reasoning and ghost zeros.

Success Criteria

  • I can use ghost zeros to give decimals the same number of places.
  • I can compare two decimals by reading digits from left to right.
  • I can order a set of decimals from smallest to largest.
  • I can explain why more decimal digits does not always mean a larger number.
Card 1 of 10

Which Is Bigger: 0.4 or 0.399?

Show both numbers. Ask students to vote or think about which is bigger.

0.4
0.399

Many people pick 0.399 because it has more digits. But let's think about what these numbers actually mean.

0.4 = 400 thousandths
0.399 = 399 thousandths

So 0.4 is bigger!
Important

More digits does NOT mean a bigger number. Do not be fooled by how long a decimal looks.

Card 2 of 10

Making Decimals the Same Length

Here is the trick: we can add zeros to the end of decimals without changing their value.

Before (different lengths):

0.4
0.399

After (same length):

0.400
0.399

Now compare: 400 thousandths vs 399 thousandths. Easy!

What Are Ghost Zeros?

Ghost zeros are zeros we add to the end of a decimal to make both numbers the same length. They do not change the value of the number.

Card 3 of 10

Comparing with Ghost Zeros

Which is larger: 0.75 or 0.8?

Step 1Write them vertically, lining up the decimal points: 0.75 and 0.8
Step 2Add a ghost zero to make them the same length: 0.75 and 0.80
Step 3Compare: 75 hundredths vs 80 hundredths
Step 480 > 75, so 0.8 > 0.75
Alternative

Compare from left to right. Tenths: 7 vs 8. Since 8 > 7, we know 0.8 is larger immediately. We did not even need the ghost zero here.

Card 4 of 10

Ordering Three Decimals

Put in ascending order: 0.2, 0.21, 0.199

Step 1Line up decimal points and add ghost zeros: 0.200, 0.210, 0.199
Step 2Compare tenths: 0.199 has 1 in the tenths place, but the others have 2. So 0.199 is the smallest.
Step 3Now compare 0.200 and 0.210. Tenths are both 2. Look at hundredths: 0 vs 1. So 0.200 < 0.210
Step 4Ascending order: 0.199, 0.2, 0.21
Key Idea

Always compare from left to right, just like with whole numbers. Ghost zeros make this easier by equalising the lengths.

Go Deeper: Decimals Investigations

These tasks will push your thinking beyond finding answers. You will explore, make conjectures, and reason like a mathematician.

Specialising
Find three decimals between 0.3 and 0.4. Then find three decimals between 0.31 and 0.32. Can you always find more decimals between any two decimals?
A mathematician's response

Between 0.3 and 0.4, I can find: 0.31, 0.35, 0.39. All three are between 0.3 and 0.4.

Between 0.31 and 0.32, I can find: 0.311, 0.315, 0.319. Now they are much closer together.

Can I find decimals between 0.311 and 0.312? Yes: 0.3111, 0.3115, 0.3119. I can zoom in further and further.

I notice that I can always zoom in closer. There are infinitely many decimals between any two different decimals. This connects to the idea of infinite divisibility in the number system.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. That means trying specific examples to gather evidence and look for patterns.
Conjecturing
"When comparing two positive decimals, the one with the larger tenths digit is always the larger number." Always, sometimes, or never true?
A mathematician's response

Let me test with 0.9 vs 0.8: tenths are 9 vs 8, and 9 > 8, so 0.9 > 0.8. True here.

Test with 0.5 vs 0.49: tenths are 5 vs 4, and 5 > 4, so 0.5 > 0.49. True again.

But wait. What about 2.3 vs 1.9? The tenths digits are 3 vs 9, so 9 > 3. But 2.3 > 1.9 because the whole-number part comes first! The statement only works when the digits to the left of the tenths place are the same.

My answer: SOMETIMES TRUE. It is true only when both numbers have the same whole-number part.

Mathematician's thinking: You are conjecturing. That means making a claim based on evidence, then testing it to find when it breaks down.
Generalising
Write a step-by-step method (like a recipe) for ordering any set of decimals from smallest to largest. Your method must work every time.
A mathematician's response

Step 1: Add ghost zeros so all decimals have the same number of decimal places.

Step 2: Compare the whole-number parts (ones, tens, etc.). Group decimals by their whole-number value.

Step 3: Within each group, compare the tenths digit. Those with smaller tenths are smaller.

Step 4: If tenths are equal, compare hundredths. Continue until you find a difference.

Step 5: If all digits match, the decimals are equal.

Test: Order 3.1, 3.09, 2.999. Ghost zeros: 3.100, 3.090, 2.999. Step 2: ones digits are 3, 3, 2. So 2.999 is smallest. Step 3: compare 3.100 and 3.090. Tenths: 1 vs 0. So 3.090 < 3.100. Final order: 2.999, 3.09, 3.1. Works!

Mathematician's thinking: You are generalising. That means taking specific examples and building a general method that works for all cases.
