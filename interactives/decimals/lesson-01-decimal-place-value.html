🤔 What is a decimal?

💬 Ask the class

What is a decimal? When you hear the word "decimal", what does it mean?

Definition

A decimal is a number that shows a part of a whole number. The digits after the decimal point represent parts that are smaller than one.

The word "decimal" comes from the Latin word decem, meaning ten, because our number system is based on groups of ten.

🔍 The Zoom Concept

On a number line, there's a big gap between 0 and 1. But that gap is full of numbers. We just need to zoom in to see them.

Each time we zoom in, we divide each section into 10 equal parts, the same ÷10 rule that governs the entire place value system.

Teaching · Expanded Form
Writing Decimals in Expanded Form

What is Expanded Form?

Expanded form means writing a number as the sum of each digit's value. We break the number apart so you can see exactly what each digit is worth.

💡 Key Idea

Every digit in a decimal has a place and a value. Expanded form shows us those values added together.

Start with What You Know

You've seen expanded form with whole numbers before:

354
Expanded Form

300 + 50 + 4

Each digit is multiplied by the value of its column:

Hundreds 3 3 × 100 = 300
Tens 5 5 × 10 = 50
Ones 4 4 × 1 = 4

The same idea works for decimals, we just keep going past the decimal point.

Worked Example

Writing 0.347 in Expanded Form

0.347

Step 1: Identify each digit's place value column.

Tenths 3 3 × 0.1 = 0.3
Hundredths 4 4 × 0.01 = 0.04
Thousandths 7 7 × 0.001 = 0.007

Step 2: Write the values as a sum.

✅ Expanded Form

0.3 + 0.04 + 0.007

Read it aloud: "three tenths plus four hundredths plus seven thousandths"

Worked Example

Writing 5.209 in Expanded Form

5.209

This number has a whole number part and a decimal part. We expand both!

Point .

Hundredths 0 0 × 0.01 = 0
Thousandths 9 9 × 0.001 = 0.009
✅ Expanded Form

5 + 0.2 + 0.009

🤔 What about the zero?

The digit 0 in the hundredths place has a value of 0. We don't write 0 in expanded form, but it still matters! It holds the place so the 9 stays in the thousandths column, not hundredths.

The Zero Rule

When a digit is 0, its value is 0. We leave it out of the expanded form, but it's still doing an important job as a placeholder.

Example: 12.045

10 + 2 + 0.04 + 0.005

The 0 in the tenths column is skipped, but without it, 0.45 would be a different number!

Example: 3.08

3 + 0.08

The 0 in the tenths column is skipped, the 8 stays in hundredths.

Expanded Form, Summary

📌 Remember

Expanded form = writing a number as the sum of each digit's value.

Digits that are 0 are left out of the expanded form (their value is 0), but they still hold the place for other digits.

You can go both ways: decimal → expanded form, or expanded form → decimal.

Q13
Comparing decimals
Which is larger: 0.45 or 0.405? Explain how you know.
0.45 is larger
✅

Compare tenths first: both have 4 tenths. Compare hundredths: 0.45 has 5 hundredths, but 0.405 has 0 hundredths. 5 hundredths > 0 hundredths, so 0.45 > 0.405.

Q14
Comparing decimals
Which is larger: 0.8 or 0.72?
0.8 is larger
✅

0.8 = 0.80 (8 tenths). 0.72 = 7 tenths and 2 hundredths. 8 tenths > 7 tenths, so 0.8 > 0.72. More digits doesn't mean larger!

Q15
Place value reasoning
The digit 5 appears in both 0.5 and 0.05. How many times larger is the 5 in 0.5 than the 5 in 0.05?
10 times larger
✅

In 0.5, the 5 is in the tenths place (value = 0.5). In 0.05, the 5 is in the hundredths place (value = 0.05). Since 0.5 ÷ 0.05 = 10, the 5 in 0.5 is 10 times larger.

🔓 Go Deeper: Decimal Place Value Investigations

These tasks push your thinking beyond naming columns and identifying digits. You will investigate why the place value system works the way it does, and explore patterns in decimal multiplication.

Specialising
When you multiply a decimal by 10, what happens to each digit? Try these multiplications and track where each digit moves.
Multiply each by 10. What do you notice about where the digits end up?
A mathematician's response

I started with 0.35 × 10. The 3 is in the tenths column (worth 0.3). After multiplying by 10, the answer is 3.5. The 3 is now in the ones column (worth 3). So the 3 moved one column to the left.

I tried another: 0.47 × 10 = 4.7. Both digits moved one place left. The 4 went from tenths to ones, and the 7 went from hundredths to tenths.

Then I tried 0.8 × 10 = 8. The 8 in the tenths column became 8 in the ones column. Same pattern again.

Every time, every digit shifted exactly one column to the left. I was starting to think this always happens when you multiply by 10.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. That means you are trying specific examples to gather evidence and look for patterns before making any claims.
Conjecturing
"When you multiply any decimal by 10, every digit moves exactly one place to the left. The decimal point moves, too. You can think of it as the decimal point sliding one place to the right."
Is this always true, sometimes true, or never true? Give examples to support your answer.
A mathematician's response

My conjecture: always true.

I tested it with lots of different decimals. 0.1 × 10 = 1. The single 1 moved from tenths to ones. 0.5 × 10 = 5. Same thing. 0.9 × 10 = 9. Still the same.

But I thought, what about decimals with more columns? I tried 0.25 × 10 = 2.5. The 2 moved from tenths to ones, and the 5 moved from hundredths to tenths. Both moved left. I tried 0.99 × 10 = 9.9. Same result.

I tried to find a case where it broke. But it never did. Every single time, the decimal point slid to the right, which is the same as saying every digit moved one column left. That's what multiplying by 10 means in our place value system.

Mathematician's thinking: You are conjecturing. That means you are making a claim based on evidence from examples. Your conjecture might be proven true or false later.
Justifying
Look at these two decimals: 0.6 and 0.06. A student says that 0.6 is 10 times bigger than 0.06.
Is the student right? Can you prove it using place value, not just division?
A mathematician's response

I need to think carefully about this. The student says 0.6 is 10 times bigger than 0.06. Let me check by dividing: 0.6 ÷ 0.06 = 10. Yes, so mathematically the student is right.

But the student asked me to prove it using place value, not just division. So let me think about the columns.

0.6 means 6 tenths. 0.06 means 6 hundredths. The 6 in 0.6 is in the tenths column. The 6 in 0.06 is in the hundredths column. The tenths column is one column to the left, so it is 10 times bigger than the hundredths column.

Since the same digit (6) appears in each number, but in columns that are 10 times apart, the whole number 0.6 must be 10 times bigger than 0.06. The student is right, and now I have shown why using the structure of place value itself.

Mathematician's thinking: You are justifying. That means you are explaining why something is true, not just whether it works. You are building a proof based on the rules you know.
