Learning Intention and Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning that adding same-denominator fractions can make a sum greater than one whole, and that we can rename that sum as a mixed number.

Success Criteria

  • I can add same-denominator fractions whose total is greater than one whole.
  • I can see how many whole ones a top-heavy fraction makes by grouping the pieces.
  • I can convert an improper fraction to a mixed number, for example I know that nine eighths is one and one eighth.
  • I can explain how a mixed number is built from a whole-number part and a fraction part.

Go Deeper

These tasks push your thinking. You will explore, test a claim, and justify your reasoning like a mathematician.

Specialising
Write down some improper fractions with a denominator of 4 that are bigger than one whole, such as 5/4, 6/4, 7/4 and 8/4. Change each one into a mixed number or a whole number.
What do you notice happens to the whole-number part as the top number grows?
A mathematician's response

I worked through each one. 5/4 is 1 whole and 1/4, so 1 1/4. Then 6/4 is 1 2/4 and 7/4 is 1 3/4.

When I got to 8/4, the leftover was 0, so it is exactly 2 wholes and no fraction part at all.

I noticed the whole-number part jumps up by one each time the top number reaches the next multiple of 4. The fraction part counts up from 1/4 to 3/4, then resets when a new whole is made.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. That means you are trying specific examples to gather evidence before making any claim.
Conjecturing
Look at 10/10. Is it a whole number or a fraction? A student says it must be a fraction because it is written with a top and a bottom.
Is the student right? Write what you think and explain it with a picture or an example.
A mathematician's response

At first I thought the student had a point, because 10/10 does look like a fraction with a top and a bottom.

But then I drew a bar in 10 equal pieces and shaded all 10 of them. The whole bar was shaded, which is exactly one whole. So 10/10 is the same as 1.

So my answer is that it is both, in a way. It is written as a fraction, but its value is the whole number 1. Any fraction where the top equals the bottom, like 3/3 or 8/8, is worth exactly one whole. The student was wrong to say it cannot be a whole number.

Mathematician's thinking: You are conjecturing. That means you make a claim you believe is true, then test it carefully before you trust it.
Justifying
A student changed 9/4 into a mixed number and wrote 9/4 = 1 5/4, saying "4 goes into 9 once, with 5 left over".
Is the answer fully correct? If not, explain the mistake and write the correct mixed number.
A mathematician's response

I checked the student's leftover. They said 4 goes into 9 once with 5 left over, but 1 times 4 is only 4, and 9 − 4 = 5. So the leftover really is 5. But that is the warning sign.

A proper fraction part must be smaller than one whole. The leftover 5/4 is bigger than one whole, so the job is not finished. 4 actually goes into 9 two times: 2 times 4 is 8, and 9 − 8 = 1.

So the correct answer is 9/4 = 2 1/4. The student stopped too early, leaving a fraction part that was still bigger than a whole.

Mathematician's thinking: You are justifying. That means you are checking whether an answer and its reason really hold up, and finding exactly where a mistake creeps in.
