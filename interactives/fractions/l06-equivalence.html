Learning Intention and Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning that the same quantity can be named by different fractions when we re-divide the whole.

Success Criteria

  • I can re-divide a whole into more pieces without changing the amount that is shaded.
  • I can write equivalent fractions using a diagram, in words, and in fraction notation.
  • I can find an equivalent fraction by multiplying the numerator and denominator by the same number.
  • I can explain why we must do the same thing to the top and the bottom.
Card 1 of 9

The Coin Swap

I have one $1 coin. You have two 50c coins.

Who has more money?

Same amount, different names

Nobody has more. $1 and 50c + 50c are the same amount of money, just shared into a different number of coins.

Fractions work the same way. We can cut the same amount into a different number of pieces and the amount does not change. When two fractions name the same amount we call them equivalent.

Card 2 of 9

Re-dividing the Bar

Here is one whole chocolate bar. It is the number 1. I have shaded exactly one half of it.

One half shaded

Watch what happens to the name of the shaded part as I keep cutting, without eating or adding any chocolate.

Big idea

The shaded amount never changed. Only the number of pieces changed. 1/2, 2/4 and 4/8 all name the same amount. They are equivalent fractions.

Card 3 of 9

Example 1: Building the Half Family

1
Start with the whole

This bar is one whole, the number 1. I shade 12 of it.

1 shaded piece out of 2 equal pieces is 1/2
2
Re-divide into quarters

I cut each of the two pieces in half. Now there are 4 equal pieces. The same chocolate is shaded, but now it is 2 of the pieces.

2 shaded out of 4 is 2/4

The amount did not change, so 1/2 = 2/4.

3
Re-divide into eighths

I cut every piece in half again. Now there are 8 equal pieces and 4 are shaded.

4 shaded out of 8 is 4/8
4
Record the equivalent fractions
1/2 = 2/4 = 4/8
Three ways to record it

Diagram: the same shaded bar. Words: one half equals two quarters equals four eighths. Notation: 1/2 = 2/4 = 4/8.

Re-dividing each piece into 2 doubled both the shaded pieces and the total pieces. That is the secret we will turn into a number rule next.

Card 4 of 9

Example 2: Thirds, and the Number Shortcut

1
Shade one third

A new whole, cut into 3 equal pieces. I shade 13.

1 out of 3 is 1/3
2
Re-divide into sixths

I cut each third in half. Now 6 pieces, and 2 are shaded.

2 out of 6 is 2/6
1/3 = 2/6
3
The number shortcut

Cutting every piece into 2 multiplied the top by 2 and the bottom by 2:

1/3 → top 1 × 2 = 2, bottom 3 × 2 = 6 → 2/6
The rule

To make an equivalent fraction, multiply the numerator and the denominator by the same number. What we do to the bottom, we must do to the top, because that is exactly what re-dividing the whole does to a diagram.

So 2/3 = (2×2)/(3×2) = 4/6 as well.

Round Complete

Go Deeper

These tasks push your thinking. You will explore, test a claim, and justify your reasoning like a mathematician.

Specialising
Start with 1/2. Re-divide the whole into more and more pieces and write down three different fractions that are equal to 1/2.
Look at the numbers in your fractions. What do you notice about the numerator and the denominator each time?
A mathematician's response

I started by cutting each half in two to get 2/4. Then I cut those in two to get 4/8. Then I tried cutting the halves into three to get 3/6.

So three fractions equal to one half are 2/4, 4/8 and 3/6.

I noticed that the denominator is always double the numerator: 4 is double 2, 8 is double 4, 6 is double 3. That makes sense, because one half means the shaded part is always exactly half of the whole.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. That means you are trying specific examples to gather evidence before making any claim.
Conjecturing
A student says: "To make an equivalent fraction, I can add the same number to the top and the bottom. So 1/2 is the same as 2/3 because I added 1 to each."
Is this always true, sometimes true, or never true? Use a diagram or the number rule to test it.
A mathematician's response

My answer is never true for equivalence. I tested the claim. Is 1/2 the same as 2/3?

I drew one half, then I drew two thirds. Two thirds covers more of the bar than one half, so they are not the same amount. The student is wrong.

I worked out why. Re-dividing the whole multiplies the pieces, it does not add to them. When I cut each half in two, the 1 shaded piece became 2, which is times 2, not plus 1. Adding the same number to the top and bottom changes how big the shaded part is compared with the whole, so it cannot give an equivalent fraction.

Mathematician's thinking: You are conjecturing and then testing. A claim in maths has to survive being tested with examples and a counterexample beats it.
Justifying
Is 3/4 equivalent to 6/8?
Prove your answer in two different ways: once with a diagram by re-dividing the whole, and once with the number rule.
A mathematician's response

Yes, they are equivalent. Here are my two proofs.

Diagram: I shade 3 out of 4 pieces of a bar. Then I cut each piece in half, giving 8 pieces. The shaded part is now 6 pieces. I did not add or remove any shading, so 3/4 and 6/8 are the same amount.

Number rule: the bottom went from 4 to 8, which is times 2. So I do the same to the top: 3 × 2 = 6. That gives 6/8, so they match.

Both methods agree, so I am confident the answer is yes.

Mathematician's thinking: You are justifying. Proving the same result two different ways is powerful, because if the methods agree you can really trust the answer.
