Learning Intention and Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning to add and subtract fractions that have the same denominator, where the denominator names the size of the piece and does not change.

Success Criteria

  • I can explain that the denominator names the size of each piece and stays the same when I add or subtract.
  • I can add two fractions with the same denominator by adding only the numerators.
  • I can subtract two fractions with the same denominator and interpret the difference.
  • I can spot and fix the mistake of adding the denominators together.

Each slice is one eighth
What this shows

Nobody cut the pizza again, so the slices are still eighths. We ate 2 + 3 = 5 slices, so together that is 5/8, not 5/16. Only the top number changed.

Round Complete

Go Deeper

These tasks push your thinking. You will explore, test a claim, and justify your reasoning like a mathematician.

Specialising
Choose any denominator, such as 8. Write down three different addition or subtraction questions with that denominator, such as 2/8 + 5/8 and 7/8 − 1/8. Work out each answer.
Look at the bottom number in every question and every answer. What do you notice about it?
A mathematician's response

I picked eighths. My three questions were 2/8 + 5/8 = 7/8, then 7/8 − 1/8 = 6/8, then 3/8 + 4/8 = 7/8.

I noticed that the bottom number was 8 every single time, in the questions and in the answers. The only number that changed was the top one.

That makes sense, because the pieces never changed size. They were all eighths, so the answer is always counted in eighths too.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. That means you are trying specific examples to gather evidence before making any claim.
Justifying
A student says: "When I add fractions, I add the tops and I add the bottoms. So 1/4 + 1/4 = 2/8."
Is the student right? Use a diagram or a real example to prove your answer, and explain why adding the bottoms does not work.
A mathematician's response

At first I thought the student might be right, because adding both numbers feels tidy. But then I tested it with a picture.

I drew a bar in 4 equal pieces and shaded 1, that is 1/4. Then I shaded 1 more, so 2 pieces out of 4 are shaded. That is 2/4, which is one half.

The student's answer 2/8 is only a quarter, which is much less than a half. So the student is wrong.

Adding the bottoms makes the pieces smaller, but nobody actually cut the bar into more pieces. The pieces stayed as quarters, so the answer must be counted in quarters: 1/4 + 1/4 = 2/4.

Mathematician's thinking: You are justifying. That means you are not just giving an answer, you are proving why the rule must be true and why a wrong rule fails.
Generalising
Think about adding any two fractions that have the same bottom number, like a/d + b/d.
Write a rule in your own words that always works. Then explain why the bottom number stays the same no matter what.
A mathematician's response

I tried a few examples first to find the pattern. With fifths, 1/5 + 3/5 = 4/5. With sixths, 2/6 + 3/6 = 5/6. Each time I just added the tops.

My rule: if two fractions have the same bottom number, add the top numbers and keep the bottom number the same.

I tried to break my rule by changing the bottom, but then the pieces were different sizes and I could not just count them together. So the rule only works when the bottoms match.

The bottom stays the same because it names the size of one piece. I am only counting how many same-size pieces there are, and counting does not change how big each piece is.

Mathematician's thinking: You are generalising. That means you are making a rule that works every time, not just for the examples you tried.
