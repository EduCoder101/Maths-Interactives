Learning Intention and Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning that a unit fraction of a collection is found by dividing the quantity by the denominator.

Success Criteria

  • I can draw a tape diagram that splits a quantity into equal groups.
  • I can explain that the denominator tells me how many equal groups to make.
  • I can find a unit fraction of a quantity by dividing by the denominator.
  • I can write the matching division for a unit fraction of a quantity.

Go Deeper

These tasks push your thinking. You will explore, test a claim, and justify your reasoning like a mathematician.

Specialising
Find 1/2 of 20, then 1/4 of 20, then 1/5 of 20, then 1/10 of 20.
Write the four answers in order. As the denominator grows, what happens to the size of the answer? Why does that make sense?
A mathematician's response

I worked them out one at a time. 1/2 of 20 is 20 ÷ 2 = 10. 1/4 of 20 is 20 ÷ 4 = 5. 1/5 of 20 is 20 ÷ 5 = 4. 1/10 of 20 is 20 ÷ 10 = 2.

So my answers in order are 10, 5, 4, 2. The bigger the denominator, the smaller the answer.

That makes sense because a bigger denominator means I split the 20 into more groups, so each group has to be smaller. Sharing 20 between 10 people gives less each than sharing 20 between 2 people.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. That means you are trying specific examples to gather evidence before making any claim.
Conjecturing
A student says: "To find 1/4 of a number, I can find half, and then halve it again."
Is this always true, sometimes true, or never true? Test it with at least three even numbers and explain what you find.
A mathematician's response

My answer is always true when the number can be shared evenly. I tested it. Half of 20 is 10, and half of 10 is 5. 1/4 of 20 is 20 ÷ 4 = 5. They match.

I tried 16: half is 8, half again is 4, and 16 ÷ 4 = 4. I tried 40: half is 20, half again is 10, and 40 ÷ 4 = 10. They match every time.

I worked out why. Halving means dividing by 2. Halving twice is dividing by 2 and then by 2 again, which is the same as dividing by 4. So it always works, as long as the number divides evenly each time.

Mathematician's thinking: You are conjecturing and then testing. A claim in maths has to survive being tested with examples, and you also try to explain why it works.
Justifying
A student says: "1/3 of 21 must be bigger than 1/3 of 12, because 21 is bigger than 12 and the fraction is the same."
Is the student right? Work out both, and explain why the size of the whole changes the answer.
A mathematician's response

First I worked both out. 1/3 of 12 is 12 ÷ 3 = 4. 1/3 of 21 is 21 ÷ 3 = 7. So 7 is bigger than 4, and the student is right that this one is bigger.

At first I thought the student's reasoning was just lucky, so I drew two tapes. Both are split into 3 equal groups, but the second tape holds more counters in total, so each group has more in it. That is why the answer is bigger.

So the student is correct here, but the real reason is that the whole is bigger. The same unit fraction of a bigger whole gives a bigger part. The fraction being the same is not enough on its own. It is the size of the whole that decides the answer.

Mathematician's thinking: You are justifying. That means you are checking not just whether an answer is right, but whether the reason behind it is sound.
