Learning Intention and Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning to place thirds, fifths, sixths and tenths on a number line using equal jumps.

Success Criteria

  • I can split a 0 to 1 line into equal jumps to make thirds, fifths, sixths and tenths.
  • I can use equal-jump estimation when I cannot just halve the line.
  • I can place and name fractions like 1/3, 2/3, 3/5, 5/6 and 7/10 on a line.
  • I can explain where fifths and tenths line up on the same line.

Go Deeper

These tasks push your thinking. You will explore, test a claim, and justify your reasoning like a mathematician.

Conjecturing
A friend says: "It is harder to draw 1/8 on a number line than 1/10, because 8 is a smaller number than 10."
Which is actually harder to draw accurately by hand, 1/8 or 1/10? Make a claim and say why.
A mathematician's response

My claim: 1/10 is harder to draw by eye than 1/8, even though they look similar.

To draw 1/8 I can keep halving: cut in half, then half again, then half again. Halving by eye is something I am good at. After three halvings I have eighths and they are very accurate.

But 1/10 cannot be made by halving. I would have to make 10 equal jumps and hope they fit, or first make fifths with five jumps and then halve each one. There are more steps where my estimate could be off. So the number being bigger or smaller is not what matters. What matters is whether I can reach it by halving.

Mathematician's thinking: You are conjecturing. That means you are making a clear claim and giving a reason, ready to test it.
Specialising
Draw a 0 to 1 line with fifths above it and tenths below it. Find every place where a fifth-mark lines up exactly with a tenth-mark.
Write each matching pair as two fractions. What pattern do you notice in the numbers?
A mathematician's response

I lined them up and checked each fifth. I found 1/5 meets 2/10, 2/5 meets 4/10, 3/5 meets 6/10, and 4/5 meets 8/10.

At first I thought maybe only some of them would match, but every single fifth landed exactly on a tenth.

The pattern is that the tenths number is always double the fifths number: 2 is double 1, 4 is double 2, and so on. That makes sense, because each fifth is the same length as two tenths.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. That means you are working through each case carefully to collect evidence and spot a pattern.
Justifying
On a 0 to 1 line, which is closer to the middle mark of 1/2: the fraction 3/6 or the fraction 3/5?
Explain how you can be sure, using the line and not just a guess.
A mathematician's response

I started by placing the middle mark, 1/2, halfway along.

Then I checked 3/6. Six jumps make sixths, and three jumps is halfway, so 3/6 sits exactly on 1/2. The distance from the middle is zero.

Next I checked 3/5. Five jumps make fifths, and three jumps is a bit past halfway, because two and a half jumps would be the middle. So 3/5 is a small step to the right of 1/2.

So 3/6 is closer to the middle, because it lands right on it. I am sure because I placed both on the line and compared their positions, not just the numbers.

Mathematician's thinking: You are justifying. That means you are proving your answer with the line, so anyone can see why it must be true.
