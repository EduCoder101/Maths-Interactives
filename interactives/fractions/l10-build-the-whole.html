Learning Intention and Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning to work backwards from a fractional part to find the whole.

Success Criteria

  • I can use a known part to find the value of one equal part.
  • I can build the whole by counting all the parts.
  • I can use a tape diagram to work backwards.
  • I can solve word problems where the whole is unknown.

Go Deeper

Specialising
Different fractions of a whole are all equal to 12. If 2/3 of a number is 12, find the whole. Then do the same for 3/4 is 12, and 4/5 is 12.
What happens to the size of the whole as the fraction gets closer to 1?
A mathematician's response

If 2/3 is 12: one third is 6, whole is 18. If 3/4 is 12: one quarter is 4, whole is 16. If 4/5 is 12: one fifth is 3, whole is 15.

I noticed the whole gets smaller as the fraction gets closer to 1. That makes sense: if a bigger chunk of the whole is still only 12, the whole cannot be very big.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. Trying ordered cases reveals a pattern.
Conjecturing
A student says: "To find the whole, you always multiply the part by the bottom number." For 3/4 is 9, they did 9 × 4 = 36.
Is their method always right, sometimes right, or never right? Check it.
A mathematician's response

It is only sometimes right. It works when the part is a UNIT fraction (top number 1), because then the part already is one part. But for 3/4 is 9, the 9 is three quarters, not one quarter.

The correct whole is 12, not 36. You must first divide by the top to get one part (9 ÷ 3 = 3), then multiply by the bottom (3 × 4 = 12). The student skipped the dividing step.

Mathematician's thinking: You are conjecturing and testing a method with examples to find when it breaks.
Justifying
If 5/6 of a number is 20, is the whole bigger or smaller than 20?
Answer without fully calculating first, then check by working it out. Explain how you knew.
A mathematician's response

I knew the whole had to be bigger than 20 straight away. 5/6 is less than one whole, so if only five sixths is 20, the full six sixths must be more than 20.

Then I checked: one sixth is 20 ÷ 5 = 4, so the whole is 4 × 6 = 24. Yes, 24 is bigger than 20. My reasoning held up.

Mathematician's thinking: You are justifying. Reasoning about size before calculating is a powerful check.
