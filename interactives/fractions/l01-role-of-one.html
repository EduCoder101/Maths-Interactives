Learning Intention and Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning that a fraction only has meaning once we know the whole it came from.

Success Criteria

  • I can explain why one half can be different amounts on different wholes.
  • I can use the number 1 as the whole, the common anchor.
  • I can say why two fractions can only be compared fairly when the whole is the same.

Go Deeper

These tasks push your thinking about why the whole matters.

Conjecturing
"1/2 is always bigger than 1/4."
Is this always true, sometimes true, or never true? Use two different wholes to test it.
A mathematician's response

It is only sometimes true. It is true when both fractions are of the same whole. Half a cake is more than a quarter of the same cake.

But I found a case where it fails. Half of a tiny biscuit is much less than a quarter of a giant cake. So if the wholes are different, 1/2 can be the smaller amount. The claim is only safe once we fix the whole.

Mathematician's thinking: You are conjecturing. A claim has to survive a test, and one counterexample is enough to limit it.
Justifying
A shop sells "1/2 price" at one store and "1/4 off" at another. A friend says the first is always the better deal.
When is the friend right, and when could they be wrong? Explain.
A mathematician's response

The friend is comparing fractions of two different wholes, the two different prices. "Half price" saves more only if the starting prices are the same or close.

If the first shop's item costs far more to begin with, half off a high price could still leave you paying more than a quarter off a low price. To judge the deal you must look at the whole, the actual price, not just the fraction.

Mathematician's thinking: You are justifying. You are testing whether a reason holds in every case, not just the obvious one.
