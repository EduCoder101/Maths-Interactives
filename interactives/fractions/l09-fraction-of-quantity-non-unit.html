Learning Intention and Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning to find a non-unit fraction of a quantity, such as three quarters of a number.

Success Criteria

  • I can find one part by dividing the quantity by the denominator.
  • I can find the fraction I want by multiplying that part by the numerator.
  • I can use a tape diagram to show my thinking.
  • I can solve word problems that ask for a fraction of an amount.

The big idea

Three quarters is 3 lots of 5, which is 15. Find one part, then take as many as the top asks for.

The rule

Divide by the bottom to find one part, then multiply by the top.

Card 3 of 8
Find 34 of 16
 
3/4 of 16 = (16 ÷ 4) × 3 = 4 × 3 = 12
Check it makes sense

Three quarters is most of the whole, so 12 sits just below 16.

Go Deeper

These tasks push your thinking. You will explore, test a claim, and justify your reasoning.

Specialising
Find 2/3, 3/3 and 4/3 of 12.
What happens when the numerator equals the denominator? What happens when it is bigger?
A mathematician's response

One third of 12 is 4. So 2/3 is 8, 3/3 is 12, and 4/3 is 16.

I noticed 3/3 of 12 is just 12, the whole thing. That makes sense, because three thirds is one whole. And 4/3 is bigger than 12, because four thirds is more than one whole.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. Trying ordered examples helps you see a pattern before you make a claim.
Justifying
A student says 3/4 of 20 must be bigger than 2/3 of 20, "because three quarters has bigger numbers than two thirds".
Are they right? Work out both and explain whether their reason is a good one.
A mathematician's response

I worked both out. 3/4 of 20 is 15. 2/3 of 20 needs 20 ÷ 3, which is not a whole number, so I would round in a real problem, but the comparison still works: two thirds of 20 is about 13.3.

So the student's answer is right that 3/4 gives more here. But their reason is shaky. It is not about which fraction has bigger numbers, it is about which fraction is closer to one whole. Three quarters is closer to 1 than two thirds, so it takes more of the same amount.

Mathematician's thinking: You are justifying. A right answer with a weak reason is still a problem worth fixing.
Generalising
When is a non-unit fraction of a number a whole number, and when is it not?
Try several fractions of 24. Write a rule about the quantity and the denominator.
A mathematician's response

I tried fractions of 24. Thirds work because 24 ÷ 3 = 8. Quarters work because 24 ÷ 4 = 6. Sixths work, eighths work. But fifths do not: 24 ÷ 5 is not a whole number.

My rule: a fraction of a number gives a whole number when the denominator divides the quantity exactly. The numerator never causes a problem, it only multiplies a part you already have. So the denominator is the part to watch.

Mathematician's thinking: You are generalising. You made a rule that works beyond the examples you tried.
