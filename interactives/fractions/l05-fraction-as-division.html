Learning Intention and Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning that a fraction is the answer to a sharing problem, so a fraction is a division.

Success Criteria

  • I can share a number of wholes equally and work out how much each person gets.
  • I can write a sharing situation as a fraction.
  • I can explain that the top number is what is shared and the bottom number is the number of sharers.
  • I can show that a divided by b gives the fraction a/b.
Card 1 of 9
3 chocolate bars, shared fairly by 4 children.
Sharing is dividing

No. There are only 3 bars for 4 children, so each share is less than one whole. The number sentence is 3 ÷ 4.

Card 2 of 9
Cut each bar into 4 and share.
One child gets 3 quarter pieces.
Big idea

Each piece is a quarter, and one child gets 3 of them, so 3 ÷ 4 = 3/4. A fraction is the answer to a division.

Card 3 of 9
2 cakes shared by 3 children.
One child gets 2 third pieces.
The rule

2 ÷ 3 = 2/3. The amount shared (2) is the top number, the number of sharers (3) is the bottom.

Card 4 of 9
2 pizzas shared by 5 children.
One child gets 2 fifth pieces.
The same rule

2 ÷ 5 = 2/5. Amount shared on top, number of sharers on the bottom. The rule works every time.

Independent Practice

Write each sharing as a fraction.

Round Complete

Go Deeper

These tasks push your thinking. You will explore, test a claim, and justify your reasoning like a mathematician.

Specialising
Some sharing problems give each person a whole number, not a fraction. For example, 6 shared among 3 gives 2 whole each. Find three more sharing problems that give each person a whole number.
When does sharing give a whole number, and when does it give a fraction? What do you notice about the two numbers?
A mathematician's response

I started by trying 8 shared among 4. That gives 2 whole each. Then 6 shared among 2 gives 3 whole each. Then 8 shared among 2 gives 4 whole each.

At first I thought it just needed the top number to be bigger, so I tried 5 shared among 2. But that gives 5/2, which is not a whole number. So just being bigger is not enough.

I noticed the whole-number ones all had a top number that divided exactly by the bottom number. 8 divides by 4, 6 divides by 2. So sharing gives a whole number when the amount shared is a multiple of the number of sharers.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. That means you are trying specific examples to gather evidence before making any claim.
Conjecturing
A student says: "When you share, the bigger number always goes on the bottom of the fraction." So they write 5 shared among 2 as 2/5.
Is this rule always true, sometimes true, or never true? Test it by sharing 5 things among 2 people with a drawing.
A mathematician's response

My answer is never true. The student has the rule the wrong way round.

I tested it. I shared 5 cakes among 2 people. I gave each person 2 whole cakes, that uses 4 cakes, then I cut the last cake in half and gave each person a half. So each person got 2 and a half, which is 5/2.

If the answer was 2/5 that would be less than one cake each, which cannot be right when there are more cakes than people. So the amount shared goes on top, not the bottom. The correct fraction is 5/2, which is bigger than 1.

Mathematician's thinking: You are conjecturing and then testing. A claim in maths has to survive being tested, and one good counterexample beats it.
Justifying
When is the answer to a sharing problem bigger than 1? Use 5 shared among 2 and 3 shared among 4 in your explanation.
Explain why, using both the sharing picture and the two numbers in the fraction.
A mathematician's response

The answer is bigger than 1 when there is more to share than there are people, so the top number is bigger than the bottom number.

5 shared among 2: there are 5 cakes and only 2 people. Each person can have a whole cake or more, so the share must be bigger than 1. The fraction is 5/2, and the top (5) is bigger than the bottom (2).

3 shared among 4: there are only 3 bars but 4 people. There is not even one whole bar each, so the share must be less than 1. The fraction is 3/4, and the top (3) is smaller than the bottom (4).

So the share is bigger than 1 exactly when the numerator is bigger than the denominator.

Mathematician's thinking: You are justifying. That means you are explaining why something must be true, using both the picture and the numbers, not just checking one case.
