Learning Intention and Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning to add and subtract fractions with related denominators by renaming one fraction so the denominators match.

Success Criteria

  • I can spot when one denominator is a multiple of the other.
  • I can rename a fraction using equivalence so both fractions have the same denominator.
  • I can add or subtract once the denominators are the same, keeping the denominator and working with the numerators.
  • I can solve word problems that need related-denominator addition or subtraction.

Go Deeper

These tasks push your thinking. You will explore, test a claim, and justify your reasoning like a mathematician.

Specialising
Find three different pairs of related-denominator fractions that add up to exactly 7/8. One fraction in each pair must use a denominator of 2, 4 or 8.
As you find each pair, write down the renamed version in eighths. What stays the same every time?
A mathematician's response

I knew the total had to be 7/8, so I worked in eighths the whole time. I started with 6/8 and 1/8. I renamed 6/8 back to 3/4, so my first pair is 3/4 + 1/8 = 7/8.

Then I tried 4/8 and 3/8. Renaming 4/8 gives 1/2, so my second pair is 1/2 + 3/8 = 7/8.

For my third pair I tried 2/8 + 5/8. I could rename 2/8 to 1/4, so 1/4 + 5/8 = 7/8. Every time, once I renamed to eighths the two numerators added to 7. That is what stayed the same.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. That means you are trying specific examples to gather evidence and notice a pattern before making a claim.
Conjecturing
A student adds 1/2 + 1/4 by adding the tops and adding the bottoms. They write 1/2 + 1/4 = 2/6.
Is this answer always wrong, sometimes wrong, or never wrong? Use a bar diagram to test it and explain what the student forgot to do.
A mathematician's response

My claim is that adding the tops and bottoms is always wrong for adding fractions. I tested it on this one. The real answer to 1/2 + 1/4 is 3/4, which I checked on a bar by renaming the half to 2/4 and adding to get 3/4.

But the student's answer was 2/6. I drew 2/6 on a bar and it is smaller than a half. That cannot be right, because I am adding something to a half, so the answer must be bigger than a half.

The student forgot to rename so the denominators matched. Adding the bottoms changes the size of the pieces, which is not allowed. You must make the pieces the same size first, then only the numerators are added.

Mathematician's thinking: You are conjecturing and then testing. A claim in maths has to survive being tested, and a single clear counterexample can settle it.
Justifying
When you add two related-denominator fractions, you always rename to the larger denominator, never the smaller one. Explain why renaming to the smaller denominator does not work for 1/8 + 1/4.
Try renaming both ways and see what happens. Which way gives whole-number pieces you can actually count?
A mathematician's response

First I tried renaming to the larger denominator, 8. I renamed 1/4 to 2/8, so 1/8 + 2/8 = 3/8. That worked neatly.

Then I tried renaming to the smaller denominator, 4. To turn 1/8 into quarters I would have to cut a quarter into a half-piece, because one eighth is half of a quarter. That gives me half a quarter, which is not a whole number of quarters, so I cannot count it cleanly.

So renaming to the larger denominator works because the larger pieces split evenly into the right number of smaller pieces. Going the other way would force me to chop a piece into a fraction of a piece, which defeats the purpose.

Mathematician's thinking: You are justifying. That means you are explaining why a method must work, not just checking that it gives the right answer once.
