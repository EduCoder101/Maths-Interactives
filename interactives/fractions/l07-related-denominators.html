Learning Intention and Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning to compare fractions and find missing numbers when the denominators are related, using an area model.

Success Criteria

  • I can subdivide a rectangle by its length and its width to rename a fraction.
  • I know that denominators are related when one is a multiple of the other (such as 2, 4, 8 or 3, 6 or 5, 10).
  • I can rename one fraction so two fractions share the same denominator, then compare them with <, = or >.
  • I can find a missing number that makes two fractions with related denominators equivalent.

The method

Rename the smaller denominator so both match, then compare the top numbers. So 3/4 > 5/8. The same idea works for the 3, 6 and 5, 10 families: 1/3 = 2/6 and 3/5 = 6/10.

Go Deeper

These tasks push your thinking. You will explore, test a claim, and justify your reasoning like a mathematician.

Specialising
The fraction 1/2 can be renamed in the 2, 4, 8 family as 2/4 and 4/8. Find every fraction equal to 1/2 that you can write with a denominator of 2, 4, 6, 8 or 10.
Which denominators worked, and which did not? What is special about the ones that worked?
A mathematician's response

I tried each denominator in turn. For 4 I got 2/4, for 6 I got 3/6, for 8 I got 4/8, and for 10 I got 5/10. All of these are equal to 1/2.

I thought every even denominator would work, so I tested 3 as well to be sure about odd ones. With a denominator of 3, half would need a top number of one and a half, which is not a whole number, so it does not work.

I noticed the ones that worked all had an even denominator, and the top number was always exactly half of the bottom number: 3 is half of 6, 5 is half of 10. So 1/2 can be made whenever the denominator is even.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. That means you are trying specific cases to gather evidence before making any claim.
Conjecturing
A student says: "To compare any two fractions I can just look at the bigger bottom number. The fraction with the bigger denominator is always the smaller fraction." They use this to claim 5/8 is smaller than 3/4.
Is this rule always true, sometimes true, or never true? Find an example that breaks it if you can.
A mathematician's response

My answer is sometimes true. The rule only works when the top numbers are the same.

For the student's example it happens to be right: 3/4 = 6/8, and 6/8 is bigger than 5/8, so 5/8 really is smaller. So far the rule looks good.

Then I looked for a counterexample. I tried 7/8 and 1/4. The bigger denominator is 8, so the rule says 7/8 should be the smaller one. But 1/4 = 2/8, and 7/8 is much bigger than 2/8. So the rule is broken. You cannot just look at the denominators. You must rename so the denominators match, then compare the top numbers.

Mathematician's thinking: You are conjecturing and then testing. A claim in maths has to survive being tested, and one good counterexample beats it.
Justifying
Two children compare 5/6 and 2/3. One renames 2/3 as sixths. The other renames 5/6 as thirds and gets stuck.
Why can you always rename the fraction with the smaller denominator, but not always the other way round? Use 5/6 and 2/3 to explain.
A mathematician's response

The first child is right. 6 is a multiple of 3, so I can cut each third into 2 to make sixths: 2/3 = 4/6. Now I compare 5/6 with 4/6, and 5/6 is bigger.

The second child got stuck for a good reason. To turn sixths into thirds I would have to join sixths together in pairs, but 5 sixths cannot be split into a whole number of thirds, because 5 is odd. There is one sixth left over.

So I justify it like this: renaming to a bigger denominator means cutting each piece into more pieces, which always works. Renaming to a smaller denominator means joining pieces together, which only works if they group up evenly. With related denominators, always rename the one with the smaller denominator up to the bigger one. That is why the first child succeeded.

Mathematician's thinking: You are justifying. That means you are explaining why a method must work, not just showing that it gave an answer once.
