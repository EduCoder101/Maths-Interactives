Learning Intention and Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning to subtract a fraction from 1 by renaming the whole as a fraction with the same denominator.

Success Criteria

  • I can rename the number 1 as a fraction such as 5/5 or 8/8.
  • I can explain that any fraction with the same top and bottom is equal to 1.
  • I can subtract a fraction from 1 by renaming 1 to match the denominator.
  • I can find the complement of a fraction mentally, the part that makes it up to a whole.

The big idea

The whole bar is 8/8. Take one eighth and 7/8 is left. To subtract from 1, we rename 1 as a fraction with the same denominator. The part left is called the complement.

Go Deeper

These tasks push your thinking. You will explore, work backwards, and justify your reasoning like a mathematician.

Specialising
Subtract each of these from 1: 1/4, 2/4 and 3/4. Write the complement each time.
Look at the fraction you started with and the complement you found. What do the two numerators always add up to?
A mathematician's response

I renamed the whole as 4/4 each time. For 1 − 1/4 I got 3/4. For 1 − 2/4 I got 2/4. For 1 − 3/4 I got 1/4.

I lined up the starting numerator and the complement numerator: 1 and 3, then 2 and 2, then 3 and 1.

Every single time the two numerators added up to 4, which is the denominator. That makes sense, because together they have to rebuild the whole, and the whole is 4/4.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. That means you are trying specific examples to spot a pattern before you make a claim.
Justifying
A fraction is subtracted from 1 and the complement that is left is 2/10. What was the fraction that was subtracted? Explain how you know there is only one answer.
Use the idea that the two parts must rebuild the whole. How can you check your answer?
A mathematician's response

I know the whole is 10/10 because the complement is in tenths. The part that was left is 2/10, so the part that was taken away must be the rest of the whole.

At first I thought I might need to try lots of fractions, but then I realised the two parts have to add to 10/10. So the missing numerator is 10 − 2 = 8. The fraction subtracted was 8/10.

There is only one answer because once the complement is fixed at 2/10, the other part has no choice, it must be 8/10. I checked: 8/10 + 2/10 = 10/10 = 1, so it is correct.

Mathematician's thinking: You are justifying. That means you are not just finding an answer, you are explaining why it must be the only one.
Generalising
Write a rule that works for subtracting any fraction from 1, no matter what the denominator is. Then test your rule on a denominator you have not used yet, such as 12.
Can you describe the rule in words so a friend could follow it without drawing a bar?
A mathematician's response

My first try at a rule was "take the top away from the bottom". I tested it on 1 − 3/8: bottom take away top is 8 − 3 = 5, and the answer is 5/8. That worked.

But I wanted to be careful, so I wrote the rule more fully: rename the whole 1 as a fraction with the same denominator, keep that denominator, and the new numerator is the old denominator take away the old numerator.

I tested it on a new denominator, 12. For 1 − 5/12, the new numerator is 12 − 5 = 7, so the answer is 7/12. The denominator stays 12. My rule still works.

Mathematician's thinking: You are generalising. That means you are making a rule that works every time, not just for the examples you tried.
