Learning Intention and Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning that a bigger denominator means a smaller piece, and that we can use one half as a benchmark.

Success Criteria

  • I can explain that the more people share one whole, the smaller each share becomes.
  • I can compare two unit fractions and say which is bigger using the symbols < and >.
  • I can use one half as a benchmark to judge whether a fraction is close to 0, one half, or 1.
  • I can put unit fractions in order from largest to smallest.

Go Deeper

These tasks push your thinking. You will explore, test a claim, and justify your reasoning like a mathematician.

Specialising
Write these unit fractions in order from largest to smallest: 1/4, 1/10, 1/2, 1/6, 1/3.
Once you have ordered them, look at the denominators. What do you notice about how they line up?
A mathematician's response

I pictured the fraction wall. The piece cut into the fewest parts is the biggest, so 1/2 comes first.

Then I went up by denominator: 1/3, then 1/4, then 1/6, then 1/10. So my order from largest to smallest is 1/2, 1/3, 1/4, 1/6, 1/10.

I noticed something neat. When the unit fractions are in order from largest to smallest, the denominators are in order from smallest to largest: 2, 3, 4, 6, 10. The two orders run in opposite directions.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. That means you are working through specific examples to notice a pattern before you make any claim.
Conjecturing
A student says: "For unit fractions, the fraction with the bigger denominator is always the bigger fraction, because bigger numbers mean more."
Is this always true, sometimes true, or never true? Test it with a few unit fractions before you decide.
A mathematician's response

The student is thinking about whole numbers, where 8 is more than 4. So I tested whether that idea works for unit fractions.

I compared 1/4 and 1/8. On the wall, 1/8 has the bigger denominator, but its piece is smaller, not bigger. So already the claim fails.

I checked one more, 1/3 against 1/10. The bigger denominator is 10, but 1/10 is a tiny sliver, much smaller than 1/3.

So my answer is never true for unit fractions. The student has it backwards. For unit fractions, a bigger denominator means the whole is cut into more pieces, so each piece is smaller.

Mathematician's thinking: You are conjecturing and then testing. A single counterexample is enough to prove a claim false.
Justifying
Mark sorts 5/8 as "close to one half". Sara sorts the very same fraction as "close to 1". One of them has used the benchmark better.
Who is right, and how would you convince the other person? Use the 1/2 benchmark and the distance to 1 in your explanation.
A mathematician's response

First I found the benchmark. One half of 8 is 4, so 4/8 is exactly one half. The fraction 5/8 is only one eighth past that.

Then I checked the distance to 1. The whole is 8/8, so 5/8 is 3/8 short of 1. That is a big gap.

So 5/8 is 1/8 away from one half but 3/8 away from 1. It is much nearer to one half. Mark is right.

To convince Sara, I would say: count the eighths. From one half you take just one more eighth to reach 5/8, but you would need three more eighths to reach the whole. The smaller gap wins, so it is close to one half.

Mathematician's thinking: You are justifying. You are not just naming an answer, you are measuring the distance to each landmark to prove which one is nearer.
