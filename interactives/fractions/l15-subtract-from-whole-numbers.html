Learning Intention and Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning to subtract a fraction from a whole number by cutting one whole into a fraction with the right denominator.

Success Criteria

  • I can decompose a whole number, keeping the other wholes and cutting one into d/d.
  • I can subtract the fraction from the one whole I cut up.
  • I can write the answer as a mixed number, a whole number and a fraction together.
  • I can solve multi-step length problems that need this kind of subtraction.
Card 1 of 8
5 cakes. Someone eats 1/4 of one. How much is left?
The big idea

Keep the other wholes. Cut just one into equal pieces and subtract from that one. The answer is a mixed number.

Card 2 of 8

Example 1: 3 − 15

3 whole bars
3 − 1/5 = 2 and 5/5 − 1/5 = 2 4/5
Card 3 of 8

Example 2: 4 − 110

4 whole bars
4 − 1/10 = 3 and 10/10 − 1/10 = 3 9/10
A quick check

The whole-number part is always one less, because one whole was cut up. The fraction left is the complement: 10 − 1 = 9, so 9/10.

Round Complete

Go Deeper

These tasks push your thinking. You will explore, work backwards, and justify your reasoning like a mathematician.

Specialising
Work out 5 − 1/4, 7 − 1/4 and 9 − 1/4. Write each answer as a mixed number.
Look at the whole-number part of each answer and the fraction part. What changes and what stays the same?
A mathematician's response

For 5 − 1/4 I kept 4 wholes and cut one into quarters, so I got 4 3/4. For 7 − 1/4 I got 6 3/4. For 9 − 1/4 I got 8 3/4.

I lined them up. The fraction part was 3/4 every single time. That part never changed.

What changed was the whole-number part. It was always exactly one less than the number I started with: 4 from 5, 6 from 7, 8 from 9. That is because I used up one whole each time to make the quarters.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. That means you are trying specific examples to notice what stays fixed and what changes.
Justifying
A whole number had a fraction subtracted from it and the answer was 5 3/8. The fraction subtracted was a single fraction less than 1. What was the whole number, and what fraction was subtracted?
Think about how the whole-number part of a mixed-number answer is related to the starting whole number. How can you check?
A mathematician's response

The answer was 5 3/8, so the fraction part is 3/8. I knew from my pattern that the starting whole number is one more than the whole-number part of the answer, because one whole always gets cut up. So the starting number was 5 + 1 = 6.

The whole that was cut up started as 8/8 and ended as 3/8. So the part taken away was 8/8 − 3/8 = 5/8. The fraction subtracted was 5/8.

I checked by working forwards: 6 − 5/8 = 5 and 8/8 − 5/8 = 5 3/8. That matches, so the whole number was 6 and the fraction was 5/8.

Mathematician's thinking: You are justifying. That means you work backwards and then check forwards to be sure your answer is the right one.
Generalising
Write a rule for working out any whole number take away any fraction less than 1, without drawing bars. Test your rule on 8 − 5/6.
Describe both parts of the answer: the whole-number part and the fraction part. Can you say each in one short sentence?
A mathematician's response

My first attempt at a rule was just "the answer is one less, and a fraction". But that was too vague, because I did not say which fraction.

So I made it precise. The whole-number part of the answer is the starting whole number take away 1. The fraction part is the complement of the fraction over its denominator, which is the denominator take away the numerator, all over the denominator.

I tested it on 8 − 5/6. The whole-number part is 8 − 1 = 7. The fraction part is 6 − 5 = 1 over 6, which is 1/6. So the answer is 7 1/6. My rule works.

Mathematician's thinking: You are generalising. That means you are turning a pattern into a rule that works for every whole number and every fraction less than 1.
