Learning Intention and Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning to place halves, quarters and eighths on a number line by halving the half.

Success Criteria

  • I can fold a strip in half, then in half again, to make equal parts.
  • I can find one half of a 0 to 1 line, then halve it to find quarters and eighths.
  • I can place and label 1/2, 1/4, 3/4, 1/8, 3/8, 5/8 and 7/8 on a number line.
  • I can explain why 4/8 sits in the same place as 1/2.

Go Deeper

These tasks push your thinking. You will explore, test a claim, and justify your reasoning like a mathematician.

Specialising
On a 0 to 1 line, the mark for 1/2 sits exactly in the middle. Halve the line again and again. Write down every fraction you find that lands on that same middle point.
Try halving to quarters, then eighths, then sixteenths. What do you notice about the fractions that land on the middle?
A mathematician's response

I cut the line into 4 parts and the middle mark was 2/4. Then I cut it into 8 parts and the middle mark was 4/8. Then into 16 parts, where the middle was 8/16.

So the fractions on the middle point are 1/2, 2/4, 4/8 and 8/16.

I noticed the top number is always exactly half of the bottom number. That makes sense, because the middle point is always half way along, no matter how many parts I cut the line into.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. That means you are trying specific examples to gather evidence before you make any claim.
Justifying
A student says: "On the number line, 4/8 must be further along than 1/2, because 4 and 8 are bigger numbers than 1 and 2."
Is the student right? Use the line to prove your answer.
A mathematician's response

The student is wrong. At first I could see why they might think that, because 4 and 8 do look like bigger numbers.

But I tested it on the line. I counted 4 steps of one eighth from 0. That landed exactly in the middle, in the same place as 1/2.

The reason is that an eighth is a much smaller step than a half. Four small eighth-steps cover the same distance as one big half-step. So 4/8 and 1/2 are the same point, not different ones. On a number line you must look at the position, not just the size of the numbers.

Mathematician's thinking: You are justifying. That means you are not just giving an answer, you are explaining with the line exactly why it is true.
Conjecturing
Now use a line that runs from 0 to 2, cut into eighths. Where would 11/8 sit? Is it before or after the number 1?
Make a claim about how you can tell, just from the fraction, whether a point is before or after 1.
A mathematician's response

I knew that 8/8 is one whole, because eight eighths fill the first whole. So the mark for 1 on this line is at 8/8.

I first guessed 11/8 might be before 1, but then I counted: 11 steps of one eighth is more than 8 steps, so it must be past the 1 mark. It sits 3 eighths after 1.

My claim: if the top number is smaller than the bottom number, the point is before 1. If the top number is bigger than the bottom number, the point is after 1. If they are equal, the point is exactly on 1.

Mathematician's thinking: You are conjecturing. That means you are turning what you noticed into a clear rule that you could test on more examples.
