Fractions I

What is a fraction? Naming the parts of a whole and of a set

Learning Intention & Success Criteria ▼

Learning Intention

We are learning to name a fraction by counting the parts of a whole or of a set, and to explain what the numerator and denominator tell us.

Success Criteria

  • I can split a shape into equal parts and say what fraction is shaded
  • I can say what fraction of a set of objects has a feature, such as how many are coloured
  • I can explain that the numerator counts the parts I want and the denominator is the total number of equal parts
1
Equal parts make a whole

A fraction is what we get when we split one whole into equal parts. The parts must be the same size.

One whole, split into 4 equal parts

Here the whole bar has been split into 4 equal parts. Each part is one quarter of the whole.

2
Numerator and denominator

A fraction has two numbers. We write one above the other.

34
numerator the top number. It counts the parts we want. Here, 3 parts.
denominator the bottom number. It is the total number of equal parts. Here, 4 parts.
3 of the 4 equal parts are shaded, so the fraction is three quarters
3
A fraction of a shape

To name the fraction shaded, count in two steps.

Step one: count all the equal parts. There are 3, so the denominator is 3.

Step two: count the shaded parts. There are 2, so the numerator is 2.

The fraction shaded is 23 (two thirds). Numerator on top, denominator on the bottom.
4
A fraction of a set

Fractions work for a group of objects too. The whole is the whole set.

What fraction of the counters are purple?

Step one: count every counter. There are 5, so the denominator is 5.

Step two: count the purple counters. There are 2, so the numerator is 2.

25 of the counters are purple (two fifths).

Your turn

Work this one out in your head before you reveal it.

What fraction of the bar is shaded?
There are 5 equal parts (denominator) and 3 are shaded (numerator), so the fraction is 35 (three fifths).
