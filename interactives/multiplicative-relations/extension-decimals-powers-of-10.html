Multiply and divide decimals by 10, 100, and 1,000

Learning Intention & Success Criteria ▼

Learning Intention

We are learning to multiply and divide decimal numbers by powers of 10 by understanding how the decimal point shifts.

Success Criteria

  • I can multiply a decimal by 10, 100, or 1,000.
  • I can divide a whole number or decimal by 10, 100, or 1,000.
  • I can explain the pattern: multiplying moves digits left, dividing moves digits right.
  • I can solve problems that mix multiplication and division by powers of 10.

The decimal point shift

When you multiply by a power of 10, the digits move to the left (the number gets bigger). When you divide, they move to the right (the number gets smaller). The decimal point stays still; the digits slide past it.

Multiplying

x 10: shift digits 1 place left.

x 100: shift digits 2 places left.

x 1,000: shift digits 3 places left.

Dividing

÷ 10: shift digits 1 place right.

÷ 100: shift digits 2 places right.

÷ 1,000: shift digits 3 places right.

Worked example

Calculate: 3.7 x 100

Multiplying by 100 means shifting the digits 2 places to the left.

3.7 → 37 (1 place) → 370 (2 places)

3.7 x 100 = 370

The 3 moved from the ones to the hundreds. The 7 moved from the tenths to the tens. A zero fills the empty ones column.

Common mistake

Adding zeros to the end without thinking about the decimal point. For example, 0.6 x 10 is 6, not 0.60. The digit moves, the decimal point does not.

Specialising
Start with 0.001. Keep multiplying by 10 and write down each result until you reach 10,000. How many multiplications does it take? Now try starting from a different number, like 0.05. What changes?
Write out the full chain of values. What pattern do you notice?
A mathematician's response

From 0.001: 0.01, 0.1, 1, 10, 100, 1,000, 10,000. That is 7 multiplications.

From 0.05: 0.5, 5, 50, 500, 5,000. I reach 5,000 after 5 steps but never hit exactly 10,000.

The pattern: each multiplication shifts the decimal point one place. The number of steps from 0.001 to 10,000 is 7, because the digits need to cross 7 place value columns.

Conjecturing
"Multiplying by 10 and then dividing by 10 always gives you back the number you started with." Is this always true? What about multiplying by 100 then dividing by 10 twice? Test with at least three different starting numbers.
Does it matter what order you do the operations?
A mathematician's response

Test 3.7: x 10 = 37, then ÷ 10 = 3.7. Back to start.

Test 0.04: x 10 = 0.4, then ÷ 10 = 0.04. Back to start.

My conjecture: multiplying then dividing by the same power of 10 always returns you to the original number. This is because they are inverse operations. Also, multiplying by 100 then dividing by 10 twice is the same as multiplying by 100 then dividing by 100, since 10 x 10 = 100.

Generalising
Explain in your own words why our number system is called a "base 10" system. How does this connect to why multiplying and dividing by powers of 10 is as simple as shifting the decimal point?
Think about what makes 10 special compared to other numbers.
A mathematician's response

Our system is called base 10 because each place value column is worth 10 times the one to its right. Ones, tens, hundreds, thousands: each step is x 10. Tenths, hundredths, thousandths: each step is ÷ 10.

Because the columns are built on powers of 10, multiplying a number by 10 is the same as moving every digit one column to the left. Dividing moves every digit one column right. That is why the "shortcut" works: it is not really a shortcut at all, it is the structure of the number system itself.

If we used base 8 (like some computers do), multiplying by 8 would be the simple shift instead.

Mathematician's thinking: You are generalising. That means you are connecting a specific skill (decimal shifts) to the bigger mathematical idea (place value structure).
