Lesson 1 · Year 5 Mathematics · Multiplicative Relations

The Multiplication Radar

Estimation as a checking tool for multiplication

Learning Intention & Success Criteria ▼

Learning Intention

We are learning to use estimation to judge whether a multiplication answer is reasonable before we calculate.

Success Criteria

  • I can round a number to its nearest 10 or 100 to make a quick estimate.
  • I can say whether a multiplication answer is reasonable by comparing it to my estimate.
  • I can multiply a one-digit number by 10, 100, and 1000 mentally.
Phase 1 · The Hook
My Radar for Error is going off...

Spot the Problem

A teacher marked a test from another class and saw this answer:

Student's Answer
On the test paper 47 x 6 = 1,282

Before even checking the working, the teacher knew this answer was wrong. How?

Think About It

47 is close to 50. What is 50 x 6? Could the answer possibly be in the thousands?

The Reasoning

47 is close to 50. And 50 x 6 = 300. So the answer must be somewhere near 300. An answer of 1,282 is over four times too large. The student made a place value error, but you do not need to diagnose it to know it is wrong. A quick Radar check catches it instantly.

The Radar for Products

Mathematicians do not just calculate. They know roughly what the answer should be before they pick up a pen.

In multiplication, this is even more important than in addition and subtraction, because errors can be much larger. Today we are turning on our Radar for Products.

The Process

1. Estimate first (round to a friendly number).
2. Calculate.
3. Check: does my answer match my estimate?

Phase 2 · Explicit Instruction
Modelling the estimation process

Example 1: Estimate then calculate

Problem 38 x 7

Step 1: Round to a friendly number.

38 → 40

Estimate: 40 x 7 = 280

Step 2: Check direction.

I rounded up from 38 to 40, so my estimate is slightly too big. The real answer should be a little less than 280.

Step 3: Calculate and check.

38 x 7 = 266

266 is close to 280. Radar confirmed.

Example 2: Larger number

Problem 246 x 4
246 → 250

Estimate: 250 x 4 = 1,000

Rounded up slightly, so the real answer should be a little less than 1,000.

246 x 4 = 984

984 is close to 1,000. Reasonable.

Example 3: Catching an error

Student's Answer
On the test paper 46 x 7 = 282

Radar check: 50 x 7 = 350

The answer should be near 350, maybe a little less. But 282 is almost 70 below the estimate. That is suspicious.

Finding the error: The student calculated 7 x 6 = 42 correctly (writing 2, carrying 4). But then did 7 x 4 = 28 and forgot to add the carried 4. They wrote 28 instead of 32.

Correct answer: 322

The Radar caught the carry error. Without estimating first, this mistake could go unnoticed.

Extension · Go Deeper
Mathematicians think deeper

Complete the Radar practice to unlock

When you get a Sharp Radar (average distance 7% or better), this extension will unlock.

Task 1: Specialising

Look at how rounding in different directions affects your estimate.

Specialising
Estimate these three multiplications by rounding each number to the nearest ten:
28 x 4, 32 x 6, 49 x 3
Now work out the exact answers. Which estimate was closest to the actual answer? Which was furthest away?
Model Response

Estimates:

28 x 4: Round to 30 x 4 = 120

32 x 6: Round to 30 x 6 = 180

49 x 3: Round to 50 x 3 = 150

Actual answers:

28 x 4 = 112 (estimate off by 8)

32 x 6 = 192 (estimate off by 12)

49 x 3 = 147 (estimate off by 3)

Answer: The estimate for 49 x 3 was closest (only 3 away). The estimate for 32 x 6 was furthest away (12 away). This is interesting because we rounded 49 up to 50, but 32 up to 30 less. When we round in one direction a lot, the estimate gets further away.

Why this matters: Sometimes a small rounding is better than a big rounding. If you round 32 down to 30, that is only a small change (2 away). But when you multiply by 6, that small difference becomes 12 in the answer. Mathematicians think about which way to round based on how big the change is.

Task 2: Conjecuring

Make a claim and test it with examples.

Conjecturing
A mathematician claims: "When you round one number UP and the other number DOWN, your estimate will always be too high."
Do you think this is always true, sometimes true, or never true? Find at least two examples to test the claim.
Model Response

Claim: "When you round one UP and one DOWN, the estimate is always too high."

I think this is sometimes true, not always. Let me test:

Example 1: 29 x 12

Round 29 → 30 (up), 12 → 10 (down).

Estimate: 30 x 10 = 300

Actual: 29 x 12 = 348

The estimate is too LOW, not too high!

Example 2: 48 x 31

Round 48 → 50 (up), 31 → 30 (down).

Estimate: 50 x 30 = 1,500

Actual: 48 x 31 = 1,488

The estimate is too HIGH.

Conclusion: The claim is only sometimes true. When one number rounds by a lot, it matters more than the other number. The claim depends on the sizes of the rounding changes.

Why this matters: Even when a pattern seems obvious, mathematicians test it. We found a counter-example (29 x 12) that disproved the claim. This is important because if students believe the claim is always true, they might make wrong predictions about estimates.

Task 3: Justifying

Compare two strategies and decide which is better.

Justifying
A student says: "To get the closest estimate, you should always round both numbers to the nearest ten."
Another student says: "Sometimes it is better to only round one number and keep the other exact." Who do you agree with? Justify with an example.
Model Response

I agree with the second student. Sometimes rounding both numbers makes the estimate worse, not better.

Example: 48 x 5

Strategy 1: Round both numbers

48 → 50 and 5 → 10

Estimate: 50 x 10 = 500

Actual: 48 x 5 = 240

Off by 260. That is terrible!

Strategy 2: Keep 5 exact, round only 48

48 → 50, keep 5 as 5

Estimate: 50 x 5 = 250

Actual: 48 x 5 = 240

Off by only 10. Much better!

Why: The number 5 is already a friendly number. Rounding it to 10 makes no sense. Rounding only 48 to 50 gives a much better estimate.

Why this matters: Good mathematicians are flexible. They do not follow a rule blindly ("always round both"). Instead, they think: "Is this number already friendly? Do I actually need to round this one?" This is a much more powerful strategy.
