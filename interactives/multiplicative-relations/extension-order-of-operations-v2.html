Learning Intention and Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning to evaluate and construct number sentences that involve more than one operation, following the correct order of operations.

Success Criteria

  • I can evaluate expressions with two operations (no brackets) by doing multiplication or division before addition or subtraction.
  • I can evaluate expressions with brackets by doing the bracketed part first.
  • I can insert brackets into an expression to make it equal a given value.
  • I can evaluate longer expressions with three operations and identify the correct answer.
  • I can arrange numbers into an expression using all four operations to reach a target.
Card 1 of 4

Why order matters

When an expression has more than one operation, the order in which you calculate changes the answer. Mathematicians agreed on a set of rules so that everyone gets the same result.

The rules

Step 1: Do anything inside brackets first.

Step 2: Do multiplication and division (left to right).

Step 3: Do addition and subtraction (left to right).

Card 2 of 4

Worked example

Evaluate: 3 + 4 x 2

There are no brackets. We have addition and multiplication. Which comes first?

Multiplication comes before addition.

First: 4 x 2 = 8

Now do the addition.

3 + 8 = 11

The answer is 11, not 14. If you added first, you would get 7 x 2 = 14, which is wrong.

Common mistake

Working left to right without thinking about the rules. Always scan the whole expression for multiplication and division before doing addition and subtraction.

🔓 Go Deeper: Bracket Investigations

These tasks push your thinking beyond evaluating expressions. You will explore how brackets change meaning, make conjectures, and reason about structure.

Specialising
Start with the expression 2 + 3 x 4 + 5. By placing one pair of brackets in different positions, how many different answers can you make? List each one.
Try every possible bracket position. Be systematic.
A mathematician's response

No brackets: 2 + 3 x 4 + 5 = 2 + 12 + 5 = 19.

(2 + 3) x 4 + 5 = 5 x 4 + 5 = 25.

2 + (3 x 4) + 5 = 2 + 12 + 5 = 19 (same as no brackets, since multiplication already goes first).

2 + 3 x (4 + 5) = 2 + 3 x 9 = 2 + 27 = 29.

I found 3 different answers: 19, 25, and 29. Brackets around the multiplication did not change the answer because multiplication already happens first.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. That means you are trying specific examples to see what happens.
Conjecturing
When do brackets change the answer, and when do they not? Make a conjecture based on your examples above. Test it with a new expression of your choice.
Think about what the brackets are doing to the order of operations.
A mathematician's response

My conjecture: brackets only change the answer when they force you to do an addition or subtraction before a multiplication or division. If the brackets surround something that would already be done first (like a multiplication), they have no effect.

I tested with 10 - 2 x 3. Without brackets: 10 - 6 = 4. With (10 - 2) x 3 = 8 x 3 = 24. The brackets changed the answer because they forced subtraction to happen before multiplication.

Mathematician's thinking: You are conjecturing. That means you are making a claim you believe is true, based on evidence.
Generalising
"You can always make the answer bigger by adding brackets." Is this statement always true, sometimes true, or never true? Provide examples to support your answer.
Consider expressions with subtraction as well as addition.
A mathematician's response

Sometimes true. Adding brackets can make the answer bigger or smaller, depending on the operations involved.

Bigger: (2 + 3) x 4 = 20, versus 2 + 3 x 4 = 14. Brackets made it bigger.

Smaller: 10 - (2 + 3) x 1 = 10 - 5 = 5, versus 10 - 2 + 3 x 1 = 10 - 2 + 3 = 11. Brackets made it smaller.

The direction depends on what you group together and what operations surround the brackets. There is no universal rule for "bigger" or "smaller".

Mathematician's thinking: You are generalising by testing an always/sometimes/never claim. You found counterexamples in both directions.
