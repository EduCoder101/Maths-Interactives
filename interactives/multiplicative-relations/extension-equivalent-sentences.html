Year 6 Extension

Equivalent Number Sentences

Use inverse operations to find unknowns and write equivalent facts

Learning Intention & Success Criteria ▼

Learning Intention

We are learning to construct and solve equivalent number sentences using the relationship between multiplication and division.

Success Criteria

  • I can find a missing number in a multiplication or division sentence.
  • I can write an equivalent division sentence for a given multiplication fact, and vice versa.
  • I can explain why multiplication and division are inverse operations.
  • I can decide whether a number sentence is true or false and correct it.

Fact families

Every multiplication fact has a family of related facts. If you know one, you can write all the others.

Example: the 7, 8, 56 family

7 x 8 = 56

8 x 7 = 56

56 ÷ 7 = 8

56 ÷ 8 = 7

These four sentences all use the same three numbers. They are equivalent because they express the same relationship.

Using inverses to find unknowns

When a number sentence has a missing value, you can use the inverse operation to find it.

Example: find the missing number in _ x 6 = 54

Multiplication and division are inverses. To undo "x 6", we divide by 6.

Rewrite using the inverse: 54 ÷ 6 = ?

54 ÷ 6 = 9. So the missing number is 9.

Check: 9 x 6 = 54. Correct.

Remember

The inverse of multiplication is division. The inverse of division is multiplication. You can always check your answer by substituting it back into the original sentence.

🔓 Go Deeper: Inverse Investigations

These tasks push your thinking beyond finding single answers. You will explore families of facts, make conjectures, and reason about structure.

Specialising
Pick any product, for example 60. Write down every multiplication fact that gives 60 (using whole numbers only). Then write the matching division facts for each one. How many equivalent sentences can you find?
Try to be systematic. How will you make sure you have found them all?
A mathematician's response

I listed the factor pairs of 60 systematically, starting from 1:

1 x 60, 2 x 30, 3 x 20, 4 x 15, 5 x 12, 6 x 10.

Each factor pair gives 4 equivalent sentences (two multiplications, two divisions). That is 6 pairs x 4 = 24 equivalent sentences in total. I stopped looking for new pairs when the smaller factor exceeded the square root of 60 (about 7.7), because after that I would just be repeating pairs in reverse.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. That means you are working with a specific example to gather evidence and spot patterns.
Conjecturing
Compare the number of equivalent sentences for 60 with the number for 36 and 17. Make a conjecture: what determines how many equivalent sentences a number has? Does it depend on whether the number is prime or composite?
Think about what makes some numbers have more factor pairs than others.
A mathematician's response

36 has factor pairs: 1 x 36, 2 x 18, 3 x 12, 4 x 9, 6 x 6. That is 5 pairs, giving 20 sentences (though the 6 x 6 pair only gives 2 unique sentences since both factors are the same).

17 is prime: its only pair is 1 x 17. Just 4 sentences.

My conjecture: the more factors a number has, the more equivalent sentences it produces. Composite numbers with many factors (like 60) have far more than prime numbers.

Mathematician's thinking: You are conjecturing. That means you are making a claim you believe is true, based on evidence, but it still needs to be tested further.
Generalising
Write a general rule: if a number has n factor pairs, how many equivalent multiplication and division sentences can you write? Explain why your rule works. Does it change when both factors are the same (like 6 x 6 = 36)?
Try to explain your rule in words that a younger student could understand.
A mathematician's response

General rule: each factor pair where the two factors are different gives 4 equivalent sentences (two multiplications, two divisions). A factor pair where both factors are the same (a square root pair) gives only 2 unique sentences.

This works because swapping the order of multiplication gives a different sentence (7 x 8 versus 8 x 7), and each multiplication has a matching division. But when both factors are identical, swapping changes nothing, so you get fewer unique sentences.

For a younger student: "Every way you can split a number into two groups gives you a little family of four facts. But if both groups are the same size, the family only has two."

Mathematician's thinking: You are generalising. That means you are writing a rule that works for every number, not just the ones you tested.
