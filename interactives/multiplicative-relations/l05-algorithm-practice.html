Key idea

The algorithm is a compressed version of the area model. Every step in the algorithm corresponds to something we did in the area model. Same maths, faster writing.

1.8 × 7 = 56. This is the right section of the area model. Write 6 in the ones column, carry 5 (that is 5 tens).
2.8 × 4 tens = 32 tens. Plus the 5 carried tens = 37 tens. Write 7 in the tens column.
3.The 3 from 37 tens goes into the hundreds column. That gives us 3 hundreds.
4.Answer: 376. Check against the area model: 320 + 56 = 376. Correct.
The carry is not magic

The carry is the tens (or hundreds) that were too large to fit in the current column. The area model makes this visible: every carried digit is a place value regrouping.

Go Deeper

You have mastered the algorithm for multiplying 3-digit (or 2-digit) numbers by a one-digit number. These three tasks will help you explore the mathematics at a deeper level: spotting errors, testing claims, and finding patterns.

Task 1. Specialising
A student solved 347 × 8 using the algorithm. Their working shows correct steps, but they got 2,876. The correct answer is 2,776. Find the error in their working.
This is a real error that happened in a Year 5 classroom. Work the algorithm yourself first.
Thinking like a mathematician

I'll work 347 × 8 step by step to compare:

Ones: 8 × 7 = 56. Write 6, carry 5 (that is 5 tens).

Tens: 8 × 4 = 32, plus the 5 carried tens = 37 tens. Write 7, carry 3 (that is 3 hundreds).

Hundreds: 8 × 3 = 24 hundreds, plus the 3 carried hundreds = 27 hundreds. Write 27.

Answer: 2,776.

The student got 2,876. Comparing: the hundreds and thousands are swapped. The error must be in how they wrote down their answer after the working. They calculated correctly but wrote the final number as 2,876 instead of 2,776.

Actually, wait. Let me check if the error was in the tens carry: if the student carried 4 instead of 3 from the tens column, then: 8 × 3 = 24, plus 4 = 28. Writing this gives 2,876. Yes, that is it.

The error: In the tens column, the carry was written as 4 instead of 3. This mistake in the carry then affected the hundreds calculation.
Task 2. Conjecturing
Is this statement always true, sometimes true, or never true?

When you multiply a 3-digit number by a 1-digit number using the algorithm, the answer is always a 4-digit number.
A good mathematician tests a claim with examples. Can you find an example that proves it false?
Testing the claim

I need to find out if the answer is always, sometimes, or never a 4-digit number.

Test 1: 100 × 1 = 100. That is a 3-digit number, not 4 digits.

Test 2: 999 × 9 = 8,991. That is a 4-digit number.

Test 3: 100 × 9 = 900. That is a 3-digit number again.

I found examples where the answer has 3 digits and examples where it has 4 digits. This means the claim is sometimes true.

Why? Small 3-digit numbers multiplied by small 1-digit numbers give 3-digit answers. Larger 3-digit numbers (like 999) multiplied by larger 1-digit multipliers give 4-digit answers. It depends on the size of both numbers.
Task 3. Generalising
You have the digits 3, 5, 7, and 9. You must use exactly three of them to make a 3-digit number and multiply by the remaining digit. Each digit is used exactly once. Find the arrangement that gives the largest possible product. Then write a rule explaining how to arrange digits to maximise the product.
Hint: not all arrangements need to be tested if you think about place value. What matters most?
Finding the pattern

First thought: I need to check which digit should be the multiplier and how to arrange the other three.

The multiplier has a big impact. Let me test using 9 as the multiplier (since it is the largest digit):

If multiplier is 9:

Remaining digits are 3, 5, 7. To make the 3-digit number as large as possible, I arrange them in descending order: 753.
753 × 9 = 6,777

What if I used a different multiplier? Say 7 as the multiplier:

Remaining digits are 3, 5, 9. Best arrangement: 953.
953 × 7 = 6,671

The first option (753 × 9 = 6,777) is larger.

The rule: Use the largest available digit as the multiplier. Arrange the remaining three digits in descending order to form the 3-digit number. This maximises the product because the multiplier affects every digit of the number being multiplied, and the hundreds digit of the 3-digit number has the most place value.
