Strategy Showdown

Three methods, one answer. Which strategy wins?

Learning Intention & Success Criteria ▼

Learning Intention

We are learning to choose the most efficient multiplication strategy for a given problem and justify our choice.

Success Criteria

  • I can solve a multiplication using the area model, lattice, and formal algorithm.
  • I can compare my answers across methods to verify they are correct.
  • I can identify when a mental strategy is more efficient than a written one.
  • I can explain why I chose a particular strategy for a particular problem.

Three methods, one answer

You have learned three written strategies for multiplication: the area model, the lattice method, and the formal algorithm. All three give the same answer because they are doing the same maths, just arranged differently.

In this activity, you will solve each problem using all three methods and decide which one felt most efficient. There is no single "best" method. The right choice depends on the numbers.

🔓 Go Deeper: Strategy Investigations

These tasks push your thinking beyond finding answers. You will explore, make conjectures, and reason like a mathematician.

Specialising
Pick any 5 two-digit numbers. Multiply each one by 25 using the method of your choice. Now look at your answers.
What do you notice about every answer? Can you explain why this happens?
A mathematician's response

I tried 25 x 12 = 300, 25 x 17 = 425, 25 x 36 = 900, 25 x 43 = 1,075, and 25 x 58 = 1,450.

I noticed every answer ends in 00, 25, 50, or 75. That is interesting. I first thought it was a coincidence, so I tried a few more and the same thing happened.

Then I thought about why. 25 x 4 = 100. So every group of 4 gives a whole hundred. If the other number is a multiple of 4, the answer ends in 00. If there is a remainder of 1, you get an extra 25. Remainder 2 gives 50. Remainder 3 gives 75. That explains the four possible endings.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. That means you are trying specific examples to gather evidence, looking for a pattern before you make any claims.
Conjecturing
"When one of the numbers in a multiplication is close to a power of 10 (like 9, 11, 49, 99, or 101), a mental strategy is always the best choice."
Is this always true, sometimes true, or never true? Give examples to support your answer.
A mathematician's response

My first instinct was "always". 99 x 13 = 100 x 13 - 13 = 1,287. That is quick. And 49 x 6 = 50 x 6 - 6 = 294. Also quick.

But then I tried 99 x 87. Mentally: 100 x 87 - 87 = 8,700 - 87. I found that tricky to do in my head because 8,700 - 87 is not simple to subtract mentally. I could do it, but a written method would have been faster for me.

So my conjecture is: sometimes true. The mental strategy works well when the compensation step (the adjustment) is small and easy. But when the second number is large, the compensation step can be harder than just using a written method.

Mathematician's thinking: You are conjecturing. That means you are making a claim that you believe is true, based on your evidence, but you know it still needs to be proved.
Generalising
Write a set of rules that a Year 4 student could follow to decide whether to use a mental strategy, a written strategy, or a calculator for any multiplication problem.
Try to make your rules work for every multiplication, not just the ones you have practised. Test your rules on at least 3 different problems to check they give good advice.
A mathematician's response

I started by listing the types of problems where mental worked well for me: multiplying by 5, 10, 25, 50, or numbers near them like 9, 11, 49, 99. The key seemed to be whether I could see a shortcut that avoided hard arithmetic.

I first tried writing the rule as "use mental if the numbers are small", but that failed. 47 x 6 is small, but there is no obvious shortcut. I would use a written method.

After more testing, here are my rules: First, check for convenient factors. Can you break a number into factors that make the multiplication easy? (Like 25 x 16 = 25 x 4 x 4.) If so, try mental. Second, check for near-multiples of 10. Can you round one number and adjust? (Like 99 x 13.) If the adjustment is simple, try mental. Third, if neither shortcut works, use a written method. Fourth, use a calculator when the numbers are very large or when you need to focus on understanding the answer, not finding it.

I tested these rules on 25 x 36 (mental: 25 x 4 x 9 = 900), 47 x 38 (no shortcut, use written), and 2,847 x 193 (too large, use calculator). The rules gave sensible advice all three times.

Mathematician's thinking: You are generalising. That means you are making a rule that works every time, not just for the examples you tried. A good general rule is one that someone else could follow and get good results.
