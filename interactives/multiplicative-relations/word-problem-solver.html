Learning Intention & Success Criteria ▼

Learning Intention

We are learning to solve multi-step multiplication word problems by identifying hidden steps and ignoring extraneous information.

Success Criteria

  • I can use the four-step protocol: Visualise, Estimate, Calculate, Check.
  • I can identify hidden steps in a word problem.
  • I can identify extraneous information that is not needed.
  • I can choose an efficient strategy for the calculation.
  • I can check my answer against a Radar estimate.

The four-step protocol

Every word problem follows the same four steps. This keeps your thinking organised.

Step 1: Visualise

Read the problem. Picture it. Draw a quick sketch if it helps.

Step 2: Estimate

Use your Radar to estimate. Round the numbers and calculate roughly.

Step 3: Calculate

Choose your strategy (mental, area model, lattice, algorithm) and solve.

Step 4: Check

Compare your answer with your Radar estimate. Is it reasonable?

Hidden steps and extraneous information

Some word problems have hidden steps: you need to do more than one calculation, even though the problem looks like it only asks one question.

Example: hidden step

"A hall has 24 rows of 15 chairs, plus 6 extra chairs at the front for teachers. How many chairs in total?"

The hidden step: first multiply 24 x 15 = 360, then add 6. Answer: 366.

Other problems have extraneous information: facts that are true but not needed for the calculation.

Example: extraneous info

"Sarah is 11 years old. She buys 6 boxes of cat food. Each box has 12 pouches. How many pouches?"

Sarah's age is extraneous. You only need 6 x 12 = 72 pouches.

🔓 Go Deeper: Problem Design Investigations

Now that you can solve word problems, explore what makes a problem tricky in the first place.

Specialising
Take this simple problem: "A shop has 15 shelves with 24 books on each. How many books?" Now add one piece of extraneous information to it. Then add a hidden step. Write out all three versions of the problem.
What changed about the difficulty each time? Did the calculation itself change?
A mathematician's response

Original: "A shop has 15 shelves with 24 books on each. How many books?" Answer: 360.

With extraneous info: "A shop has 15 wooden shelves, each 90 cm wide, with 24 books on each. How many books?" The width is extraneous. The answer is still 360.

With a hidden step: "A shop has 15 shelves with 24 books on each. The shop has 3 rooms like this. How many books in total?" Now you need 15 x 24 = 360, then 360 x 3 = 1,080.

I first thought adding extraneous info would change the difficulty a lot, but it mainly just tests whether you can spot what matters. The hidden step actually changes the calculation itself.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. That means you are working through a specific example carefully to understand how different features of a problem affect its difficulty.
Conjecturing
Make a conjecture: is it possible to write a word problem that has both a hidden step AND extraneous information, where the extraneous information looks like it could be the hidden step? Try to write one.
This is the trickiest kind of word problem. What makes it so hard?
A mathematician's response

I tried: "A baker makes 24 trays with 15 biscuits each. The oven uses 3 kilowatts of electricity. He packs biscuits into bags of 6. How many bags does he need?"

The electricity (3 kilowatts) looks like a number you might use, but it is extraneous. The hidden step is: first calculate 24 x 15 = 360 biscuits, then divide 360 ÷ 6 = 60 bags.

It is hard because the extraneous number (3) is tempting. You could mistakenly multiply by 3 instead of dividing by 6. The trick is always to re-read the question and ask: "What am I actually being asked?"

Mathematician's thinking: You are conjecturing. That means you are testing whether something is possible by trying to construct an example.
Generalising
Write a set of rules for making a word problem harder without changing the core multiplication. Your rules should work for any word problem, not just the examples you have tried.
Think about all the tricks you have seen. Can you organise them into a list from least to most difficult?
A mathematician's response

My rules, from least to most difficult:

1. Add extraneous information that is clearly unrelated (like a person's age). This is easy to spot.

2. Add a hidden step that uses a different operation (multiply then add, or multiply then divide). This changes the calculation.

3. Add extraneous information that uses the same operation as the main calculation (a number that looks like it should be multiplied). This is hard to spot.

4. Combine a hidden step with extraneous info that looks like the hidden step. This is the hardest because the solver must decide which numbers matter and which do not.

I first tried ordering by number size, but realised bigger numbers do not actually make a problem harder to understand. What makes it harder is the reasoning needed to decide which calculation to do.

Mathematician's thinking: You are generalising. That means you are making a set of rules that works for all word problems, not just the ones you have tried.
