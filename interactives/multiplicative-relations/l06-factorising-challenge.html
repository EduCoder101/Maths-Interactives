Explicit Instruction
Two strategies for harder multiplications

Part A: The four-section area model

34 × 27

Split both numbers by place value: 34 = 30 + 4 and 27 = 20 + 7.

This gives us four sections:

1.Top-left: 30 × 20 = 600
2.Top-right: 4 × 20 = 80
3.Bottom-left: 30 × 7 = 210
4.Bottom-right: 4 × 7 = 28
5.Total: 600 + 80 + 210 + 28 = 918

Part B: Factorising to simplify

Sometimes we can avoid the full area model by breaking a number into its factors. This is called factorising.

Example 1: 12 × 250

1.Think: 12 = 4 × 3
2.Rewrite: 4 × 3 × 250
3.Rearrange: 4 × 250 × 3 = 1,000 × 3 = 3,000

Example 2: 15 × 24

1.Think: 15 = 3 × 5
2.Rewrite: 3 × 5 × 24
3.Calculate: 5 × 24 = 120, then 3 × 120 = 360
Connection

Factorising is just another way of partitioning. The area model partitions by place value. Factorising partitions by factors. Both break a hard multiplication into easier steps.

When does factorising work?

Factorising works when a number has convenient factors. Prime numbers like 23 cannot be usefully factorised, because their only factors are 1 and 23. Use the area model or algorithm instead.

Go Deeper: Extension Tasks

You have mastered factorising to simplify multiplication. These tasks will help you explore deeper mathematical thinking: specialising, conjecturing, and generalising.

Task 1: Specialising
Try factorising these three multiplications in at least two different ways each: 36 × 15, 48 × 25, 72 × 14. For each one, which way of factorising made the calculation easiest?
Think about which factors created the simplest intermediate steps.
Sample response

36 × 15 = 540

(a) 36 × 5 × 3 = 180 × 3 = 540

(b) 36 × 3 × 5 = 108 × 5 = 540

(c) 4 × 9 × 15 = 60 × 9 = 540

All three work, but (c) might be easiest if you know that 9 × 15 = 135 and then 4 × 135 = 540.

48 × 25 = 1200

(a) 48 × 5 × 5 = 240 × 5 = 1200

(b) 12 × 4 × 25 = 12 × 100 = 1200 (easiest!)

Method (b) is best because 4 × 25 = 100, which is a landmark number.

72 × 14 = 1008

(a) 72 × 7 × 2 = 504 × 2 = 1008

(b) 72 × 2 × 7 = 144 × 7 = 1008

Both work reasonably well. (a) is perhaps easier because 2 is the simplest multiplier.

Mathematician's insight: The best factorisation often depends on which intermediate products you find easiest to calculate. There is no single "right" answer if you've factorised correctly and arrived at the correct final answer.
Task 2: Conjecturing
"Factorising always makes multiplication easier than doing it the normal way." Is this always true, sometimes true, or never true?
Give an example to support your answer.
Sample response

This is sometimes true.

Factorising is only helpful when a number has convenient factors. If a number is prime, or if its factors don't create simpler intermediate calculations, then factorising might not help at all.

Example where factorising helps:

48 × 25 = 12 × 4 × 25 = 12 × 100 = 1200. The landmark number 100 makes this much easier than calculating 48 × 25 directly using the area model.

Example where factorising doesn't help:

47 × 13. Both 47 and 13 are prime numbers. They cannot be factorised into smaller factors (except 1 and themselves). So factorising is useless here. You would have to use the area model or column multiplication: 47 × 13 = 611.

Mathematician's insight: Factorising is a strategic tool. You choose to use it only when it genuinely simplifies the problem. The best mathematicians know when to apply which strategy, rather than applying the same method to every problem.
Task 3: Generalising
When you multiply by 25, there is a shortcut using factorising. Try: 16 × 25, 32 × 25, 48 × 25, 84 × 25. Can you write a rule that always works for multiplying any number by 25?
Hint: What is 25 related to? What does 4 have to do with it?
Sample response

The pattern:

16 × 25 = 4 × 100 = 400

32 × 25 = 8 × 100 = 800

48 × 25 = 12 × 100 = 1200

84 × 25 = 21 × 100 = 2100

The rule:

n × 25 = (n ÷ 4) × 100

Why it works:

25 = 100 ÷ 4. So n × 25 = n × 100 ÷ 4 = (n ÷ 4) × 100. Divide by 4, then multiply by 100.

What about numbers that aren't divisible by 4?

Example: 15 × 25.

Using the rule: 15 ÷ 4 = 3.75, then 3.75 × 100 = 375. This works mathematically.

But in Year 5, you might prefer to split it: 15 × 25 = (12 × 25) + (3 × 25) = 300 + 75 = 375.

Mathematician's insight: This rule works for all positive numbers, not just ones divisible by 4. When you spot a pattern, you can test it by writing a general rule (using n for "any number"). The best generalisation works for every case, not just the examples you tried.
