Learning Intention and Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning to use the formal multiplication algorithm, understanding why each step works.

Success Criteria

  • I can set up and solve a multiplication algorithm for numbers multiplied by a one-digit number.
  • I can solve the algorithm for 2-digit and 3-digit numbers multiplied by 2-digit numbers.
  • I can explain what each partial product row represents.
  • I can check my algorithm answer with a Radar estimate.

Key idea

The algorithm is a compressed version of the area model. Every step in the algorithm corresponds to something in the area model. Same maths, faster writing.

Go Deeper: Algorithm Investigations

These tasks push your thinking deeper: spotting errors, testing claims, and finding patterns.

Specialising
A student solved 347 × 8 using the algorithm. They got 2,876. The correct answer is 2,776. Find the error.
Work the algorithm yourself first, then compare.
A mathematician's response

I worked 347 × 8 step by step:

Ones: 8 × 7 = 56. Write 6, carry 5.

Tens: 8 × 4 = 32, plus 5 = 37. Write 7, carry 3.

Hundreds: 8 × 3 = 24, plus 3 = 27. Answer: 2,776.

The student got 2,876. What if they carried 4 instead of 3? Then 24 + 4 = 28, giving 2,876. That is the error.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. That means you are tracing through a specific example step by step to find exactly where a mistake occurred.
Conjecturing
"When you multiply a 3-digit number by a 1-digit number, the answer is always a 4-digit number." Always, sometimes, or never true?
Test the claim with examples. Can you find a counterexample?
A mathematician's response

100 × 1 = 100 (3 digits). 999 × 9 = 8,991 (4 digits). 100 × 9 = 900 (3 digits).

The claim is sometimes true. Small 3-digit numbers with small multipliers give 3-digit answers.

Mathematician's thinking: You are conjecturing. Finding just one counterexample is enough to prove "always" is wrong.
Generalising
You have the digits 3, 5, 7, and 9. Use exactly three to make a 3-digit number, multiply by the remaining digit. Each digit used once. Find the arrangement giving the largest product.
Write a rule explaining how to arrange any digits to maximise the product.
A mathematician's response

With 9 as multiplier: 753 × 9 = 6,777.

With 7 as multiplier: 953 × 7 = 6,671. The first is larger.

Rule: Use the largest digit as the multiplier, arrange the rest in descending order.

Mathematician's thinking: You are generalising. You formed a rule that works for any set of digits, not just these four.
