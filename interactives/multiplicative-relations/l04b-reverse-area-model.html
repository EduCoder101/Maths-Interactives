Reversing the Area Model

Division hiding inside a multiplication model

Learning Intention & Success Criteria

▼

Learning Intention

We are learning to use the area model in reverse: given the total area and one side, find the missing side.

Success Criteria

  • I can identify when a problem requires reversing the area model.
  • I can choose a friendly chunk of the total that divides evenly by the known side.
  • I can work through the chunks step by step to find the missing side.
  • I can check my answer by multiplying to confirm the total.

Big idea

This is division hiding inside a multiplication model. When we know the total area and one side, finding the missing side is division. But we can use the same area model to work it out.

The big idea
Bidirectional thinking

The area model works both ways. Forward: you know both sides, you find the area. Reverse: you know the area and one side, you find the missing side. Same model. Different direction. This is how multiplication and division are connected.

The strategy

To reverse the area model: (1) find a friendly chunk of the total that divides evenly, (2) work out the remainder, (3) divide the remainder, (4) add the section widths together.

Challenge Tasks

You have completed the reverse area model. Now extend your understanding with three increasingly difficult thinking tasks.

Task 1: Specialising

Specialising Task
Two students reversed the area model for 252 ÷ 7:
Student A: 7 × 30 = 210, remainder 42, then 7 × 6 = 42. Missing side: 30 + 6 = 36
Student B: 7 × 20 = 140, remainder 112, then 7 × 10 = 70, remainder 42, then 7 × 6 = 42. Missing side: 20 + 10 + 6 = 36
Both got the same answer. Try splitting 252 ÷ 7 in a third way.
Model Thinking

Third way: Start with 7 × 35 = 245, remainder 7, then 7 × 1 = 7. Missing side: 35 + 1 = 36

Or: Start with 7 × 25 = 175, remainder 77, then 7 × 11 = 77. Missing side: 25 + 11 = 36

Notice that all three ways give the same answer, 36. The missing side does not depend on which chunks you choose, as long as they divide evenly and sum to the total.

Mathematician's note: When you use different chunks, you are using the distributive property of division: (a + b) ÷ n = (a ÷ n) + (b ÷ n). As long as your chunks add up to 252 and each divides by 7, the section widths will always sum to 36.

Task 2: Conjecturing

Conjecture Task
When reversing the area model, starting with the biggest possible friendly chunk always gives fewer steps than starting with a smaller chunk.
Is this statement: Always true, sometimes true, or never true? Give an example to support your answer.
Model Thinking

The statement is SOMETIMES true.

Counterexample: Consider 372 ÷ 3.

If you start with the biggest possible friendly chunk, 3 × 100 = 300, you need 3 steps: 100, 20, 4.

But if you start with 3 × 120 = 360 (a smaller chunk than 100 would give), you only need 2 steps: 120, 4. Remainder is 12, and 3 × 4 = 12.

So a smaller chunk can be more efficient! The trick is to choose chunks that divide evenly AND are friendly in size, not just the biggest possible chunk.

Mathematician's note: Efficiency in chunking depends on both divisibility and how quickly you can reach zero. A chunk like 120 is "friendlier" than 100 even though it's larger, because 372 - 360 = 12 is easier to divide by 3 than 372 - 300 = 72.

Task 3: Justifying

Justifying Task
Does it matter which friendly chunks you choose when reversing the area model? Could you ever get a WRONG answer by choosing different chunks?
Justify your reasoning using the distributive property of division.
Model Thinking

You can NEVER get a wrong answer.

As long as:

  • Every chunk divides evenly by the width
  • The chunks add up to the total area

Then the missing side will ALWAYS be the same.

Why? This is the distributive property of division over addition:

(a + b) ÷ n = (a ÷ n) + (b ÷ n)

When you split 252 into 210 + 42, you get (210 ÷ 7) + (42 ÷ 7) = 30 + 6 = 36

When you split 252 into 140 + 70 + 42, you get (140 ÷ 7) + (70 ÷ 7) + (42 ÷ 7) = 20 + 10 + 6 = 36

When you split 252 into 245 + 7, you get (245 ÷ 7) + (7 ÷ 7) = 35 + 1 = 36

Key insight: The distributive property guarantees that as long as your chunks are valid, your answer must be correct. The missing side is determined by the area and width alone, not by how you split the area into chunks.
