Card 5 of 11

The tricky cases

Why is 1 not prime?

Prime means exactly two factors. The number 1 has only one factor (itself). One factor is not two, so 1 is neither prime nor composite.

Why is 0 neither?

Every number is a factor of 0 (because 0 divided by anything is 0). This makes 0 a special case that does not fit either definition.

The only even prime

2 is the only even prime number. Every other even number has 2 as an additional factor, making it composite.

Card 6 of 11

Quick checks

Before testing every number, check these first:

Even?
If a number is larger than 2 and even, it is composite (divisible by 2).
Ends in 0 or 5?
If a number is larger than 5 and ends in 0 or 5, it is composite (divisible by 5).
Digit sum divisible by 3?
Add the digits. If the sum divides by 3, then the number is divisible by 3.

Let us classify 15 together

Think first. Is it even? Does it end in 0 or 5? What are its factors?

Answer

Ends in 5, so divisible by 5. We have 15 = 3 x 5. So the factors are 1, 3, 5, 15. That is four factors, so 15 is composite.

Card 8 of 11

Let us classify 23 together

Is it even? Ends in 0 or 5? What about the digit sum?

Answer

Not even. Does not end in 0 or 5. Digit sum is 2 + 3 = 5 (not divisible by 3). Testing 2, 3, 4... none divide evenly into 23. So 23 has only two factors: 1 and 23. 23 is prime.

Card 9 of 11

Let us classify 51 together

This one is tricky. It looks prime but is it?

Answer

Not even. Does not end in 0 or 5. But the digit sum is 5 + 1 = 6, which is divisible by 3. So 51 must be divisible by 3. We have 51 ÷ 3 = 17. So 51 = 3 x 17. The factors are 1, 3, 17, 51. 51 is composite.

Why this matters

This is why we check divisibility rules. 51 tricks many people because it is odd and does not end in 0 or 5.

Task 1: Specialising

Test a student's claim about primes

Task 1: Specialising
A student claims that 51 is prime because it is odd and does not end in 0 or 5. Is the student correct?
Check by testing whether 51 has any factors other than 1 and itself. Try dividing 51 by 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, and 7.
Model Response

Let me test each division:

51 ÷ 2 = 25.5 (not a whole number)

51 ÷ 3 = 17 (this works!)

I do not need to keep testing. I found that 51 = 3 × 17. So 51 has factors: 1, 3, 17, 51. That is four factors, so 51 is COMPOSITE, not prime.

The student's rule is wrong. Not all odd numbers that do not end in 0 or 5 are prime. Just because a number is odd does not mean it is prime.

The Lesson: When you find even one factor other than 1 and the number itself, you know it is composite. You do not need to test all numbers.

Task 2: Conjecturing

Test a rule about even numbers

Task 2: Conjecturing
"Every even number greater than 2 is composite."
Is this always true, sometimes true, or never true? Write down your thinking. Then test it with some examples.
Model Response

Let me think about this carefully.

If a number is even and greater than 2, it must be divisible by 2. That means 2 is a factor.

But also, the number itself is a different factor (it is not equal to 2).

And of course 1 is always a factor.

So every even number greater than 2 has at least three factors: 1, 2, and itself. That means it cannot be prime.

Let me test:

4 = 2 × 2 (composite)

6 = 2 × 3 (composite)

8 = 2 × 4 (composite)

100 = 2 × 50 (composite)

This rule is ALWAYS true. Every even number greater than 2 is composite.

The Lesson: 2 is the only even prime number. All other even numbers have 2 as a factor, so they cannot be prime.

Task 3: Generalising

Explore gaps between primes

Task 3: Generalising
Can you find two consecutive numbers (numbers next to each other, like 14 and 15) where both are composite? Can you find three pairs? Is it possible to find three consecutive composite numbers?
Try to write a rule about when prime numbers appear as numbers get bigger.
Model Response

Let me search for consecutive composites:

Pair 1: 8, 9 (because 8 = 2 × 4 and 9 = 3 × 3)

Pair 2: 14, 15 (because 14 = 2 × 7 and 15 = 3 × 5)

Pair 3: 24, 25 (because 24 = 2 × 12 and 25 = 5 × 5)

Easy! Now can I find three consecutive composites?

Triple 1: 24, 25, 26

Let me check: 24 = 2 × 12, 25 = 5 × 5, 26 = 2 × 13. Yes, all composite!

Triple 2: 32, 33, 34 also works.

The Rule I Found: As numbers get bigger, the gaps between consecutive prime numbers get larger. This means longer runs of composite numbers appear. But primes never completely stop appearing. Even very large numbers have primes among them.

The Lesson: Primes become rarer as numbers get bigger, but they never completely disappear. This is one of the great mysteries in mathematics.
