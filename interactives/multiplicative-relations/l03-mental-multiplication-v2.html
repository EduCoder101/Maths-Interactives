Learning Intention and Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning to use mental strategies to multiply efficiently, including doubling and halving and near multiples.

Success Criteria

  • I can multiply any number by 10, 100, or 1,000 by shifting place value.
  • I can estimate a product by rounding to a friendly multiple of 10 or 100.
  • I can use doubling and halving to solve multiplication problems mentally.
  • I can use the near multiples strategy to solve problems involving numbers close to a multiple of 10.

The big idea

Mental multiplication is about looking at the numbers first. Before you start calculating, ask yourself two questions:

Is one number even?

Try doubling and halving. Halve one number, double the other. The product stays the same.

Is one number close to a multiple of 10?

Try near multiples. Round to the friendly number, then adjust.

Is one number a multiple of 10, 100, or 1,000?

Use place value shifting. Multiply the non-zero parts, then shift digits left.

Card 4 of 13

Part A: Multiplying by 10, 100, 1,000

When we multiply by 10, every digit shifts one place to the left. By 100, two places. By 1,000, three places.

Common misconception

Students often say "add a zero". But the digit is moving, not gaining a zero. This matters later with decimals: 2.4 × 10 = 24, not 2.40.

What about multiples of 10 or 100? Break them apart:

Ex 17 × 20
= 7 × 2 × 10
= 14 × 10
= 140
Ex 26 × 300
= 6 × 3 × 100
= 18 × 100
= 1,800

Part B: Doubling and Halving

The key idea: if you halve one number and double the other, the product stays the same.

Ex 118 × 5
Halve 18 → 9. Double 5 → 10
New problem: 9 × 10 = 90
Ex 224 × 5
Halve 24 → 12. Double 5 → 10
New problem: 12 × 10 = 120
Ex 314 × 50
Halve 14 → 7. Double 50 → 100
New problem: 7 × 100 = 700
Pattern

Multiplying by 5 is always the same as halving and multiplying by 10. Multiplying by 50 is halving and multiplying by 100.

Part C: Near Multiples

When one number is close to a multiple of 10, round it, multiply, then adjust.

Ex 119 × 6, because 19 is one less than 20
20 × 6 = 120
Subtract one group of 6: 120 − 6 = 114
Ex 221 × 7, because 21 is one more than 20
20 × 7 = 140
Add one group of 7: 140 + 7 = 147
Ex 329 × 4, because 29 is one less than 30
30 × 4 = 120
Subtract one group of 4: 120 − 4 = 116
Connection

This is the same idea as compensation from the Additive Relations unit. You adjust to a friendly number, then correct. Same thinking, new operation.

Go Deeper: Mental Strategy Investigations

These tasks will push your thinking beyond finding answers. You will explore, make conjectures, and reason like a mathematician.

Exploring
When you multiply two numbers, does the order matter? Try these pairs and see what you notice.
12 × 8 and 8 × 12. Then try 25 × 4 and 4 × 25. What happens when you use the doubling and halving strategy? Does it matter which number you halve?
A mathematician's response

Order does not matter with multiplication. 12 × 8 and 8 × 12 both equal 96. Same with the second pair: 25 × 4 = 100 and 4 × 25 = 100.

With doubling and halving, it does matter which number you halve, only in the sense that you need one number to be even. If I do 12 × 8, I can halve the 12 to get 6 × 16 = 96, or halve the 8 to get 12 × 4 = 48. Wait, that second one is wrong. Let me recalculate. If I halve 8, I double the other: 24 × 4 = 96. Yes, both work!

The strategy is flexible. You just need to pick which number to halve and which to double based on what makes the calculation easier.

Mathematician's thinking: This is commutativity in action. One of the most useful properties in maths. Knowing that order doesn't matter lets you rearrange the problem to make it easier.
Pattern hunting
Look at these multiplications by 5: 6 × 5, 8 × 5, 12 × 5, 14 × 5.
Find the answers using the doubling and halving strategy. What do you notice about the last digit of each answer? Can you make a rule about what the last digit will always be when you multiply by 5?
A mathematician's response

6 × 5 = 30, 8 × 5 = 40, 12 × 5 = 60, 14 × 5 = 70. The last digits are 0, 0, 0, 0. They're all zero!

Why? Because 5 × 2 = 10. Whenever we halve an even number and multiply by 5, we are really multiplying by something and then multiplying by 10. And multiplying by 10 always ends in 0.

But wait. What if the number being multiplied by 5 is odd? Let's try: 7 × 5 = 35, 9 × 5 = 45, 11 × 5 = 55. The last digits are 5, 5, 5. When you multiply an odd number by 5, the answer always ends in 5!

My rule: Even number times 5 equals a number ending in 0. Odd number times 5 equals a number ending in 5.

Mathematician's thinking: You found a pattern and turned it into a rule. That's a core mathematical skill. You can use this rule to check your multiplication answers quickly.
Generalising
You have the digits 2, 4, 6, and 8. Each digit must be used exactly once. Arrange them into two 2-digit numbers and multiply them together.
Find the arrangement that gives the largest product. Then find the arrangement that gives the smallest product. Can you write a rule for how to arrange any set of 4 digits to get the largest product?
A mathematician's response

I started by trying some arrangements. 82 × 64 = 5,248. 86 × 42 = 3,612. Big difference! The first one is much larger.

I tried to think about why. In 82 × 64, the two biggest digits (8 and 6) are in the tens places. In 86 × 42, both 8 and 6 ended up in the same number, leaving the other number small.

I tested 84 × 62 = 5,208. Close, but 82 × 64 = 5,248 is still bigger. The trick is that the smaller remaining digits go in the ones place of the larger tens digit. So 8 pairs with 2, and 6 pairs with 4.

For the smallest: 26 × 48 = 1,248. The two smallest digits in the tens places, and each paired with the largest remaining digit.

My rule: To make the largest product from 4 digits, put the two largest digits in the tens columns of each number. Then put the smallest remaining digit with the largest tens digit. This spreads the value across both numbers as evenly as possible, which makes the product larger.

Mathematician's thinking: You are generalising. That means you are making a rule that works every time, not just for the examples you tried. A good rule explains why it works, not just what to do.
