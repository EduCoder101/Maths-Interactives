Partitioning with multiples

To divide a larger number, peel off known multiples of the divisor one chunk at a time.

Example: 348 ÷ 4

Radar first: 360 ÷ 4 = 90. The answer should be near 90.

Step 1: Find a large multiple of 4 that fits inside 348.

4 x 80 = 320. That fits.

Step 2: Subtract to find what is left.

348 - 320 = 28

Step 3: Divide the leftover.

4 x 7 = 28. That fits exactly.

Step 4: Add the partial quotients.

80 + 7 = 87

Check: 4 x 87 = 348. Correct. Close to our Radar of 90.

Equivalent division

Look at these three divisions:

12 ÷ 4 = 3

24 ÷ 8 = 3

6 ÷ 2 = 3

They all give the same quotient. Why?

The rule

If you multiply or divide both the dividend and the divisor by the same number, the quotient stays the same. From 12 ÷ 4, doubling both gives 24 ÷ 8. Halving both gives 6 ÷ 2. The quotient is always 3.

Think of it as sharing: 12 objects among 4 groups gives 3 each. If you double both the objects and the groups, each group still gets 3.

Justifying

A mathematician's response

I think this is never true as the only option. Let me find a counterexample.

Start with 15 ÷ 5 = 3. Multiply both by 3: 45 ÷ 15 = 3. That is equivalent, and I multiplied by 3, not 2. So the student is wrong.

I also tried multiplying by 10: 150 ÷ 50 = 3. Still works. And dividing both by 5: 3 ÷ 1 = 3. Also works.

The actual rule: you can multiply or divide both the dividend and the divisor by any number (not just 2) and the quotient stays the same. The student's claim is sometimes true (multiplying by 2 does work), but it is not the whole story.
