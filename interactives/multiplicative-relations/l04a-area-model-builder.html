The Area Model

Understanding how the area model reveals the structure inside multiplication

Learning Intention & Success Criteria

▼

Learning Intention

We are learning to use the area model to partition and multiply 2- and 3-digit numbers by a one-digit number.

Success Criteria

  • I can draw and label an area model to represent a multiplication.
  • I can partition a number into hundreds, tens, and ones and multiply each part separately.
  • I can add the partial products to find the final answer.
  • I can explain how the area model connects to the formal algorithm.
The Hook
The Tiled Floor Problem

The school hall

The school hall is 24 metres long and 6 metres wide. How many square metres of tiles are needed?

Most of you would try 24 × 6 straight away. But what if we could see what this looks like as a shape?

24
20
20 × 6= 120
4
4 × 6= 24
6
Total: 120 + 24 = 144 square metres

The distributive property

What we just did has a formal name: the distributive property.

We distributed the multiplication across a sum:

6 × 24 = 6 × (20 + 4)
= 120 + 24
= 144

Instead of one hard multiplication, we get two easy ones.

Key vocabulary

Each smaller product (120 and 24) is called a partial product. We add all the partial products to get the final product.

The formal algorithm

Written algorithm   4 7 ×  8 3 7 6

The answer is 376. But why does this work? What is actually happening at each step?

Discussion

How could you work out 47 × 8 without using the formal algorithm? What if you broke 47 apart into friendlier numbers?

40

7

Total: 320 + 56 = 376
5
Connecting the two
The connection

The algorithm is a compressed version of the area model. In the algorithm, when you multiply 8 × 7 = 56, that is the right-hand section of the area model. When you multiply 8 × 40 = 320, that is the left-hand section. The algorithm just writes it more compactly.

Algorithm   4 7 ×  8 3 7 6
8 × 7 = 56 → right box
8 × 40 = 320 → left box

The connection
Algorithm = compressed area model

In the algorithm, multiplying the ones digit (4 × 6 = 24) gives the right box. Multiplying the tens (4 × 30 = 120) gives the middle box. Multiplying the hundreds (4 × 200 = 800) gives the left box. Same maths, different layout.

Estimation check

We estimated 960. Our answer is 944. The difference is only 16. Radar confirmed.

Task 1: Specialising

Your Challenge
A student split 47 × 8 into (40 × 8) + (7 × 8) and got:
40 × 8 = 360
7 × 8 = 56
Total = 416
Is the area model correct? Find and explain any error.
Model Answer

The error is in the first partial product. 40 × 8 = 320, not 360. You might have multiplied 45 × 8 by mistake, or added 40 + 8 instead of multiplying them.

The correct calculation is:

40 × 8 = 320
7 × 8 = 56
Total = 320 + 56 = 376
Mathematician's Tip: Always double-check each partial product. A small multiplication error gets carried through to the final answer!

Task 2: Conjecturing

Your Challenge
"Splitting a number into more parts in the area model always gives a different final answer."
Is this statement Always true, Sometimes true, or Never true? Use 47 × 8 to show your thinking.
Model Answer

Never true. The total is always the same regardless of how you partition the numbers. Here's why:

Let me try 47 × 8 three different ways:

(40 + 7) × 8 = (40 × 8) + (7 × 8) = 320 + 56 = 376

(30 + 17) × 8 = (30 × 8) + (17 × 8) = 240 + 136 = 376

(20 + 20 + 7) × 8 = (20 × 8) + (20 × 8) + (7 × 8) = 160 + 160 + 56 = 376

Every way gives 376! The partition doesn't matter. Only the original numbers (47 and 8) determine the answer.

Big Idea: This is because multiplication is distributive. 47 × 8 equals the same thing no matter how you split 47.

Task 3: Justifying

Your Challenge
A student says that 99 × 7 is easier to solve with the area model by splitting it as (100 - 1) × 7 instead of (90 + 9) × 7.
Do you agree? Justify your reasoning by showing the calculation both ways.
Model Answer

I agree that the first method is easier. Both ways give the same answer, but (100 - 1) × 7 is faster for mental maths.

Method 1: (100 - 1) × 7

(100 - 1) × 7 = (100 × 7) - (1 × 7) = 700 - 7 = 693

Method 2: (90 + 9) × 7

(90 + 9) × 7 = (90 × 7) + (9 × 7) = 630 + 63 = 693

Both equal 693, but Method 1 is faster because 100 × 7 is easier to calculate mentally than 90 × 7. For numbers close to 100 or 1000, this trick is very handy!

Mathematician's Tip: Look for numbers that are close to a "friendly" number like 10, 100, or 1000. Splitting using complementary subtraction can be faster than addition.
