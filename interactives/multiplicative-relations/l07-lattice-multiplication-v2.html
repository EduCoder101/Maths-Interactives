Learning Intention

We are learning to use the lattice method to multiply two-digit numbers, and explain how it connects to place value.

Success Criteria

  • I can set up a lattice grid correctly for a 2-digit by 2-digit multiplication.
  • I can fill in the diagonal cells and add along the diagonals correctly.
  • I can explain what each diagonal strip represents in place value terms.
  • I can check my lattice answer using a Radar estimate.

Setting Up: 47 × 38

Follow along step by step.

1. Draw a 2 × 2 grid. Write 4 and 7 along the top. Write 3 and 8 down the right side.
2. Draw a diagonal line from top-right to bottom-left through each cell. This splits each cell into a tens triangle (top-left) and an ones triangle (bottom-right).
3a. Top-left cell: 4 × 3 = 12. Write 1 above the diagonal, 2 below.
3b. Top-right cell: 7 × 3 = 21. Write 2 above, 1 below.
3c. Bottom-left cell: 4 × 8 = 32. Write 3 above, 2 below.
3d. Bottom-right cell: 7 × 8 = 56. Write 5 above, 6 below.
4. Add along diagonals from bottom-right: 6, then 2 + 5 + 1 = 8, then 3 + 2 + 2 = 7, then 1. Read the answer: 1,786.

Why the Diagonals Work

Place Value Connection

Each diagonal strip corresponds to a place value column. The bottom-right diagonal is ones, the next is tens, then hundreds, then thousands.

This is why the lattice avoids regrouping errors - the place value alignment is built into the grid.

The lattice, area model, and standard algorithm are all the same maths arranged differently. The lattice just handles the place value automatically through its diagonals.

Go Deeper

Three tasks to strengthen your mathematical thinking about the lattice method.

Task 1: Specialising
A student used the lattice method to multiply 68 × 47. They got all the cell products right, but when they added the diagonals, they got 2,196 instead of 3,196. Find the diagonal addition error.
Hint: Which diagonal passes a value to the next diagonal?
Model Response

Let me work through the lattice grid for 68 × 47.

First, the cell products:

  • 6 × 4 = 24 (tens = 2, ones = 4)
  • 8 × 4 = 32 (tens = 3, ones = 2)
  • 6 × 7 = 42 (tens = 4, ones = 2)
  • 8 × 7 = 56 (tens = 5, ones = 6)

Now the diagonals (reading from bottom-right to top-left):

  • Ones diagonal: 6
  • Tens diagonal: 2 + 2 + 5 = 9 (no regrouping)
  • Hundreds diagonal: 4 + 3 + 4 = 11 → write 1, rename 1
  • Thousands diagonal: 2 + 1 (renamed) = 3

The correct answer is 3,196. The student got 2,196, which means they wrote 2 in the thousands place instead of 3. They forgot to add the renamed value from the hundreds diagonal. This is the most common mistake when using the lattice method!

Why this matters: The lattice method still relies on regrouping between place values. We need to keep track of that regrouping even though we're using a different layout. It helps to mark the renamed value clearly as you write it.
Task 2: Conjecturing
In a lattice multiplication, swapping the two numbers (for example, doing 46 × 73 instead of 73 × 46) gives a different pattern in the grid but should give the same final answer. Is this: Always true, Sometimes true, or Never true?
Test your conjecture with at least one example.
Model Response

Let me test this with 46 × 73 and 73 × 46.

For 46 × 73:

  • Cell products: 4 × 7 = 28, 4 × 3 = 12, 6 × 7 = 42, 6 × 3 = 18
  • Final answer: 3,358

For 73 × 46:

  • Cell products: 7 × 4 = 28, 7 × 6 = 42, 3 × 4 = 12, 3 × 6 = 18
  • Final answer: 3,358

The grid layout looks different, but all the same products appear (just in different positions), so the diagonal sums are the same. This is ALWAYS true.

Why this works: Multiplication is commutative, which means a × b = b × a. The lattice method is just a tool for organising our multiplying. No matter which order we put the numbers in, we get the same answer.
Task 3: Generalising
Look at the ones diagonal in any two-digit times two-digit lattice multiplication. The ones diagonal always contains just one number: the ones digit of the product of the two ones digits. Is this always true? Why or why not?
Test this with at least two examples before explaining your thinking.
Model Response

Let me test this with two examples.

Example 1: 46 × 73

  • Ones digits: 6 and 3
  • 6 × 3 = 18 → ones digit is 8
  • The answer 3,358 has a ones digit of 8 ✓

Example 2: 57 × 34

  • Ones digits: 7 and 4
  • 7 × 4 = 28 → ones digit is 8
  • The answer 1,938 has a ones digit of 8 ✓

This is ALWAYS true. Here's why: In a 2 × 2 lattice grid, the ones digit of the final answer comes only from the ones diagonal. The ones diagonal contains only the ones digit of the bottom-right cell, which is the product of the two ones digits. The tens values from other cells affect tens and higher places, not the ones place.

Big idea: The ones digit of any product depends only on the ones digits of the numbers being multiplied. This works for any multiplication, not just lattice method! For instance, 23 × 47 has the same ones digit as 3 × 7 because only the ones digits matter for the ones place.
