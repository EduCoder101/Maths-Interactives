The Division Radar

Estimation as a checking tool for division

Learning Intention & Success Criteria ▼

Learning Intention

We are learning to estimate the result of a division before we calculate, so we can judge whether our answer is reasonable.

Success Criteria

  • I can find a compatible number close to the dividend that divides evenly by the divisor.
  • I can use that compatible number to make a quick estimate of the quotient.
  • I can judge whether a division answer is reasonable by comparing it to my estimate.

How to estimate a division

Before you calculate, find a compatible number close to the dividend that divides evenly. This gives you a quick estimate to check your answer against.

The Process

1. Think of multiples of the divisor near the dividend.
2. Pick the closest one. That gives your estimate.
3. Calculate and check: does your answer match your estimate?

Example 1: Estimate then check

Problem
78 ÷ 6

Step 1: Think of multiples of 6 near 78.

6 x 12 = 72
6 x 13 = 78

78 is right on a multiple of 6.

Step 2: Pick the compatible number.

78 is exactly 6 x 13

Estimate: 13

78 is an exact multiple, so this estimate should be spot on.

Step 3: Calculate and check.

78 ÷ 6 = 13
Radar Confirmed

Exactly 13. Our estimate matched perfectly.

Example 2: Larger number

Problem
246 ÷ 4

Step 1: Think of multiples of 4 near 246.

4 x 60 = 240
4 x 70 = 280

246 sits between 240 and 280, much closer to 240.

Step 2: Pick the closest compatible number.

240 is only 6 away from 246

Estimate: about 60

I rounded DOWN from 246 to 240, so the real answer is a little more than 60.

Step 3: Calculate and check.

246 ÷ 4 = 61 remainder 2
Radar Confirmed

61 is just above 60. Exactly what we predicted.

Example 3: Catching an error

Student's Answer
On the test paper
347 ÷ 6 = 97

Radar check: Find multiples of 6 near 347.

6 x 50 = 300
6 x 60 = 360

The answer should be between 50 and 60.

Estimate: about 58

Compare the student's answer to our estimate.

Student's answer: 97
Our estimate: about 58
Radar Alarm

97 is way above our estimate of 58. Something has gone wrong. We do not even need to check the working to know this answer is too large.

The correct answer:

347 ÷ 6 = 57 remainder 5

The student likely made a place value error in their calculation. Without a Radar estimate, this mistake could go unnoticed.

Go Deeper: Division Investigations

These tasks push your thinking beyond finding answers. You will explore, make conjectures, and reason like a mathematician.

Specialising
Estimate each of these divisions using compatible numbers (find a nearby number that divides evenly). Then calculate the exact answer. How close was each estimate?
156 ÷ 7     283 ÷ 4     497 ÷ 9
Which estimate was closest to the actual answer? Which was furthest away? Why do you think that happened?
A mathematician's response

I looked for multiples of each divisor that were close to the dividend.

156 ÷ 7: I know 7 x 22 = 154. That is only 2 away from 156. So my estimate was 22. The actual answer is 22 remainder 2. Spot on.

283 ÷ 4: I know 4 x 70 = 280. That is only 3 away. Estimate: 70. Actual: 70 remainder 3. Very close.

497 ÷ 9: I know 9 x 55 = 495. That is only 2 away. Estimate: 55. Actual: 55 remainder 2. Spot on again.

All three estimates were extremely close because the compatible numbers I found happened to be very near the dividends. The trick is knowing your times tables well enough to spot those nearby multiples quickly.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. That means you are trying specific examples to gather evidence and notice what makes an estimate better or worse.
Conjecturing
"If the dividend increases by exactly the divisor, the quotient always increases by exactly 1."
Is this always true, sometimes true, or never true? Test it with at least three different examples.
A mathematician's response

I tested this with several examples.

47 ÷ 6 = 7 remainder 5. Now add 6 to the dividend: 53 ÷ 6 = 8 remainder 5. Quotient went from 7 to 8. Up by 1.

100 ÷ 7 = 14 remainder 2. Add 7: 107 ÷ 7 = 15 remainder 2. Up by 1.

55 ÷ 8 = 6 remainder 7. Add 8: 63 ÷ 8 = 7 remainder 7. Up by 1.

I started to think it was always true, but then I stopped and thought about why. Adding the divisor to the dividend means you are sharing one more "full group". The remainder stays the same because the extra amount is exactly one full group. So the quotient always increases by exactly 1.

My answer: always true.

Mathematician's thinking: You are conjecturing. That means you are making a claim you believe is true, based on evidence, and then trying to explain why it works every time.
Justifying
A student estimates 347 ÷ 6 like this: "I will round 347 to 350 and round 6 to 10. Then 350 ÷ 10 = 35. My estimate is 35."
The actual answer is 57 remainder 5. What went wrong with the student's strategy? Explain why rounding the divisor caused such a big error.
A mathematician's response

The student's estimate of 35 is very far from the actual answer of 57 remainder 5. That is off by more than 22. Let me figure out what went wrong.

The student rounded the divisor from 6 to 10. That looks like a small change, but it changes everything. Dividing by 10 gives a much smaller quotient than dividing by 6, because you are sharing among more groups.

I first tried rounding only the dividend: 348 ÷ 6 = 58. That estimate is only 1 away from the actual quotient. Much better.

The lesson: never round the divisor. A small change to the divisor causes a huge change in the quotient. In multiplication you can sometimes round both numbers, but in division the divisor must stay exact. Only round the dividend.

Mathematician's thinking: You are justifying. That means you are not just saying the student is wrong. You are explaining why the strategy fails, so that everyone can avoid the same mistake.
