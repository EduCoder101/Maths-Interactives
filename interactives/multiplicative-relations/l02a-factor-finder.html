Lesson 2: Factor Finder

Find all the factor pairs of a number systematically

Learning Intention & Success Criteria ▼

Learning Intention

We are learning to find all the factor pairs of a whole number and use them to describe its structure.

Success Criteria

  • I can use the word product correctly to describe the result of a multiplication.
  • I can write a whole number as a product in at least two different ways.
  • I can find all the factor pairs of a given number systematically.
  • I can classify a number as prime, composite, or neither.

What are factor pairs?

When two numbers multiply to give a product, they are called factors of that product. Together they form a factor pair.

Example

The number 12 can be made from these factor pairs:

1 x 12 = 12
2 x 6 = 12
3 x 4 = 12

So the factors of 12 are: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12.

1 x 12
2 x 6
3 x 4

Each rectangle uses exactly 12 tiles, just arranged differently.

The systematic method

To find all factor pairs of a number, start at 1 and work upward.

Stopping Rule

Stop when the next number you would check has already appeared as the other half of a pair you found.

Finding all factor pairs of 24:

1 x 24 = 24

Does 1 go into 24? Yes. Factor pair: 1 and 24.

2 x 12 = 24

Does 2 go into 24? Yes. Factor pair: 2 and 12.

3 x 8 = 24

Does 3 go into 24? Yes. Factor pair: 3 and 8.

4 x 6 = 24

Does 4 go into 24? Yes. Factor pair: 4 and 6.

5 x ? = 24

Does 5 go into 24? No. Skip.

6 x 4 = 24 ← STOP

6 has already appeared in the pair "4 and 6". We have found them all.

Factors of 24: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 24. That is 8 factors.

Prime, composite, and neither

A prime number has exactly two factors: 1 and itself. There is only one rectangle possible.

A composite number has more than two factors. Multiple rectangles are possible.

Special Cases

0 is neither prime nor composite (division by 0 is undefined).
1 is neither prime nor composite (it has only one factor, not two).
2 is the only even prime number.

Go Deeper

Ready for a challenge? Explore the deeper patterns and logic behind factor pairs.

Task 1: Specialising
Find all the factor pairs of 36. Now find all the factor pairs of 48. Which number has more factor pairs? Can you find a number less than 50 that has even MORE factor pairs than either of these?
Work out the factor pairs first, then think about which number wins.
One way to think about it

Let me find the factor pairs:

Factor pairs of 36: 1 x 36, 2 x 18, 3 x 12, 4 x 9, 6 x 6

That means the factors are: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 9, 12, 18, 36. That is 9 factors altogether.

Factor pairs of 48: 1 x 48, 2 x 24, 3 x 16, 4 x 12, 6 x 8

The factors are: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 16, 24, 48. That is 10 factors.

So 48 has MORE factor pairs than 36.

Can we find a number less than 50 with more than 10 factors? Let me think about the highly composite numbers... 24 has 8 factors (1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 24). That is fewer than 48. 36 has 9 factors. Actually, 48 is already the number less than 50 with the most factors!

The insight: Some numbers are "highly composite" because they have many small factors. The more times a number can be broken into smaller factors, the more factor pairs it has.
Task 2: Conjecuring
A classmate claims: "A number with more factor pairs is always a bigger number." Is this always true, sometimes true, or never true? Find examples to support your answer.
Think about whether bigger numbers must have more factors.
One way to think about it

This is sometimes true, not always.

Let me test: 12 has factors 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12 (that is 6 factors). But 13 is bigger, and it only has 2 factors (1 and 13, since it is prime).

So 12 has MORE factor pairs than 13, even though 12 is SMALLER.

However, sometimes a bigger number does have more factors. For example, 48 (10 factors) is bigger than 5 (2 factors).

The insight: It depends on what the numbers are, not just their size. Prime numbers always have only 2 factors, no matter how big they are. Highly composite numbers can be smaller but still have many more factors.
Task 3: Justifying
A student claims: "Square numbers always have an odd number of factors." Is this true? Investigate by looking at the factor pairs of at least 3 square numbers and 3 non-square numbers.
A square number is one that can be made by multiplying a number by itself (like 16 = 4 x 4).
One way to think about it

Testing square numbers:

4 = 2 x 2 → Factors: 1, 2, 4. That is 3 factors (odd) ✓

9 = 3 x 3 → Factors: 1, 3, 9. That is 3 factors (odd) ✓

25 = 5 x 5 → Factors: 1, 5, 25. That is 3 factors (odd) ✓

36 = 6 x 6 → Factors: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 9, 12, 18, 36. That is 9 factors (odd) ✓

Testing non-square numbers:

6 → Factors: 1, 2, 3, 6. That is 4 factors (even)

8 → Factors: 1, 2, 4, 8. That is 4 factors (even)

12 → Factors: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12. That is 6 factors (even)

The claim is ALWAYS true!

Why? In a square number, one factor pairs with itself. For example: 6 x 6 = 36. All other factors come in pairs with different numbers. So we have pairs plus one "unpaired" factor in the middle, which makes the total odd. With non-square numbers, every factor pairs with a different number, so the total is always even.
