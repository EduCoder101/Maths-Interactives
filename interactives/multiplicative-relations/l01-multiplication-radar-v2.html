Learning Intention and Success Criteria

Learning Intention

We are learning to use estimation to judge whether a multiplication answer is reasonable before we calculate.

Success Criteria

  • I can round a number to its nearest 10 or 100 to make a quick estimate.
  • I can say whether a multiplication answer is reasonable by comparing it to my estimate.
  • I can multiply a one-digit number by 10, 100, and 1000 mentally.
Card 1 of 11

Spot the Problem

A teacher marked a test from another class and saw this answer:

Student's Answer
On the test paper 47 x 6 = 1,282

Before even checking the working, the teacher knew this answer was wrong. How?

Think About It

47 is close to 50. What is 50 x 6? Could the answer possibly be in the thousands?

The Reasoning

47 is close to 50. And 50 x 6 = 300. So the answer must be somewhere near 300. An answer of 1,282 is over four times too large. The student made a place value error, but you do not need to diagnose it to know it is wrong. A quick Radar check catches it instantly.

The Radar for Products

Mathematicians do not just calculate. They know roughly what the answer should be before they pick up a pen.

In multiplication, this is even more important than in addition and subtraction, because errors can be much larger. Today we are turning on our Radar for Products.

The Process

1. Estimate first (round to a friendly number).
2. Calculate.
3. Check: does my answer match my estimate?

Card 3 of 11

Example 1: Estimate then Calculate

Problem 38 x 7

Step 1: Round to a friendly number.

38 → 40

Estimate: 40 x 7 = 280

Step 2: Check direction.

I rounded up from 38 to 40, so my estimate is slightly too big. The real answer should be a little less than 280.

Step 3: Calculate and check.

38 x 7 = 266

266 is close to 280. Radar confirmed.

246 x 4 = 984

984 is close to 1,000. Reasonable.

Card 5 of 11

Example 3: Catching an Error

Student's Answer
On the test paper 46 x 7 = 282

Radar check: 50 x 7 = 350

The answer should be near 350, maybe a little less. But 282 is almost 70 below the estimate. That is suspicious.

Finding the error: The student calculated 7 x 6 = 42 correctly (writing 2, carrying 4). But then did 7 x 4 = 28 and forgot to add the carried 4. They wrote 28 instead of 32.

Correct answer: 322

The Radar caught the carry error. Without estimating first, this mistake could go unnoticed.
