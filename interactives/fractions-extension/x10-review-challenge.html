Learning Intention and Success Criteria ▼

Learning Intention

We are applying everything from the Fractions Extension across a mixed challenge.

Success Criteria

  • I can simplify, compare and convert fractions.
  • I can add, subtract, multiply and divide fractions and mixed numerals.
  • I can choose the right method for a worded problem.
  • I can explain and justify my reasoning.
Quick reminders ▼

No new skills here, just everything you have learned. Use these reminders if you get stuck, then take on the challenge levels.

Simplify: divide top and bottom by their highest common factor.
Compare: rewrite over a common denominator, then compare numerators.
Mixed numeral: divide the top by the bottom for the whole and remainder.
Add and subtract: common denominator first, then carry or borrow whole numbers.
Multiply: multiply the tops and multiply the bottoms.
Divide: multiply by the reciprocal (flip the second fraction).
Always give your answer in its simplest form, and write a mixed numeral if it is more than one whole.

The Challenge

Pick a level. Read the instruction above each question, work it out, and check to see the full working. Use 0 for the whole if the answer is less than one.

Go Deeper: Final Reasoning

These tasks pull your fraction thinking together. Explore, test claims, and reason like a mathematician.

Conjecturing
Pick any fraction, say 2/3. Multiply it by 3/4, then divide the result by 3/4.
What do you get back? Does this always happen? Make a claim and test it with another fraction.
A mathematician's response

2/3 × 3/4 = 1/2. Then 1/2 ÷ 3/4 = 1/2 × 4/3 = 4/6 = 2/3. I got back to 2/3.

My claim: multiplying then dividing by the same fraction takes you back to the start. I tested it with 1/5: 1/5 × 3/4 = 3/20, then 3/20 ÷ 3/4 = 3/20 × 4/3 = 12/60 = 1/5. It works again. Dividing undoes multiplying.

Mathematician's thinking: You are conjecturing. You made a claim from a pattern and tested it.
Justifying
Put these in order from smallest to largest, and explain how you decided: 2/3, 3/5, 5/8.
Can you decide using benchmarks before doing any common denominators?
A mathematician's response

All three are just over 1/2, so benchmarks alone are not enough. I used a common denominator of 120: 2/3 = 80/120, 3/5 = 72/120, 5/8 = 75/120.

So in order: 3/5 (72), then 5/8 (75), then 2/3 (80). The common denominator settled it cleanly when benchmarks could not.

Mathematician's thinking: You are justifying. You chose a method that proves the order beyond doubt.
Generalising
You have learned to multiply by a fraction (makes smaller) and divide by a fraction (makes bigger), when the fraction is less than one.
Write a clear rule about when an operation makes a number bigger or smaller, covering multiplying and dividing by fractions.
A mathematician's response

Multiplying by a number bigger than 1 makes a number bigger; multiplying by a number between 0 and 1 makes it smaller. Dividing is the opposite: dividing by a number bigger than 1 makes it smaller, and dividing by a number between 0 and 1 makes it bigger.

The dividing line is the number 1. Multiplying or dividing by exactly 1 leaves a number unchanged, because one whole group is the original amount.

Mathematician's thinking: You are generalising. You stated one rule covering many cases, with 1 as the key boundary.
