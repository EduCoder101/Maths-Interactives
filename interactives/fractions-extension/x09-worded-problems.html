Year 5 Fractions Extension

Multi-Step and Worded Problems

Choose and combine your fraction skills
Learning Intention and Success Criteria ▼

Learning Intention

We are learning to choose and combine fraction operations to solve worded and multi-step problems.

Success Criteria

  • I can decide which operation a problem needs.
  • I can break a multi-step problem into steps.
  • I can work carefully with common denominators and simplest form.
  • I can check whether my answer is sensible.
Learn this skill ▼

Worded problems do not tell you which operation to use, so read carefully. Words like "in total" suggest adding, "how much is left" suggests subtracting, "of" suggests multiplying, and "how many fit" or "shared between" suggests dividing. For multi-step problems, work out one step at a time and keep your answers in simplest form.

Worked example: a jug holds 34 of a litre. You pour out 13 of what is in the jug. How much is left?
1Find how much you pour out.
"1/3 of 3/4" means multiply: 1/3 × 3/4 = 3/12 = 1/4.
Split the 34 into 3 equal parts. Each part is 14.
2Take that away.
Pour out one part, 14.
3Work out what is left, and check.
3/4 − 1/4 = 2/4 = 1/2 of a litre is left.
Check: half the jug is left, which looks right for pouring out a third.
Always check

When you finish, ask if the answer makes sense. If you take part of something away, the answer must be smaller. If you join amounts, it must be larger. A quick sense check catches most mistakes.

Practice

Choose a level. Read carefully, work step by step, and give your answer in simplest form (use 0 for the whole if it is less than one).

Go Deeper: Problem-Solving Investigations

These tasks push your thinking beyond getting the answer. Explore, test claims, and reason like a mathematician.

Specialising
A pizza is shared so that the first person takes 1/2, the next takes 1/2 of what is left, the next takes 1/2 of what is left again.
How much does each person get? How much is left after three people? What pattern do you see?
A mathematician's response

First takes 1/2, leaving 1/2. Second takes 1/2 of 1/2 = 1/4, leaving 1/4. Third takes 1/2 of 1/4 = 1/8, leaving 1/8.

The shares are 1/2, 1/4, 1/8, each half the one before. The amount left also halves each time. After three people, 1/8 is left.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. You traced the steps to reveal a halving pattern.
Justifying
A problem says: "Sam ate 1/3 of a cake and Mia ate 1/4 of the cake. How much is left?" A student answers 1/2, saying "they ate about 1/2, so 1/2 is left".
Is the answer right? Work it out exactly and explain.
A mathematician's response

Eaten: 1/3 + 1/4 = 4/12 + 3/12 = 7/12. Left: 1 − 7/12 = 5/12.

So 5/12 is left, not 1/2. The estimate was close because 7/12 is just over a half, but the exact answer needs a common denominator. Estimating is a good check, but it is not the final answer.

Mathematician's thinking: You are justifying. You showed why the exact method beats a rough guess.
Generalising
In many "how much is left" problems you work out the parts used, add them, then subtract from one whole.
Write a general method for any "how much is left of one whole" problem, and say when the answer would be zero or negative.
A mathematician's response

My method: add all the parts used over a common denominator, then subtract that total from 1 (which is the whole). The result is what is left.

If the parts add to exactly 1, nothing is left, so the answer is 0. If the parts add to more than 1, then more than a whole was claimed, which is impossible, so the problem would not make sense. That is a useful check on the question itself.

Mathematician's thinking: You are generalising. You described a method for a whole family of problems and tested its limits.
