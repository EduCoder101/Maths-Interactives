Year 5 Fractions Extension

Comparing and Ordering Fractions

Compare any two fractions using a common denominator
Learning Intention and Success Criteria ▼

Learning Intention

We are learning to compare and order fractions with different denominators by rewriting them with a common denominator.

Success Criteria

  • I can find a common denominator for two fractions.
  • I can decide which of two fractions is larger, or whether they are equal.
  • I can put three or more fractions in order of size.
  • I can compare fractions to solve worded problems.
Learn this skill ▼

You cannot compare fractions fairly while the pieces are different sizes. The trick is to rewrite both fractions with the same denominator (a common denominator), so the pieces match. Then the fraction with more pieces is the larger one. A common denominator that always works is the two denominators multiplied together, but the smallest one is tidier.

Worked example: which is larger, 23 or 34?
1Find a common denominator.
The denominators are 3 and 4.
The smallest number both divide into is 12.
2Rewrite both with denominator 12.
23 = 812 (times top and bottom by 4)
34 = 912 (times top and bottom by 3)
On the bars, each one is now cut into 12 equal pieces.
3Compare the numerators.
9 pieces is more than 8 pieces.
So 34 is larger than 23.
Quick check

To compare with a benchmark, ask if each fraction is more or less than 12. A fraction is more than a half when the numerator is more than half the denominator. That often settles it without any rewriting.

Practice

Choose a level. Compare or order the fractions, then check to see the full working.

Go Deeper: Comparing Investigations

These tasks push your thinking beyond getting the answer. Explore, test claims, and reason like a mathematician.

Specialising
Compare each of these with one half: 3/5, 4/7, 5/9, 6/11.
Which are bigger than a half? What do you notice about the numerator and the denominator each time?
A mathematician's response

I compared each one with 1/2. For 3/5, half of 5 is 2.5, and 3 is more than that, so 3/5 is bigger than a half. The same happened for 4/7, 5/9 and 6/11: each numerator is just over half its denominator.

So every one is a little more than a half. I noticed a fraction beats 1/2 exactly when the numerator is more than half the denominator. That gives me a quick test without finding a common denominator.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. That means trying specific cases to spot a pattern before making a claim.
Justifying
A student says: "When two fractions have the same numerator, the one with the bigger denominator is the bigger fraction." For example, they claim 3/8 is bigger than 3/5.
Is this always, sometimes, or never true? Justify your answer.
A mathematician's response

I checked 3/8 and 3/5 with a common denominator of 40: 3/8 = 15/40 and 3/5 = 24/40. So 3/5 is actually the bigger one. The student has it backwards.

It makes sense: if the numerators are the same, you are taking the same number of pieces, but a bigger denominator means smaller pieces. Smaller pieces give a smaller amount. So for equal numerators the claim is never true. The bigger denominator always gives the smaller fraction.

Mathematician's thinking: You are justifying. You are not just finding the answer, you are explaining why the reasoning must hold every time.
Generalising
Find a way to compare a/b and c/d without writing out a common denominator.
Write a rule that always works, and explain why it works.
A mathematician's response

I tried 2/3 and 3/4. If I multiply across, 2 × 4 = 8 and 3 × 3 = 9. The second is bigger, and indeed 3/4 is bigger. I tested a few more and it kept working.

My rule: to compare a/b and c/d, compare a × d with c × b. The larger product belongs to the larger fraction. It works because that is exactly the numerators you would get if you rewrote both over the common denominator b × d, just without writing the denominator each time.

Mathematician's thinking: You are generalising. You found one rule that works for any two fractions, not only the examples you tried.
