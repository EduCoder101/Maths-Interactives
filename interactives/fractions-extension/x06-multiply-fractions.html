Year 5 Fractions Extension

Multiplying a Fraction by a Fraction

Use the area model to see why it works
Learning Intention and Success Criteria ▼

Learning Intention

We are learning to multiply two fractions, and to use the area model to understand why we multiply the numerators and the denominators.

Success Criteria

  • I can multiply two fractions by multiplying the tops and the bottoms.
  • I can use an area model to show what the answer means.
  • I can simplify the answer and handle a mixed numeral.
  • I can explain why multiplying by a fraction less than one makes the amount smaller.
Learn this skill ▼

The word "of" means multiply. To work out 23 of 34, picture one whole square. Shade 34 of it one way, and 23 of it the other way. The part shaded both ways is the answer.

Worked example: 23 × 34
1Shade the first fraction across.
Split the width into 3 and shade 2 columns: that is 23 across.
2Shade the second fraction down.
Split the height into 4 and shade 3 rows: that is 34 down.
The overlap, shaded twice, is the product.
3Count the cells.
Overlap: 2 columns by 3 rows = 6 cells. Whole square: 3 × 4 = 12 cells.
So 23 × 34 = 612 = 12.
The rule, and the size

Multiply the numerators and multiply the denominators: 2 × 3 = 6 over 3 × 4 = 12. The area model shows why the bottom multiplies: cutting both ways makes 12 small cells. Notice the answer is smaller than both fractions, because taking a fraction of a fraction takes only part of a part.

Go Deeper: Multiplying Investigations

These tasks push your thinking beyond getting the answer. Explore, test claims, and reason like a mathematician.

Specialising
Work out 1/2 × 1/2, then 1/2 × 1/3, then 1/2 × 1/4.
What happens to the answer as the second fraction gets smaller? Why?
A mathematician's response

1/2 × 1/2 = 1/4, 1/2 × 1/3 = 1/6, 1/2 × 1/4 = 1/8.

The answers get smaller: 1/4, 1/6, 1/8. That makes sense, because I am taking half of a smaller and smaller piece each time. Half of a small thing is tinier than half of a big thing. I also spotted that half of 1/n is 1/(2n).

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. You used ordered examples to see how the answer changes.
Justifying
A student thinks multiplying two fractions should make them bigger, the way adding does. They expect 1/2 × 1/2 to be about 1.
Is multiplying two fractions the same as adding them? Explain using a picture or the area model.
A mathematician's response

Adding gives 1/2 + 1/2 = 1. Multiplying gives 1/2 × 1/2 = 1/4. They are not the same at all.

The area model shows why: 1/2 × 1/2 means half OF a half. I shade half the square one way, then half the other way, and the overlap is just one of the four small squares, which is 1/4. Taking a part of a part makes things smaller, while adding joins amounts together.

Mathematician's thinking: You are justifying. You used a model to explain why multiplying and adding behave differently.
Generalising
Explain, using the area model, why a/b × c/d = (a × c)/(b × d).
Say what each multiplication on the right means in the picture.
A mathematician's response

I split the square into b columns one way and d rows the other way. That makes b × d small cells in total, which is the denominator.

I shade a of the columns and c of the rows. The overlap is a columns by c rows, so a × c cells, which is the numerator. So the answer is (a × c) cells out of (b × d).

Mathematician's thinking: You are generalising. You explained the rule with letters by linking each part to the picture.
