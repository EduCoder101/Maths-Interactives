Year 5 Fractions Extension

Adding and Subtracting Mixed Numerals

Combine fractions and mixed numerals with unlike denominators
Learning Intention and Success Criteria ▼

Learning Intention

We are learning to add and subtract fractions and mixed numerals with unlike denominators, using equivalence.

Success Criteria

  • I can find a common denominator before adding or subtracting.
  • I can add and subtract mixed numerals.
  • I can carry a whole when the parts make more than one, and borrow a whole when I need to.
  • I can give my answer as a mixed numeral in its simplest form.
Learn this skill ▼

To add or subtract mixed numerals, first give the fraction parts a common denominator so the pieces match. Then deal with the whole numbers and the fraction parts. Sometimes the parts make more than one whole, so you carry a whole. When subtracting, if the first part is too small, you borrow a whole and turn it into extra pieces.

Worked example: 234 + 112
1Give the parts a common denominator.
The denominators are 4 and 2. Use 4.
112 = 124
2Add the wholes, then the parts.
Wholes: 2 + 1 = 3.
Parts: 3/4 + 2/4 = 5/4.
3Carry the extra whole.
5/4 is 1 whole and 1/4, so carry one whole.
3 + 1 = 4 wholes, with 1/4 left. Answer: 414.
Borrowing when subtracting

For 314 − 112, the parts are 1/4 and 2/4. Since 1/4 is too small, borrow one whole from the 3: it becomes 2 wholes and 5/4. Now 5/4 − 2/4 = 3/4 and 2 − 1 = 1, giving 134.

Practice

Choose a level. Give every answer as a mixed numeral in its simplest form (use 0 for the whole if the answer is less than one).

Go Deeper: Mixed Numeral Investigations

These tasks push your thinking beyond getting the answer. Explore, test claims, and reason like a mathematician.

Specialising
Work out 1/2 + 1/3, then 1/3 + 1/4, then 1/4 + 1/5.
Look at each answer. Is there a quick pattern linking the two denominators to the answer?
A mathematician's response

1/2 + 1/3 = 5/6. 1/3 + 1/4 = 7/12. 1/4 + 1/5 = 9/20.

I noticed the bottom of each answer is the two denominators multiplied: 2 × 3 = 6, 3 × 4 = 12, 4 × 5 = 20. The top is the two denominators added: 2 + 3 = 5, 3 + 4 = 7, 4 + 5 = 9. So for two unit fractions, the answer is (sum of denominators) over (product of denominators).

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. You tried ordered examples to uncover a hidden pattern.
Justifying
A student adds 1 1/2 + 2 1/3 by writing 3 2/5 (they added the wholes, then added the tops and added the bottoms).
Is the answer right? Explain exactly what went wrong, and give the correct answer.
A mathematician's response

The wholes are fine: 1 + 2 = 3. The mistake is in the parts. The student did 1/2 + 1/3 = 2/5 by adding tops and bottoms, but that is not how fractions add.

The pieces are different sizes, so I must use a common denominator. 1/2 = 3/6 and 1/3 = 2/6, so 3/6 + 2/6 = 5/6. The correct answer is 3 5/6. A good check: 2/5 is less than a half, but adding two positive fractions should give more than either one, so 2/5 cannot be right.

Mathematician's thinking: You are justifying. You found the error, explained why it is wrong, and checked the result for sense.
Generalising
When you subtract two mixed numerals, sometimes you have to borrow a whole and sometimes you do not.
Write a rule that tells you, before you start, whether you will need to borrow.
A mathematician's response

I compared the fraction parts. In 3 3/4 − 1 1/4 the first part 3/4 is bigger than 1/4, so no borrowing. In 3 1/4 − 1 1/2 the first part 1/4 is smaller than 2/4, so I had to borrow.

My rule: put both fraction parts over a common denominator and compare them. If the first fraction part is smaller than the second, you must borrow a whole. If it is equal or larger, you do not. The whole numbers do not affect whether you borrow, only the parts do.

Mathematician's thinking: You are generalising. You found a test that decides every case before you calculate.
