Year 5 Fractions Extension

Multiplying a Fraction by a Whole Number

From repeated addition to a quick rule
Learning Intention and Success Criteria ▼

Learning Intention

We are learning to multiply a fraction by a whole number, and to understand why the rule works.

Success Criteria

  • I can see multiplying by a whole number as repeated addition.
  • I can multiply the numerator by the whole number and keep the denominator.
  • I can simplify the answer and write it as a mixed numeral.
  • I can solve worded problems that need this skill.
Learn this skill ▼

Multiplying a fraction by a whole number just means adding that fraction over and over. Three lots of 34 is 34 added three times. The shortcut is to multiply the numerator by the whole number and keep the same denominator. Then simplify and write it as a mixed numeral if it is more than one.

Worked example: 34 × 3
1Multiplying is repeated addition.
3/4 + 3/4 + 3/4, which is three groups of three quarters.
2Count the pieces, or use the rule.
There are 9 quarters in total, so 9/4.
Rule: multiply the numerator by the whole. 3 × 3 = 9, kept over 4.
3Write it as a mixed numeral.
9/4 = 214.
Watch the size

A whole number times a proper fraction can be smaller than the whole number. For example 23 × 4 is 83, which is 223, less than 4. Taking a fraction of something less than a whole each time keeps the total down.

Practice

Choose a level. Give every answer in its simplest form (use 0 for the whole if it is less than one).

Go Deeper: Multiplying Investigations

These tasks push your thinking beyond getting the answer. Explore, test claims, and reason like a mathematician.

Specialising
Work out 2/3 × 3, 3/4 × 4, and 2/5 × 5.
What happens when the whole number you multiply by is the same as the denominator?
A mathematician's response

2/3 × 3 = 6/3 = 2. 3/4 × 4 = 12/4 = 3. 2/5 × 5 = 10/5 = 2.

Each time the answer is a whole number, and it is exactly the numerator I started with. That makes sense: multiplying by the denominator gives the numerator on top and the denominator on the bottom, and a number over itself is one whole. So the whole number left is just the numerator.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. You tried chosen examples to expose a neat special case.
Justifying
A student says: "Multiplying always makes a number bigger, so 2/3 × 4 must be more than 4."
Is the student right? Justify your answer.
A mathematician's response

I worked it out: 2/3 × 4 = 8/3 = 2 2/3. That is less than 4, not more. So the student is wrong.

The idea "multiplying always makes bigger" only holds when you multiply by a number bigger than one. Here 2/3 is less than one whole, so taking 2/3 of 4 gives less than 4. Multiplying by a number between 0 and 1 always makes the result smaller.

Mathematician's thinking: You are justifying. You tested the claim and explained the condition under which it fails.
Generalising
Write a rule for a/b × n, and say exactly when the answer is a whole number.
Explain why your rule works.
A mathematician's response

My rule: a/b × n = (a × n)/b. You multiply the numerator by the whole number and keep the denominator. It works because n lots of a pieces is a × n pieces, each still a 1/b piece.

The answer is a whole number exactly when b divides into a × n with no remainder. For example with 3/4, I need 4 to divide 3n, which happens when n is a multiple of 4.

Mathematician's thinking: You are generalising. You turned the pattern into a rule with letters and stated exactly when a whole number appears.
