Year 5 Fractions Extension

Dividing by a Fraction

How many fit, and why we multiply by the reciprocal
Learning Intention and Success Criteria ▼

Learning Intention

We are learning to divide by a fraction by asking how many of it fit, and to see why multiplying by the reciprocal works.

Success Criteria

  • I can work out how many copies of a fraction fit into an amount.
  • I can divide by a fraction by multiplying by its reciprocal (its flip).
  • I can simplify the answer and write a mixed numeral.
  • I can explain why dividing by a fraction less than one makes the answer bigger.
Learn this skill ▼

Dividing asks "how many fit?". 34 ÷ 18 asks how many eighths fit into three quarters. The shortcut is to multiply by the reciprocal: flip the fraction you are dividing by, then multiply. Dividing by a fraction smaller than one makes the answer bigger, because lots of small pieces fit in.

Worked example: 34 ÷ 18
1Mark the size you are dividing by.
Mark eighths along the bar. The shaded part is 34, which is 68.
2Count how many fit.
Count the eighths inside the shaded part: there are 6.
3Check it with the reciprocal rule.
Flip 18 to 81, then multiply: 34 × 81 = 24/4 = 6.
Why the answer grows

Dividing by 18 is the same as multiplying by 8. Because the pieces are small, many of them fit, so the answer is larger than the amount you started with. Dividing by any fraction less than one makes the result bigger.

Practice

Choose a level. For Level 1 and 2 the answer is a whole number. Give other answers in simplest form.

Go Deeper: Dividing Investigations

These tasks push your thinking beyond getting the answer. Explore, test claims, and reason like a mathematician.

Specialising
Work out 1 ÷ 1/2, 1 ÷ 1/3, and 1 ÷ 1/4.
What do you notice about the answers? Why does that happen?
A mathematician's response

1 ÷ 1/2 = 2, 1 ÷ 1/3 = 3, 1 ÷ 1/4 = 4.

The answer is just the denominator each time. That is because I am asking how many 1/n pieces fit in one whole, and a whole is made of exactly n of them. So 1 ÷ 1/n = n.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. You tried examples to expose a clean pattern.
Justifying
A student says: "Dividing always makes a number smaller, so 6 ÷ 1/2 must be 3."
Is the student right? Justify your answer.
A mathematician's response

I asked how many halves fit into 6. Each whole holds 2 halves, so 6 wholes hold 12 halves. So 6 ÷ 1/2 = 12, not 3. The student halved instead of dividing.

The idea "dividing always makes smaller" is only true when you divide by a number bigger than one. Dividing by a fraction less than one makes the answer bigger, because many small pieces fit in.

Mathematician's thinking: You are justifying. You tested the claim and explained when it fails.
Generalising
Explain why a/b ÷ c/d is the same as a/b × d/c.
Use the "how many fit" idea to explain the flip.
A mathematician's response

Dividing by c/d asks how many c/d pieces fit. First I see how many 1/d pieces fit, which means multiplying by d. But the pieces are c of those each, so I have grouped them in cs, which means dividing by c.

Multiplying by d and dividing by c is the same as multiplying by d/c. So a/b ÷ c/d = a/b × d/c. That is the flip, and it always works.

Mathematician's thinking: You are generalising. You explained the reciprocal rule for any two fractions.
