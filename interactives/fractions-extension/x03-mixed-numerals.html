Year 5 Fractions Extension

Mixed Numerals and Improper Fractions

Convert both ways and place them on a number line
Learning Intention and Success Criteria ▼

Learning Intention

We are learning to convert between improper fractions and mixed numerals, and to place them on a number line.

Success Criteria

  • I can change an improper fraction into a mixed numeral.
  • I can change a mixed numeral into an improper fraction.
  • I can place a fraction greater than one on a number line.
  • I can use these conversions in worded problems.
Learn this skill ▼

An improper fraction has a numerator larger than its denominator, like 114. It is more than one whole. A mixed numeral writes the same amount as a whole number and a fraction, like 234. To convert, you work out how many whole groups fit inside the top number.

Worked example: write 114 as a mixed numeral
1How many whole groups of four quarters fit?
11 ÷ 4 = 2 remainder 3
2Read off the whole and the part.
That is 2 whole ones, with 3 quarters left over.
3Write the mixed numeral.
114 = 234, which sits between 2 and 3 on the line.
Going the other way

To turn a mixed numeral back into an improper fraction, multiply the whole number by the denominator and add the numerator. For 234: 2 × 4 = 8, then 8 + 3 = 11, giving 114.

Practice

Choose a level. Convert or place each one, then check to see the full working.

Go Deeper: Mixed Numeral Investigations

These tasks push your thinking beyond getting the answer. Explore, test claims, and reason like a mathematician.

Specialising
Write each of these as a mixed numeral: 7/2, 9/2, 11/2, 13/2.
What do you notice about the whole-number part as the numerator goes up by 2 each time?
A mathematician's response

I converted each: 7/2 = 3 and 1/2, 9/2 = 4 and 1/2, 11/2 = 5 and 1/2, 13/2 = 6 and 1/2.

The fraction part stays 1/2 every time, but the whole number goes up by 1 each step. That makes sense: adding 2 halves is adding one whole, so each new fraction is exactly one bigger than the last.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. That means trying ordered examples to reveal a pattern.
Justifying
A student says: "If the numerator is exactly double the denominator, the mixed numeral is always just the whole number 2 with no fraction left over." For example 8/4 = 2.
Is this always, sometimes, or never true? Justify your answer.
A mathematician's response

I tried 8/4: that is 8 ÷ 4 = 2, no remainder. Then 6/3 = 2, and 10/5 = 2. Every time the numerator is double the denominator, dividing gives exactly 2 with nothing left.

So the claim is always true. If the numerator is 2 lots of the denominator, then it splits into exactly 2 whole groups with remainder 0. The mixed numeral is just 2.

Mathematician's thinking: You are justifying. You explained why it must be true for every case, not just the examples.
Generalising
Write a rule for turning any mixed numeral w and a/b into an improper fraction.
Explain why the rule works, using the idea of whole ones being made of b parts each.
A mathematician's response

I thought about 2 and 3/4. Each whole one is 4 quarters, so 2 wholes is 8 quarters. Add the 3 extra quarters to get 11 quarters, which is 11/4.

My rule: the improper numerator is (w × b) + a, kept over the same denominator b. It works because every whole one is worth b of the parts, so w wholes give w × b parts, and the leftover a parts are added on.

Mathematician's thinking: You are generalising. You turned a single example into a rule using letters that works for every mixed numeral.
