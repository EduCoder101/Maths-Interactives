Year 5 Fractions Extension

Simplifying Fractions

Write any fraction in its lowest terms
Learning Intention and Success Criteria ▼

Learning Intention

We are learning to simplify a fraction by dividing the numerator and denominator by their highest common factor.

Success Criteria

  • I can find the highest common factor of two numbers.
  • I can simplify a fraction to its lowest terms.
  • I can tell when a fraction is already in its lowest terms.
  • I can use simplifying to answer worded problems.
Learn this skill ▼

A fraction is in its lowest terms (or simplest form) when the numerator and denominator share no common factor except 1. To simplify, you divide the top and the bottom by the same number. The quickest way is to divide by their highest common factor (HCF), the largest number that divides into both. The fraction keeps the same value, it is just written with smaller numbers.

Worked example: simplify 1824
1Find the highest common factor.
Factors of 18: 1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 18
Factors of 24: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 24
The largest in both lists is 6, so the HCF is 6.
2Divide the top and bottom by the HCF.
18 ÷ 6 = 3
24 ÷ 6 = 4
On the bar, the 24 pieces group into 4 equal parts, and 3 are shaded.
3Write the answer and check it.
1824 = 34
3 and 4 share no common factor except 1, so it is in lowest terms.
Another way

If you cannot spot the HCF, divide by any common factor and repeat. For 1824: divide by 2 to get 912, then by 3 to get 34. You reach the same answer.

Practice

Choose a level, then simplify each fraction to its lowest terms. Every question shows the full working when you check it.

Go Deeper: Simplifying Investigations

These tasks push your thinking beyond getting the answer. Explore, test claims, and reason like a mathematician.

Specialising
Simplify 6/8, 9/12 and 75/100 to their lowest terms.
What do you notice about the three answers? What does that tell you about the three starting fractions?
A mathematician's response

I simplified each one. 6/8: HCF is 2, so 3/4. 9/12: HCF is 3, so 3/4. 75/100: HCF is 25, so 3/4.

All three gave 3/4. At first I thought that was a coincidence, but it cannot be. If they all simplify to the same fraction, they must all be the same amount. They are equivalent fractions, just written with different sized pieces. Simplifying is a way of finding the one simplest name they all share.

Mathematician's thinking: You are specialising. That means working through specific examples to notice a pattern before making any claim.
Justifying
A student says: "To simplify any fraction, you just keep dividing the top and bottom by 2 until you cannot any more."
Is this always, sometimes, or never true? Justify your answer with examples.
A mathematician's response

I tried 8/24 first. Divide by 2: 4/12. By 2 again: 2/6. By 2 again: 1/3. That worked, so I almost said "always".

Then I tried 9/12. Both numbers are not even, so I cannot divide by 2 at all, yet it still simplifies to 3/4 by dividing by 3. So the rule breaks.

The answer is sometimes. Dividing by 2 only works while both numbers are even. To simplify any fraction you must divide by whatever common factor the numbers actually share, not just 2.

Mathematician's thinking: You are justifying. A single counterexample is enough to show a claim is not always true.
Generalising
A fraction n/d is already in its simplest form.
What can you say about the numbers n and d? Write a rule that is always true.
A mathematician's response

I looked at simplest fractions I knew: 3/4, 2/5, 5/6, 7/10. I checked the factors of each pair. 3 and 4 share only 1. 5 and 6 share only 1.

So my rule is: a fraction is in its simplest form exactly when the HCF of the numerator and denominator is 1. Another way to say it: n and d share no common factor except 1. If they shared anything bigger, I could still divide by it, so it would not yet be simplest.

Mathematician's thinking: You are generalising. That means writing one rule that holds for every fraction, not just the examples you tried.
